# Chapter 23: Managing the Discussion

The middle of a meeting is where things get tricky: some people talk too much, others stay silent, discussions drift off-topic, and disagreements arise. This chapter gives you the language to steer the conversation, include everyone, disagree politely, and handle problems — including the important topic of European directness.

---

## 1. Inviting input

Draw out the people who haven't spoken. As host, this is one of your main jobs.

- "Sofia, you've worked on this — what's your view?"
- "I'd like to hear from the data team on this."
- "[Name], you've been quiet — anything to add?"
- "Before we decide, does anyone see a risk we've missed?"
- "What do others think?"

**Online tip:** Silence online often means people are being polite or unsure when to speak. Call on people by name to bring them in gently.

## 2. Handling interruptions

**When someone interrupts a speaker:**
- "Could I just finish this point? Then I'd love to hear your thoughts."
- "Let's hear Tom out first, then come to you, Anna."
- "Hold that thought — let [Name] finish."

**When you need to interrupt (politely):**
- "Sorry to jump in, but can I add something here?"
- "If I could just come in on that point..."
- "Can I build on what you just said?"

## 3. Keeping the meeting on track

Discussions drift. Bring them back without shutting people down.

- "That's an important topic, but let's park it for now and stay on the agenda."
- "Good point — let's take that offline so we don't run over."
- "Let's come back to the main question: do we approve the pilot?"
- "We're going a bit deep here — can we zoom back out?"
- "Let's note that as a follow-up and keep moving."

**Tip:** "Park it" and "take it offline" are standard, polite ways to defer a topic. They don't dismiss the idea — they just move it to later.

## 4. Managing people who dominate

Some people talk too much. Redirect gently but firmly.

- "Thanks, James — let's get a few other perspectives before we decide."
- "That's helpful. I want to make sure we hear from others too."
- "Great input. Let's open it up — anyone else?"
- "Let's give someone else a turn."

## 5. Managing silence (especially online)

- "I'll take silence as agreement — unless anyone objects?"
- "No concerns? Then let's proceed."
- "Take a moment to think, then let's hear a few views." (Give people time.)
- "Feel free to unmute or drop it in the chat."

**Tip:** Wait a full five seconds after asking a question online. The silence feels long to you but people need time to unmute and gather their thoughts.

## 6. Disagreeing politely — and European directness

This is one of the most important cultural skills for an Indian professional in Europe. Different European cultures have very different comfort with directness.

**The directness map:**
- **Very direct (Netherlands, Germany, Nordics):** People say exactly what they think, including "I disagree" or "I think that's wrong." This is **not rude** in their culture — it's honest and efficient. Don't take it personally.
- **More indirect (UK, Ireland):** People soften disagreement heavily. "I'm not sure that's quite right" often means "that's wrong." (See the British understatement guide in Chapter 33.)

**Three levels of disagreement — choose based on the person and culture:**

**Soft (safe everywhere, good with UK/senior people):**
- "I see it slightly differently..."
- "I wonder if there's another way to look at this."
- "That's one option — could we also consider...?"

**Medium (normal in most European workplaces):**
- "I'm not sure I agree — my concern is..."
- "I'd challenge that a little. Here's why."
- "I see the logic, but I think the data points the other way."

**Direct (fine in NL/Germany, use carefully elsewhere):**
- "I have to push back on that."
- "I disagree. Here's my reasoning."
- "I don't think that's right, and here's the evidence."

**Golden rule:** Disagree with the *idea*, never the *person*. "I disagree with this approach" is fine; "you're wrong" is not. Always give your reason — European colleagues respect a reasoned counter-argument.

**As an Indian professional:** In India, openly disagreeing with a senior person can feel disrespectful. In Europe, especially the Netherlands and Germany, it's expected — staying silent when you disagree can even make you look disengaged. Start with the medium level and adjust. It's a real adjustment, but a valuable one.

## 7. Building agreement

- "It sounds like we're aligned on this."
- "So we all agree on the direction — is that fair?"
- "Let me check we're on the same page: we're going with option B?"
- "Good — it seems we have consensus."

## 8. Technical difficulties during the meeting

Screens freeze, audio echoes, screen-shares fail. Handle it calmly.

**Frozen screen:**
- "I think your screen has frozen — could you stop sharing and try again?"
- "You've frozen for us — we'll wait a moment."

**Echo or bad audio:**
- "There's a bit of an echo — could everyone check they're not double-connected?"
- "We're getting some feedback. [Name], could you mute when not speaking?"

**Screen-share failure:**
- "The screen-share isn't coming through — bear with me."
- "While I fix this, let me just describe what you'd be seeing."
- "Let me send the slides in the chat so you can follow along."

**Someone drops off the call:**
- "Looks like we lost [Name]. Let's carry on and catch them up when they rejoin."

## Common Indian-English pitfalls in discussions

- **"I am not agreeing with this."** → "I don't agree with this." (Use simple present, not continuous — see Chapter 36.)
- **"Kindly allow me to complete."** → "Could I just finish my point?"
- **"What is your good opinion on this?"** → "What's your view on this?"
- **"He is telling that it won't work."** → "He says it won't work." (Not "telling that.")
- **Staying silent when you disagree** — in Europe this can look like you have nothing to contribute. Speak up (politely).

---

**Chapter 23 summary.** Managing a discussion means inviting quiet voices in, gently reining in dominant ones, keeping the conversation on track, and disagreeing with ideas respectfully. The biggest adjustment for Indian professionals is European directness — especially in the Netherlands and Germany, honest disagreement is expected and respected. Disagree with the idea, give your reason, and never make it personal.
