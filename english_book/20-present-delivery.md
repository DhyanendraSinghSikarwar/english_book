# Chapter 20: Delivery Skills

The words matter, but how you say them matters just as much. Two people can give the same talk — one sounds nervous and junior, the other calm and senior. The difference is delivery. This chapter focuses on pace, stress, filler words, voice on video, and slide language, with special notes for Indian speakers.

---

## 1. Pausing and pacing

**The single biggest upgrade: slow down.**

Most people, especially when nervous, speak too fast. Slowing down does three things: it makes you easier to understand, it makes you sound more senior, and it gives you time to think.

- **Pause after key points.** Say your main message, then stop for one or two seconds. Silence draws attention.
- **Pause instead of saying "um."** When you need a moment, pause silently. A pause sounds thoughtful; "um" sounds unsure.
- **Slow down for numbers and names.** "The saving is... two point three million euros." Let important figures land.

**Practice tip:** Record yourself for two minutes. If you can't comfortably understand every word at normal attention, you're too fast.

## 2. Word stress and sentence stress

English is a "stress-timed" language: some syllables and words are stressed (louder, longer, higher), and the rest are said quickly. Indian languages are often more "syllable-timed" (each syllable gets similar time). This is the biggest reason Indian English can sound different to European ears — not the accent itself, but the rhythm.

**Word stress that changes meaning:** Some words are a noun or a verb depending on which syllable you stress.

| Word | Noun (stress first) | Verb (stress second) |
|---|---|---|
| record | RE-cord (a record) | re-CORD (to record) |
| present | PRE-sent (a present/gift) | pre-SENT (to present) |
| object | OB-ject (an object) | ob-JECT (to object) |
| increase | IN-crease (an increase) | in-CREASE (to increase) |
| project | PRO-ject (a project) | pro-JECT (to project) |
| contract | CON-tract (a contract) | con-TRACT (to contract) |
| conduct | CON-duct (conduct) | con-DUCT (to conduct) |

Getting these wrong rarely blocks understanding, but getting them right makes you sound polished. "Let me PRE-sent the results" is slightly off; "let me pre-SENT the results" is correct.

**Sentence stress:** Stress the words that carry meaning (nouns, main verbs, key numbers). Say the small words (a, the, of, to, is) quickly. Compare:
- Flat: "The-mo-del-pre-dicts-churn-with-eigh-ty-per-cent-ac-cu-ra-cy." (robotic)
- Natural: "The **model** predicts **churn** with **eighty percent** accuracy." (stress the meaning words)

## 3. Which syllables Europeans expect stressed

A few common words are often stressed differently in Indian English. Aligning with the international norm helps:

| Word | Common Indian stress | International stress |
|---|---|---|
| development | de-VE-lop-ment (often fine) | de-VE-lop-ment ✓ |
| percentage | per-CENT-age | per-CENT-age ✓ |
| category | ca-te-GO-ry | CA-te-gory |
| available | a-VAIL-able ✓ | a-VAIL-able ✓ |
| comparable | com-PA-rable | COM-parable |
| hierarchy | hi-ER-archy | HI-erarchy |
| maintenance | main-TAIN-ance | MAIN-tenance |

Don't stress about mastering all of these. Focus on rhythm (meaning-words stressed, small words quick) more than individual syllables — that's what makes the biggest difference.

## 4. Eliminating filler words

Filler words make you sound unsure and junior. The worst offenders:

- **"basically"** — often adds nothing. Cut it.
- **"actually"** — usually unnecessary.
- **"like"** — as in "it's like, really important." Cut it.
- **"you know"** — filler; remove it.
- **"so yeah"** — weak ending; replace with a clean stop.
- **"I mean"** — often filler.
- **"right?"** after every sentence — drop it.

**How to remove them:** Replace the filler with a silent pause. Your brain uses fillers to buy thinking time; a pause does the same job and sounds far better.

**Practice tip:** Ask a colleague to raise a hand every time you say "basically" or "actually" in a practice run. You'll become aware of your habit fast.

## 5. Voice on video calls vs in-room

Most European corporate meetings are on video (Teams, Zoom, Google Meet). Your voice works differently there.

**On video:**
- **Speak slightly slower** — audio delay and compression eat clarity.
- **Get close to a good microphone** — clear audio matters more than video quality.
- **Pause a beat longer** before expecting a response — there's lag, and people unmute.
- **Use names** — "Anna, what do you think?" — because visual cues are weak online.
- **Vary your tone more** — video flattens energy, so add a little more warmth and emphasis than you would in person.
- **Look at the camera, not the screen,** when making a key point — it feels like eye contact.

**In-room:**
- **Project your voice** to the back of the room without shouting.
- **Use the space** — move, gesture, point at the screen.
- **Make eye contact** around the whole room, not just one person.

## 6. Slide language and design norms in Europe

European slide culture leans toward **less text, more clarity.** Walls of text and heavy decoration look dated.

**Titles:**
- Write titles as the takeaway, not the topic. Not "Q3 Results" but "Q3 churn rose 15% after the price change." The title should tell the story on its own.

**Bullet points:**
- Keep them short — a few words, not full sentences.
- Use parallel grammar: start each bullet the same way (all verbs, or all nouns).
  - Good: "Reduced cost / Improved speed / Cut errors"
  - Off: "Reduced cost / Speed was improved / We cut the errors"
- Aim for no more than five bullets per slide, and no more than a few words each.

**General norms in Europe:**
- **Less is more.** One idea per slide. Lots of white space.
- **Avoid clip-art and heavy templates.** Clean and simple looks professional.
- **Data slides:** one chart, one clear message, big enough to read from the back.
- **No reading aloud.** Slides support you; they are not your script.

## 7. Body language basics

- **Stand or sit up straight** — posture signals confidence.
- **Keep hands visible and use natural gestures** — don't hide them or fidget.
- **Make eye contact** — with the room, or the camera on video.
- **Smile at the start and end** — it builds warmth and trust.
- **Avoid nervous habits** — pacing, playing with a pen, touching your face.

## Common Indian-English delivery pitfalls

- **Speaking too fast** — the most common issue. Slow down deliberately.
- **The head wobble** — natural in India, but can confuse Europeans about whether you mean yes or no. On video especially, use a clear nod for yes.
- **Flat or sing-song rhythm** — work on stressing meaning-words and de-stressing small words.
- **Overusing "only," "itself," "na?"** in speech — see Chapter 14.
- **Ending sentences with a rising tone** when they're statements — this can sound like a question. Drop your tone at the end of statements.

## Part 4 wrap-up

Across Chapters 16–20 you have a full presentation toolkit: openings that grab attention, a body that guides the audience through data, strong closings and Q&A scripts, playbooks for special situations, and delivery skills to tie it together. The secret to all of it is preparation plus calm. Practise your opening and closing until they're automatic, slow down, pause instead of filling silence, and let your clear thinking show. You already know your data — this part makes sure everyone else can see how well you know it.
