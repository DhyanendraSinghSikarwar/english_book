# Part 10: Practice & Reference

This final part is for practice and quick reference. It contains 30 role-play scenarios with model answers, a weekly self-study checklist, a quick-reference card of 50 sentences to memorise before your first day, and notes on how to use the answer key.

---

## A. 30 Role-Play Scenarios (with model answers)

Practise these out loud. Cover the model answer, try your own version, then compare. **[DS]** marks data-science scenarios.

### Scenario 1 — Your first day: introducing yourself to the team
*You're asked to introduce yourself in the team stand-up.*
> "Hi everyone, I'm [Name]. I've just joined as a data scientist, and I'll be working on customer retention. I've spent the last few years on churn modelling. I'm looking forward to getting to know you all — please feel free to reach out any time."

### Scenario 2 — Daily stand-up update [DS]
> "Yesterday I finished cleaning the churn dataset. Today I'll train the first model and check its accuracy. One blocker: I still need access to the payments table — could someone help with that?"

### Scenario 3 — You don't understand a task
> "Just to make sure I build the right thing — when you say 'active users,' do you mean daily or monthly? It changes the analysis quite a bit."

### Scenario 4 — Presenting results to the CTO [DS]
> "Bottom line: the model predicts churn with about 85% accuracy, and acting on it could protect around €1M a year. I'm asking for approval to run a three-month pilot. Here's the reasoning in three points..."

### Scenario 5 — Disagreeing with a senior colleague
> "I see it slightly differently. My concern is that the smaller dataset might not be representative. Could we test on a larger sample before deciding?"

### Scenario 6 — Asking for leave
> "I'd like to take annual leave from the 12th to the 19th. I'll finish the pipeline work before then and hand over to [Name]. Does that work?"

### Scenario 7 — Handling a production incident call [DS]
> "Here's the situation: the nightly job failed, so today's dashboard is stale. I'm rolling back to the last good version now, and I'll have fresh data within the hour. I'll send an update the moment it's fixed."

### Scenario 8 — Admitting you don't know an answer
> "That's a fair question — I don't have that figure to hand. Let me confirm it and get back to you by end of day."

### Scenario 9 — Chasing a colleague for something overdue
> "Gentle nudge on the sales figures — they're blocking the report. Are you on track, or is there anything I can help with?"

### Scenario 10 — Declining extra work politely
> "I'd like to help, but I'm at capacity with the pilot this sprint. Could it wait until next sprint, or shall we ask if someone else has bandwidth?"

### Scenario 11 — Explaining a model to a non-technical stakeholder [DS]
> "In simple terms, the model learns patterns from past customers to spot who's likely to leave. Think of it as a very fast analyst who's read every customer's history."

### Scenario 12 — Giving difficult feedback to a peer
> "Nice work overall. One thing to strengthen: the chart titles could be clearer so a first-time reader gets the point instantly. Happy to pair on it."

### Scenario 13 — Receiving blunt feedback (Dutch/German colleague)
> "Thanks for the honest feedback — that's helpful. Could you give me a specific example so I can work on it?"

### Scenario 14 — Asking a question at an all-hands
> "Hi, I'm [Name] from data science. Quick question — how will this new strategy affect the analytics roadmap?"

### Scenario 15 — Salary negotiation
> "Thank you for the offer — I'm really keen on the role. Based on my research, the market range is €X to €Y, and given my experience I was hoping for closer to €Y. Is there flexibility?"

### Scenario 16 — Delivering bad news to a client [DS]
> "I want to be upfront: the data quality issue means the model will be ready two weeks later than planned. Here's the recovery plan, and here's what we'll deliver in the meantime."

### Scenario 17 — Rescheduling a meeting
> "I'm afraid I need to move our 3 o'clock — something urgent has come up. Would tomorrow morning suit you instead? Apologies for the short notice."

### Scenario 18 — Small talk at the coffee machine
> "Morning! Good weekend? I finally explored the old town — still finding my way around. Any spots you'd recommend?"

### Scenario 19 — Sprint retro: raising a problem
> "One thing that didn't go well: we underestimated the data cleaning. For next time, I'd suggest we add a buffer for data issues so we don't overcommit."

### Scenario 20 — Asking for help without sounding helpless [DS]
> "I'm hitting a permissions error on the payments table. I've checked my access and restarted — still failing. Has anyone seen this before?"

### Scenario 21 — Presenting a failed experiment [DS]
> "The experiment didn't confirm our hypothesis — the new features didn't beat the baseline. That's useful: it tells us the signal isn't in this data, so I'd suggest we look at the usage logs next."

### Scenario 22 — Interrupting politely in a meeting
> "Sorry to jump in — could I add something on that point before we move on?"

### Scenario 23 — Handling a dominant speaker (as host)
> "Thanks, James — really helpful. I'd love to hear a few other views before we decide. Anyone else?"

### Scenario 24 — One-on-one: asking for growth
> "I'd like to work toward a senior role. Over the past year I've owned the churn model and mentored two juniors. What would I need to demonstrate to get there?"

### Scenario 25 — Saying no to your manager (respectfully)
> "I want to be honest — that timeline isn't realistic without cutting quality. I could deliver a solid version by Wednesday next week. Would that work?"

### Scenario 26 — Networking message on LinkedIn
> "Hi [Name], thanks for connecting. I've just moved into the data scene in [City] and would value a short chat about your experience at [Company], if you're open to it. No worries if you're busy!"

### Scenario 27 — Closing a meeting you hosted
> "So we've agreed on three actions: Priya cleans the data by Wednesday, Anna drafts the plan, and I'll book the review. I'll send the notes today. Thanks, everyone."

### Scenario 28 — Explaining uncertainty in results [DS]
> "I'm confident in the direction — churn is clearly rising in month one. I'd want more data before committing to the exact percentage, but the trend is solid."

### Scenario 29 — Apologising for a mistake
> "Apologies — I found an error in yesterday's figures; the churn rate should be 12%, not 21%. I've fixed the cause and added a check to prevent it. Corrected version attached."

### Scenario 30 — Giving notice / resigning
> "I wanted to tell you first: I've decided to move on and have accepted a new role. My last day would be [date], respecting my notice. I've really valued my time here and want to make the handover smooth."

## C. Quick-Reference Card — 50 Sentences to Memorise Before Day 1

Print these. They cover 90% of your first weeks.

**Introductions & basics**
1. "Hi, I'm [Name] — I've just joined the data team."
2. "Nice to meet you!"
3. "How are you settling in?" / "Getting there, thanks."
4. "Could you say a bit more about that?"
5. "Sorry, could you repeat that?"

**Meetings**
6. "Let's get started — thanks for joining."
7. "The purpose of today's meeting is..."
8. "Just to clarify, do you mean X or Y?"
9. "Let's park that and stay on the agenda."
10. "So, to sum up, we've agreed on..."
11. "Could you take ownership of X by Friday?"
12. "I'll circulate the notes today."
13. "You're on mute." / "Can everyone hear me okay?"
14. "I'll take silence as agreement — unless anyone objects?"
15. "Let's take this offline."

**Stand-ups & status [DS]**
16. "Yesterday I... Today I'll... One blocker is..."
17. "No blockers from me."
18. "I'll get back to you on that."
19. "It's on track for Friday."
20. "I'm at capacity this sprint."

**Requests & replies**
21. "Could you send that over when you have a moment?"
22. "Would it be possible to...?"
23. "This is fairly urgent — could you prioritise it today?"
24. "No rush, but..."
25. "Happy to help — I'll sort it."

**Disagreeing & opinions**
26. "I see it slightly differently — my concern is..."
27. "I'd recommend..."
28. "That's a fair point. Building on that..."
29. "I'm not sure I agree, and here's why."
30. "In my assessment, the data suggests..."

**Not knowing / mistakes**
31. "I'll look into it and get back to you."
32. "Apologies for the delay."
33. "Let me confirm that before I commit to a number."
34. "That slipped through — I'll fix it today."
35. "Good question — I don't have that to hand, but I'll follow up."

**Presenting [DS]**
36. "Let me walk you through the key findings."
37. "The key takeaway from this chart is..."
38. "In simple terms, the model..."
39. "I want to stress that these results are preliminary."
40. "Thank you — I'm happy to take any questions."

**Small talk**
41. "Good weekend?" / "Anything nice planned?"
42. "How's your week going?"
43. "Still finding my way around — any recommendations?"
44. "Anyway, I'd better get back to it — good chatting!"
45. "Are you coming for a drink later?"

**Culture & boundaries**
46. "No need to reply until tomorrow." (for after-hours messages)
47. "I'll be on annual leave from [date]."
48. "Let's align on what success looks like first."
49. "I'd rather under-promise and over-deliver."
50. "Realistically, that'll be ready by [date]."

## D. Answer Key & How to Use It

This book is designed for active use, not just reading. Here's how to get the most from it:

- **The role-plays (Section A)** are your answer key for speaking. Cover the model answer, attempt your own, then compare. There's no single "correct" answer — the model shows one natural, professional version. If yours conveys the same meaning politely and clearly, you've succeeded.
- **For the phrasal verbs and power verbs (Parts 1–2)**, the "practice" is to use them in real messages. The best answer key is a colleague understanding you and the phrase feeling natural.
- **For grammar (Chapter 36)**, the right → wrong pairs are your key. Re-read them whenever you're unsure.
- **Self-test:** each month, revisit the self-assessment in the intro (Chapter 0) and see how many more items you can confidently tick.

---

**Part 10 summary.** Practice is what turns this book from information into ability. Rehearse the 30 role-plays out loud, run the weekly checklist, and memorise the 50-sentence card before your first day. Language is a skill, like coding — you get good by doing, not just reading. Come back to these pages often, and each time you'll find the words come faster and more naturally.
