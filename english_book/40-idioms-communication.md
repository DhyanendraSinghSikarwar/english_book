# Chapter 40: Professional Idioms — Meetings & Communication

An idiom is a group of words with a meaning you can't guess from the single words. "On the same page" has nothing to do with books — it means "in agreement." Idioms make your English sound natural and fluent. But used wrongly, they confuse people. This chapter gives you 40 idioms for meetings and communication.

**How to read each entry:** the idiom, a simple meaning, examples, a mini-scenario, a formal alternative, and an Indian-English note. **[Casual]** = keep for informal moments. **[Careful]** = can confuse non-native speakers, so use plainly with mixed audiences.

> **Note:** This part complements the Corporate Jargon Decoder (Chapter 32). Jargon there covered abbreviations and buzzwords; here we focus on true idioms — figurative phrases.

---

#### on the same page
- **Meaning:** Everyone shares the same understanding or agreement.
- **Examples:**
  - "Let's make sure we're all on the same page before we start the sprint."
  - "After the call, the whole data team was on the same page about the metric definition."
  - "I want to be on the same page with you on the pipeline scope."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Priya:** "Are we on the same page about which model goes to production?"
  - **Lars:** "Yes — the gradient-boosting one. We agreed on that yesterday."
- **Formal alternative:** "in agreement" or "aligned"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Some speakers say "on the same book" or "in the same page." The correct form is "on the same page." Keep it exact.

#### get the ball rolling
- **Meaning:** Start something so it begins to move forward.
- **Examples:**
  - "To get the ball rolling, let me share the agenda."
  - "We got the ball rolling on the migration last week."
  - "Someone needs to get the ball rolling on data cleaning today."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Anna:** "How do we start the retrospective?"
  - **Marco:** "Let me get the ball rolling by listing what went well."
- **Formal alternative:** "begin" or "start"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** This is informal. In a formal report, write "to begin" or "as a first step" instead of "to get the ball rolling."

#### touch base
- **Meaning:** Check in briefly with someone.
- **Examples:**
  - "Let's touch base on Friday to see where we are."
  - "I'll touch base with the stakeholders before the demo."
  - "Can we touch base quickly after stand-up?"
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Sofia:** "The dashboard numbers look off."
  - **Priya:** "Let's touch base at 2 pm and go through them together."
- **Formal alternative:** "check in" or "have a brief update"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Do not say "touch base with the topic." You touch base *with a person*, not with a task. For a task, say "check on" or "review."

#### on the same wavelength
- **Meaning:** Two people are thinking in a very similar way.
- **Examples:**
  - "We're on the same wavelength about the priorities."
  - "Lars and I are usually on the same wavelength during code reviews."
  - "Once we talked it through, we were on the same wavelength."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Marco:** "I feel we should test on a smaller sample first."
  - **Anna:** "Exactly what I was thinking — we're on the same wavelength."
- **Formal alternative:** "of the same view" or "thinking alike"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** This is informal and slightly figurative. In a mixed European audience that prefers plain speech, "we agree on the priorities" is safer.

#### read between the lines [Careful]
- **Meaning:** Understand a meaning that is hidden and not said directly.
- **Examples:**
  - "Reading between the lines, I think they're not happy with the timeline."
  - "Read between the lines of the client's email — they want more detail."
  - "She didn't say no, but reading between the lines, she's worried about cost."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Priya:** "The manager only said 'interesting approach.'"
  - **Sofia:** "Reading between the lines, I think she has doubts."
- **Formal alternative:** "infer the underlying meaning"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** With non-native listeners this can confuse. If clarity matters, say plainly: "I think they have concerns they didn't state directly."

#### beat around the bush
- **Meaning:** Avoid saying something directly.
- **Examples:**
  - "Let's not beat around the bush — the deadline won't be met."
  - "He beat around the bush instead of admitting the model failed."
  - "Don't beat around the bush; tell me the real blocker."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lars:** "Well, there are a few small things, and maybe some data issues, and..."
  - **Anna:** "Stop beating around the bush. Is the pipeline broken or not?"
- **Formal alternative:** "be indirect" or "avoid the main point"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** The correct form is "beat around the bush." Some say "beat about the bush" (British, also fine) but avoid "beat around the bushes" (plural).

#### get to the point
- **Meaning:** Say the main thing without delay.
- **Examples:**
  - "I'll get straight to the point: we need more budget."
  - "Please get to the point — the meeting is short."
  - "Let me get to the point about the failing tests."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Marco:** "So there's a lot of background here, and history, and..."
  - **Priya:** "Can you get to the point? What do you need from us?"
- **Formal alternative:** "state the main issue"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Fine in most settings. Telling a senior person to "get to the point" can sound blunt, so soften it: "Could you summarise the key issue?"

#### cut to the chase [Casual]
- **Meaning:** Skip the detail and reach the important part.
- **Examples:**
  - "Let me cut to the chase — the pilot worked."
  - "Cutting to the chase, the model is ready to deploy."
  - "I'll cut to the chase: we're over budget."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Sofia:** "There are twenty slides, but let me cut to the chase."
  - **Lars:** "Please do — what's the result?"
- **Formal alternative:** "get straight to the main point"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** This is casual. In a formal report or with senior leadership you don't know, write "in summary" or "the key point is" instead.

#### on the record / off the record
- **Meaning:** Official and quotable, or unofficial and not to be repeated.
- **Examples:**
  - "Off the record, I think the vendor is struggling."
  - "On the record, the project is on track."
  - "This part is off the record, so please don't share it."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Priya:** "Off the record — do you trust the new data source?"
  - **Marco:** "Off the record, no. But officially we're still testing it."
- **Formal alternative:** "confidentially" (off the record) / "officially" (on the record)
- **Indian-English pitfall:** In Europe, "off the record" is a real trust signal. If someone says it, honour it — do not repeat what they said, even casually.

#### put it on the table
- **Meaning:** Share an idea or issue openly for discussion.
- **Examples:**
  - "Let's put all the options on the table first."
  - "I want to put a concern on the table about data quality."
  - "Put your idea on the table — we'll discuss it."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Anna:** "There's a risk no one wants to mention."
  - **Lars:** "Then put it on the table. We should discuss it openly."
- **Formal alternative:** "raise" or "bring up for discussion"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Do not confuse with the British "take it off the table," which means "remove it as an option." Putting it *on* the table means opening it up.

#### think out loud
- **Meaning:** Say your thoughts as they form, not as a final decision.
- **Examples:**
  - "I'm just thinking out loud here, but what if we delayed the launch?"
  - "Let me think out loud for a second about the schema."
  - "This is me thinking out loud — don't quote me on it."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Marco:** "Just thinking out loud — could we cache the features?"
  - **Priya:** "Good idea. Let's note it and check feasibility."
- **Formal alternative:** "share an initial thought"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Say "thinking out loud," not "thinking aloud in loud." It is useful for softening half-formed ideas so people don't treat them as firm proposals.

#### play devil's advocate [Careful]
- **Meaning:** Argue the opposite side on purpose to test an idea.
- **Examples:**
  - "Let me play devil's advocate — what if users hate the change?"
  - "I'll play devil's advocate here so we stress-test the plan."
  - "Playing devil's advocate, is the extra accuracy worth the cost?"
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Sofia:** "Let me play devil's advocate: what if the data drifts next month?"
  - **Lars:** "Fair point. We should add drift monitoring."
- **Formal alternative:** "raise a counter-argument"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Some non-native listeners take it literally and think you truly disagree. Add a signal: "I don't actually oppose this, but let me test it..."

#### get a word in
- **Meaning:** Manage to speak in a busy conversation.
- **Examples:**
  - "It was so lively I could barely get a word in."
  - "Everyone was talking; I couldn't get a word in."
  - "Let her get a word in — she has data on this."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Priya:** "You were quiet in the stand-up."
  - **Marco:** "It was so fast I couldn't get a word in."
- **Formal alternative:** "contribute" or "have a chance to speak"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** The full phrase is often "get a word in edgeways." Just "get a word in" is fine and simpler; avoid "get a word inside."

#### talk shop [Casual]
- **Meaning:** Talk about work in a social setting.
- **Examples:**
  - "Let's not talk shop over lunch."
  - "They started talking shop at the dinner and lost everyone else."
  - "No talking shop at the team party, please."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Anna:** "So about the migration plan—"
  - **Lars:** "It's Friday drinks. Let's not talk shop tonight."
- **Formal alternative:** "discuss work matters"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Shop" here means work, not a store. Don't read it literally. It is casual — avoid it in writing.

#### bring someone up to speed
- **Meaning:** Give someone the latest information so they are caught up.
- **Examples:**
  - "Let me bring you up to speed on the project."
  - "I'll bring the new analyst up to speed on our pipelines."
  - "Can someone bring me up to speed after my leave?"
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Sofia:** "I just joined the sprint. What did I miss?"
  - **Priya:** "Let me bring you up to speed on the blockers."
- **Formal alternative:** "update" or "brief"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Say "bring you up to speed," not "bring you in speed" or "up to the speed." It means to inform, not to make someone work faster.

#### keep someone in the loop
- **Meaning:** Keep someone informed as things develop.
- **Examples:**
  - "Please keep me in the loop on any changes."
  - "We'll keep the client in the loop during the pilot."
  - "Keep Lars in the loop — he owns that dataset."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Marco:** "I'll start the model refactor today."
  - **Anna:** "Great — keep me in the loop on progress."
- **Formal alternative:** "keep informed" or "copy on updates"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Very common and safe. Just don't overuse "loop in" as a verb in formal writing; "include" or "inform" is cleaner there.

#### out of the loop
- **Meaning:** Not informed about something.
- **Examples:**
  - "Sorry, I've been out of the loop this week."
  - "I was on leave, so I'm out of the loop on the new schema."
  - "Don't leave the support team out of the loop."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Priya:** "Did you see the deployment was moved?"
  - **Lars:** "No — I've been out of the loop since Monday."
- **Formal alternative:** "not informed" or "unaware"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** This is the opposite of "in the loop." Don't say "outside of loop" — the fixed form is "out of the loop."

#### on the same footing
- **Meaning:** Treated equally or at the same level.
- **Examples:**
  - "Let's start the discussion with everyone on the same footing."
  - "New and senior analysts should be on the same footing in reviews."
  - "The two teams are now on the same footing on tooling."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Anna:** "The contractors don't have full data access."
  - **Sofia:** "Let's put them on the same footing so they can help."
- **Formal alternative:** "on equal terms" or "at the same level"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Say "on the same footing," not "on same foot." It is slightly formal and works well in careful, mixed-audience writing.

#### a ballpark figure
- **Meaning:** A rough estimate, not an exact number.
- **Examples:**
  - "Can you give me a ballpark figure for the cost?"
  - "As a ballpark figure, training takes about six hours."
  - "I need a ballpark, not a precise number, for now."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Marco:** "How long to build the dashboard?"
  - **Priya:** "A ballpark figure would be two sprints."
- **Formal alternative:** "a rough estimate" or "an approximate figure"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Ballpark" comes from baseball, so it may be unfamiliar to some Europeans. With a mixed audience, "a rough estimate" is clearer.

#### in a nutshell
- **Meaning:** In a very short summary.
- **Examples:**
  - "In a nutshell, the model works and we're ready to pilot."
  - "In a nutshell, the data was too small to train on."
  - "So, in a nutshell, we're two days behind."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Sofia:** "Can you summarise the findings?"
  - **Lars:** "In a nutshell, accuracy is up but latency is worse."
- **Formal alternative:** "in short" or "to summarise"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Widely understood. Say "in a nutshell," not "in a nutshell case" or "in the nutshell."

#### the bottom line
- **Meaning:** The most important point or the final result.
- **Examples:**
  - "The bottom line is we save two weeks."
  - "The bottom line: the pipeline is stable now."
  - "What's the bottom line for the customer?"
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Priya:** "There are many small trade-offs here."
  - **Marco:** "But what's the bottom line — do we ship or not?"
- **Formal alternative:** "the key point" or "the conclusion"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** In business English this means the main point, not just profit. It is clear and safe with most European audiences.

#### at the end of the day [Casual]
- **Meaning:** When everything is considered.
- **Examples:**
  - "At the end of the day, the customer decides."
  - "At the end of the day, accuracy matters more than speed here."
  - "We can debate this, but at the end of the day it's the PM's call."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Anna:** "Should we optimise for recall or precision?"
  - **Lars:** "At the end of the day, it depends on the business cost of errors."
- **Formal alternative:** "ultimately" or "when all is considered"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** This is a spoken filler, not literal ("day"). Overused, it sounds vague. In writing, use "ultimately."

#### par for the course [Careful]
- **Meaning:** Normal and expected, often for something annoying.
- **Examples:**
  - "Delays are par for the course with this vendor."
  - "Flaky tests are par for the course early in the project."
  - "Extra data cleaning is par for the course."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Sofia:** "The API failed again during the load test."
  - **Marco:** "Sadly, that's par for the course with this service."
- **Formal alternative:** "normal" or "to be expected"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Comes from golf and confuses many non-native listeners. With a mixed audience, say "that's normal for this" instead.

#### on the fence
- **Meaning:** Undecided between two choices.
- **Examples:**
  - "I'm still on the fence about which tool to use."
  - "The team is on the fence between two model designs."
  - "She's on the fence about the vendor."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Priya:** "Do you prefer Airflow or Prefect?"
  - **Lars:** "Honestly, I'm on the fence — both would work."
- **Formal alternative:** "undecided"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Say "on the fence," not "in the fence" or "sitting on fence." It means undecided, not physically avoiding something.

#### see eye to eye
- **Meaning:** Fully agree with each other.
- **Examples:**
  - "We don't always see eye to eye, but we respect each other."
  - "The two leads finally see eye to eye on the roadmap."
  - "We see eye to eye on the need for testing."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Anna:** "I thought you two argued a lot."
  - **Sofia:** "We didn't see eye to eye at first, but now we do."
- **Formal alternative:** "agree" or "share the same view"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** The fixed form is "see eye to eye," not "see eye-to-eye about everything" as a noun. It's about agreement, not literal eye contact.

#### agree to disagree
- **Meaning:** Accept that you won't reach agreement and move on.
- **Examples:**
  - "Let's agree to disagree and move on."
  - "We agreed to disagree on the naming convention."
  - "After an hour, we had to agree to disagree."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Marco:** "I still think Python; you still think R."
  - **Priya:** "Let's agree to disagree and just pick one for the pilot."
- **Formal alternative:** "accept our differing views"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Useful for ending a debate politely. Don't say "agree to not agree" — the set phrase is "agree to disagree."

#### on board
- **Meaning:** Supporting a plan or decision.
- **Examples:**
  - "Is everyone on board with the new process?"
  - "The stakeholders are on board with the pilot."
  - "I'm on board — let's do it."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lars:** "We want to switch to weekly releases."
  - **Anna:** "I'm on board, as long as testing keeps up."
- **Formal alternative:** "in support of" or "in agreement"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "On board" (supporting) is different from "onboard/onboarding" (bringing a new person into a team). Keep them apart.

#### give the green light
- **Meaning:** Approve something so it can go ahead.
- **Examples:**
  - "Leadership gave the green light for the pilot."
  - "Once security gives the green light, we deploy."
  - "The budget got the green light today."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Sofia:** "Can we start the migration?"
  - **Marco:** "Yes — the architects gave the green light this morning."
- **Formal alternative:** "approve" or "authorise"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Say "give the green light," not "give green signal" (common Indian-English variant). The international idiom uses "light."

#### a heads-up
- **Meaning:** An advance warning or early notice.
- **Examples:**
  - "Thanks for the heads-up about the meeting change."
  - "Just a heads-up: the data source will be down tomorrow."
  - "Give me a heads-up before you deploy."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Priya:** "Heads-up — the API schema changes on Monday."
  - **Lars:** "Thanks, I'll update the ingestion code before then."
- **Formal alternative:** "advance notice"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** As a noun, keep the hyphen: "a heads-up." As a verb it's two words: "heads up the team." It's informal but very common in European offices.

#### run something by someone
- **Meaning:** Ask for someone's opinion before you decide.
- **Examples:**
  - "Let me run this by the team first."
  - "Can I run the design by you before the review?"
  - "She ran the idea by her manager."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Anna:** "I want to change the feature set."
  - **Sofia:** "Run it by Marco first — he owns that module."
- **Formal alternative:** "check with" or "seek input from"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Say "run this by you," not "run this to you" or "run this on you." The correct preposition is "by."

#### on second thoughts
- **Meaning:** After thinking about it again.
- **Examples:**
  - "On second thoughts, let's push the meeting to Thursday."
  - "On second thoughts, that feature can wait."
  - "On second thoughts, we should test on more data."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Marco:** "Let's deploy today."
  - **Priya:** "On second thoughts, let's wait for the QA sign-off."
- **Formal alternative:** "on reflection" or "after reconsidering"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** British English uses "on second thoughts" (plural); American English uses "on second thought" (singular). Both are fine — just be consistent.

#### take it with a pinch of salt [Careful]
- **Meaning:** Don't fully trust it; treat it with doubt.
- **Examples:**
  - "The early numbers are rough, so take them with a pinch of salt."
  - "Take the vendor's benchmark with a pinch of salt."
  - "These forecasts are noisy — take them with a pinch of salt."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lars:** "The demo showed 99% accuracy."
  - **Anna:** "Take that with a pinch of salt — it was on tiny test data."
- **Formal alternative:** "treat with caution" or "regard as unreliable"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** American English says "grain of salt." Both mean the same. With non-native listeners, say "treat these numbers with caution" to be clear.

#### hit the nail on the head
- **Meaning:** Describe a problem or point exactly right.
- **Examples:**
  - "You hit the nail on the head — onboarding is the real issue."
  - "That comment hit the nail on the head."
  - "She hit the nail on the head about the data leakage."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Sofia:** "I think the model overfits because the split is wrong."
  - **Marco:** "You hit the nail on the head — the split leaks future data."
- **Formal alternative:** "identify exactly" or "be precisely right"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Keep it exact: "hit the nail on the head," not "hit the nail on its head" or "hit the head of the nail."

#### speak the same language
- **Meaning:** Understand each other well because you share terms or views.
- **Examples:**
  - "Once we agreed on definitions, we started speaking the same language."
  - "Data and product finally speak the same language now."
  - "We need to speak the same language on what 'active user' means."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Priya:** "Our metrics never matched theirs."
  - **Lars:** "After the glossary workshop, we speak the same language."
- **Formal alternative:** "share a common understanding"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** This is figurative — it doesn't mean literally English or German. It means shared understanding, often shared definitions.

#### put words in someone's mouth
- **Meaning:** Claim someone said something they did not actually say.
- **Examples:**
  - "I don't want to put words in your mouth — what did you mean exactly?"
  - "Don't put words in my mouth; I never said the model was ready."
  - "He put words in her mouth during the summary."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Anna:** "So you're saying the whole pipeline is broken?"
  - **Marco:** "That's putting words in my mouth. I said one step is slow."
- **Formal alternative:** "misrepresent what someone said"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Keep the whole phrase; don't shorten it to "put words." It's a mild complaint, so use it carefully with seniors.

#### get your point across
- **Meaning:** Make people understand your idea.
- **Examples:**
  - "The chart really helped me get my point across."
  - "I struggled to get my point across in the review."
  - "A simple example got the point across."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Sofia:** "Did the stakeholders understand the trade-off?"
  - **Priya:** "Yes — the visual helped me get my point across."
- **Formal alternative:** "communicate clearly" or "convey your idea"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Say "get your point across," not "get across your point" in speech, and not "put your point across the audience." The set phrase ends with "across."

#### on the tip of my tongue [Casual]
- **Meaning:** Almost able to remember something, but not quite.
- **Examples:**
  - "The name's on the tip of my tongue — give me a second."
  - "The library name is on the tip of my tongue."
  - "It's on the tip of my tongue — that clustering method..."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lars:** "Which algorithm did we use last quarter?"
  - **Marco:** "It's on the tip of my tongue... DBSCAN, that's it."
- **Formal alternative:** "I can't quite recall it right now"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Keep it "on the tip of my tongue," not "at the tip." It's casual and about memory, not taste.

#### a two-way street
- **Meaning:** Something that needs effort from both sides.
- **Examples:**
  - "Good communication is a two-way street."
  - "Feedback is a two-way street — I want yours too."
  - "Trust between teams is a two-way street."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Anna:** "The other team never shares their data early."
  - **Sofia:** "Collaboration is a two-way street — do we share ours early?"
- **Formal alternative:** "a mutual effort" or "requires effort from both sides"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Figurative, not about roads. Say "a two-way street," not "a two-way road." It stresses mutual responsibility.

#### clear the air
- **Meaning:** Resolve a misunderstanding by talking honestly.
- **Examples:**
  - "Let's have a quick chat to clear the air."
  - "We cleared the air after the tense sprint review."
  - "A short call cleared the air between the two leads."
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Priya:** "Things felt tense after yesterday's disagreement."
  - **Marco:** "Let's grab a coffee and clear the air."
- **Formal alternative:** "resolve the misunderstanding"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Say "clear the air," not "clean the air." It's about relationships, not pollution.

#### wrap your head around [Casual]
- **Meaning:** Fully understand something complex.
- **Examples:**
  - "It took me a while to wrap my head around the new system."
  - "I'm still wrapping my head around the transformer architecture."
  - "Can you help me wrap my head around this pipeline?"
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Sofia:** "The new feature store is confusing."
  - **Lars:** "Give it a day — you'll wrap your head around it soon."
- **Formal alternative:** "fully understand" or "grasp"
- **Indian-English pitfall:** This is casual. In formal writing, say "understand" or "grasp," not "wrap my head around." Keep it for speech and chat.

## Using idioms wisely (read this)

- **Match your audience.** With non-native English speakers (common in Europe), use idioms sparingly and explain if needed. In the Netherlands, Germany, and the Nordics, plain, direct language is often preferred over idioms.
- **Don't overload.** One or two idioms in a conversation sound natural; ten sound forced.
- **Get the wording exact.** "On the same page," not "on the same book." A half-remembered idiom sounds worse than plain words.
- **When in doubt, say it plainly.** "Let's agree on this" is always safe if "see eye to eye" feels risky.

**Common Indian-English note:** Some idioms differ. "Do the needful," "out of station," and "prepone" are Indian-English, not international idioms (see Chapter 14). Also avoid mixing idioms ("that's not my cup of tea to handle" — pick one).

---

**Chapter 40 summary.** These 40 idioms cover the language of meetings and communication — agreeing, updating, summarising, and being direct. Learn a few at a time, use them in real conversations, and always keep them exact. Used lightly and correctly, idioms make you sound like a natural, fluent professional.
