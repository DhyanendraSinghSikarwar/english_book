# Chapter 40: Professional Idioms — Meetings & Communication

An idiom is a group of words with a meaning you can't guess from the single words. "On the same page" has nothing to do with books — it means "in agreement." Idioms make your English sound natural and fluent. But used wrongly, they confuse people. This chapter gives you 40 idioms for meetings and communication.

**How to read each entry:** the idiom, a simple meaning, an example, and (where useful) a caution or formality note. **[Casual]** = keep for informal moments. **[Careful]** = can confuse non-native speakers, so use plainly with mixed audiences.

> **Note:** This part complements the Corporate Jargon Decoder (Chapter 32). Jargon there covered abbreviations and buzzwords; here we focus on true idioms — figurative phrases.

---

1. **on the same page** — in agreement / sharing the same understanding.
*Example:* "Let's make sure we're all on the same page before we start."

2. **get the ball rolling** — start something.
*Example:* "To get the ball rolling, let me share the agenda."

3. **touch base** — check in briefly with someone.
*Example:* "Let's touch base on Friday to see where we are."

4. **on the same wavelength** — thinking in a similar way.
*Example:* "We're on the same wavelength about the priorities."

5. **read between the lines** — understand the hidden meaning.
*Example:* "Reading between the lines, I think they're not happy with the timeline." **[Careful]**

6. **beat around the bush** — avoid saying something directly.
*Example:* "Let's not beat around the bush — the deadline won't be met."

7. **get to the point** — say the main thing without delay.
*Example:* "I'll get straight to the point: we need more budget."

8. **cut to the chase** — skip the detail and reach the important part.
*Example:* "Let me cut to the chase — the pilot worked." **[Casual]**

9. **on the record / off the record** — official / unofficial and not to be quoted.
*Example:* "Off the record, I think the vendor is struggling."

10. **put it on the table** — share an idea or issue openly.
*Example:* "Let's put all the options on the table first."

11. **think out loud** — say your thoughts as they form, not as a final view.
*Example:* "I'm just thinking out loud here, but what if we delayed the launch?"

12. **play devil's advocate** — argue the opposite side to test an idea.
*Example:* "Let me play devil's advocate — what if users hate the change?" **[Careful]**

13. **get a word in** — manage to speak in a busy conversation.
*Example:* "It was so lively I could barely get a word in."

14. **talk shop** — talk about work in a social setting.
*Example:* "Let's not talk shop over lunch." **[Casual]**

15. **bring someone up to speed** — give someone the latest information.
*Example:* "Let me bring you up to speed on the project."

16. **keep someone in the loop** — keep someone informed.
*Example:* "Please keep me in the loop on any changes."

17. **out of the loop** — not informed about something.
*Example:* "Sorry, I've been out of the loop this week."

18. **on the same footing** — treated equally / at the same level.
*Example:* "Let's start the discussion with everyone on the same footing."

19. **a ballpark figure** — a rough estimate.
*Example:* "Can you give me a ballpark figure for the cost?"

20. **in a nutshell** — in a very short summary.
*Example:* "In a nutshell, the model works and we're ready to pilot."

21. **the bottom line** — the most important point or the final result.
*Example:* "The bottom line is we save two weeks."

22. **at the end of the day** — when everything is considered.
*Example:* "At the end of the day, the customer decides." **[Casual]**

23. **par for the course** — normal and expected (often for something annoying).
*Example:* "Delays are par for the course with this vendor." **[Careful]**

24. **on the fence** — undecided.
*Example:* "I'm still on the fence about which tool to use."

25. **see eye to eye** — fully agree.
*Example:* "We don't always see eye to eye, but we respect each other."

26. **agree to disagree** — accept that you won't reach agreement.
*Example:* "Let's agree to disagree and move on."

27. **on board** — supporting a plan.
*Example:* "Is everyone on board with the new process?"

28. **give the green light** — approve something.
*Example:* "Leadership gave the green light for the pilot."

29. **a heads-up** — an advance warning.
*Example:* "Thanks for the heads-up about the meeting change."

30. **run something by someone** — ask for someone's opinion before deciding.
*Example:* "Let me run this by the team first."

31. **on second thoughts** — after reconsidering.
*Example:* "On second thoughts, let's push the meeting to Thursday."

32. **take it with a pinch of salt** — don't fully trust it.
*Example:* "The early numbers are rough, so take them with a pinch of salt." **[Careful]**

33. **hit the nail on the head** — describe a problem exactly right.
*Example:* "You hit the nail on the head — onboarding is the real issue."

34. **speak the same language** — understand each other well.
*Example:* "Once we agreed on definitions, we started speaking the same language."

35. **put words in someone's mouth** — claim someone said something they didn't.
*Example:* "I don't want to put words in your mouth — what did you mean exactly?"

36. **get your point across** — make people understand your idea.
*Example:* "The chart really helped me get my point across."

37. **on the tip of my tongue** — almost able to remember something.
*Example:* "The name's on the tip of my tongue — give me a second." **[Casual]**

38. **a two-way street** — needs effort from both sides.
*Example:* "Good communication is a two-way street."

39. **clear the air** — resolve a misunderstanding by talking honestly.
*Example:* "Let's have a quick chat to clear the air."

40. **wrap your head around** — fully understand something complex.
*Example:* "It took me a while to wrap my head around the new system." **[Casual]**

## Using idioms wisely (read this)

- **Match your audience.** With non-native English speakers (common in Europe), use idioms sparingly and explain if needed. In the Netherlands, Germany, and the Nordics, plain, direct language is often preferred over idioms.
- **Don't overload.** One or two idioms in a conversation sound natural; ten sound forced.
- **Get the wording exact.** "On the same page," not "on the same book." A half-remembered idiom sounds worse than plain words.
- **When in doubt, say it plainly.** "Let's agree on this" is always safe if "see eye to eye" feels risky.

**Common Indian-English note:** Some idioms differ. "Do the needful," "out of station," and "prepone" are Indian-English, not international idioms (see Chapter 14). Also avoid mixing idioms ("that's not my cup of tea to handle" — pick one).

---

**Chapter 40 summary.** These 40 idioms cover the language of meetings and communication — agreeing, updating, summarising, and being direct. Learn a few at a time, use them in real conversations, and always keep them exact. Used lightly and correctly, idioms make you sound like a natural, fluent professional.
