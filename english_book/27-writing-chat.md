# Chapter 27: Chat & Slack/Teams Language

Most day-to-day communication in European tech companies happens on Slack or Microsoft Teams, not email. Chat has its own register — quicker, more casual, but still professional. Getting the tone right here makes you feel like part of the team. This chapter shows you how.

---

## 1. The instant-message register

Chat sits between speech and email. It's short, informal, and fast. Full formal sentences look stiff on Slack; overly casual slang can look unprofessional. Aim for friendly and clear.

**Compare:**
- Too formal (email-style): "Dear Anna, I hope this message finds you well. I would like to enquire whether the report is ready." ❌
- Just right (chat-style): "Hey Anna, is the report ready? No rush 🙂" ✓

## 2. Common chat openers and phrases

**Starting a message:**
- "Quick question —"
- "Hey, got a sec?"
- "No rush, but..."
- "When you have a moment —"
- "Heads up:"
- "FYI —"

**Asking for something:**
- "Could you take a look at this when you get a chance?"
- "Mind reviewing my PR?"
- "Any chance you could send the file?"

**Following up / bumping a message:**
- "Bumping this ↑"
- "Just nudging this in case it got buried."
- "Following up on the above 🙂"

**Acknowledging:**
- "Got it, thanks!"
- "Makes sense 👍"
- "On it."
- "Will do."
- "Noted, thanks."

**Being unavailable:**
- "Heads down for an hour, will reply after."
- "In a meeting — back to you shortly."
- "OOO tomorrow, will pick this up Monday." (OOO = out of office)

## 3. Being brief without being cold

Short messages can accidentally seem rude. A small softener or emoji keeps them warm.

- "No." → "Ah, not this time — sorry! Maybe next sprint?"
- "Wrong." → "I think there's a small mix-up here — mind if I check?"
- "Do it now." → "Could you prioritise this? It's blocking the release 🙏"

**Tip:** A single "thanks 🙂" or "👍" at the end of a request softens it a lot. But read the room — some teams use fewer emojis than others.

## 4. Emojis at work in Europe — what's fine, what's not

Emoji use varies by company and country, but here are safe guidelines:

**Generally fine:**
- 👍 (got it / agree), 🙂 (friendly), 🙏 (thanks/please), ✅ (done), 👀 (looking), 🎉 (celebrating a win), 🔥 (great work).

**Use with care:**
- ❤️ and other affectionate emojis — fine among close teammates, avoid with seniors or clients.
- 😂 in serious threads — can seem flippant.

**Avoid at work:**
- Anything that could be read as sarcastic, romantic, or inappropriate.
- Overloading messages with emojis — one or two is plenty.

**General rule:** Match your team's style. Watch how others use emojis for a week, then follow suit. Northern European teams (Nordics, NL) often use them lightly; some German or formal teams use fewer.

## 5. Reactions instead of replies

On Slack/Teams, you can react to a message with an emoji instead of typing a reply. This is normal and efficient.
- ✅ or 👍 = "seen and agreed / done."
- 👀 = "I'm looking into it."
- 🙏 = "thanks."

This keeps channels tidy — you don't need to type "ok thanks" every time.

## 6. Asking for help without sounding helpless

There's an art to asking for help: show you've tried, be specific, and make it easy to help you.

**Weak (sounds helpless):**
- "This isn't working, help!" ❌
- "I don't understand anything about this pipeline." ❌

**Strong (shows effort, gets fast help):**
- "I'm hitting a permissions error on the payments table. I've checked my access and restarted — still failing. Any ideas?" ✓
- "Quick one: what's the right way to connect to the staging DB? I've tried X but got Y." ✓
- "Stuck on this bug for 30 min — here's what I've tried [list]. Anyone seen this before?" ✓

**The formula:** what you're trying to do + what you've already tried + the specific question. This respects the helper's time and gets you a better answer.

## 7. Channel etiquette

- **Use threads** for replies, to keep the main channel readable.
- **Post in public channels** rather than DMs when others might benefit — it builds shared knowledge.
- **@mention** only the people who need to see it; don't @channel or @here unless it's genuinely urgent for everyone.
- **Don't send "hi" and then wait.** Say your question in the first message: "Hi Anna, quick question about the data — [question]." (Waiting after "hi" wastes time and is a known annoyance.)
- **Keep it on-topic** for the channel; use the right channel for the right subject.

## 8. Status and availability

- Set your status: "🍵 back at 14:00", "🎧 focus time", "🏖️ on leave".
- Respect others' focus time and off-hours. Don't expect instant replies. (More in Chapter 33.)
- If it's urgent, say so: "Sorry to interrupt — this one's urgent."

## Common Indian-English pitfalls in chat

- **"Kindly revert."** → "Let me know 🙂" (Way too formal for chat, and avoid "revert.")
- **"Please do the needful."** → say what you need: "Could you approve the PR?"
- **"Good morning sir, I have one doubt."** → "Morning! Quick question —" (No "sir," and "question" not "doubt.")
- **"Ok ok done done"** (repetition) → "Done 👍"
- **"Sending you the file only."** → "Sending the file now." (Drop "only.")
- **Over-apologising:** "Sorry sorry, my mistake, extremely sorry" → "Ah, my bad — fixing now 🙂"

---

**Chapter 27 summary.** Chat is quick, friendly, and to the point. Skip email-style formality, put your question in the first message, use a light emoji or softener to stay warm, and ask for help by showing what you've already tried. Match your team's emoji and channel style, and respect focus time and off-hours. Get the chat register right and you'll feel — and sound — like a natural part of the team.
