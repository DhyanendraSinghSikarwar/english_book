# Chapter 5: Career & People

Work is about people, not just tasks. This chapter gives you the phrasal verbs you need to talk about roles, teams, hiring, and growth. These phrases come up in one-on-ones, team chats, and career conversations. Using them well helps you sound natural when you talk about your job and the people around you.

---

#### take up (a role)
- **Meaning:** to start a new job or role.
- **Examples:**
  - She took up the role of team lead last month.
  - I'm taking up a new position in the data team.
  - He took up the offer from Amsterdam.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** We'd like you to lead the churn project.
  - **You:** Thank you — I'd be glad to take it up.
- **Formal alternative:** assume / accept
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Take up a role," not "take up on a role." Don't confuse with "take on" (to accept work), which is very close in meaning.

#### step down
- **Meaning:** to leave a senior position by choice.
- **Examples:**
  - The manager stepped down after five years.
  - She stepped down as team lead but stayed on the team.
  - He's stepping down at the end of the quarter.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Colleague:** Why is Marco leaving the lead role?
  - **You:** He's stepping down to focus on hands-on work.
- **Formal alternative:** resign (from a role) / relinquish
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Step down" means to leave a senior role, not to walk downstairs. It is neutral, not negative.

#### step up
- **Meaning:** to take more responsibility, often in a difficult moment.
- **Examples:**
  - She stepped up when the lead was on leave.
  - We need someone to step up and own this.
  - He really stepped up during the incident.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** The lead is off sick this week.
  - **You:** I can step up and run the stand-ups.
- **Formal alternative:** take charge / rise to the occasion
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Step up" (take responsibility) is different from "step up to" a place. Alone, it means to show leadership.

#### fill in for
- **Meaning:** to do someone's work while they are away.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll fill in for Anna next week.
  - Can you fill in for me at the review?
  - She filled in for the manager during his holiday.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Colleague:** Who's running the meeting? Priya's away.
  - **You:** I'm filling in for her today.
- **Formal alternative:** cover for / substitute for
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Fill in for" a person; "fill in" a form. Don't say "fill up for me." "Fill up" is for tanks and bottles.

#### report to
- **Meaning:** to have someone as your manager.
- **Examples:**
  - I report to the head of analytics.
  - She reports to me now.
  - Who do you report to?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **New colleague:** Who's your manager?
  - **You:** I report to Sofia, the data science lead.
- **Formal alternative:** answer to
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Report to [person]," not "report under [person]." "Reporting under X" is common in India but sounds odd in Europe.

#### get along with
- **Meaning:** to have a good relationship with someone.
- **Examples:**
  - I get along well with the engineering team.
  - They don't get along.
  - She gets along with everyone.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** How's the new team?
  - **You:** Great — I get along really well with them.
- **Formal alternative:** have a good rapport with
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Get along with," not "get along to." Some Indians say "adjust with" — in Europe, use "get along with."

#### look up to
- **Meaning:** to admire and respect someone.
- **Examples:**
  - Juniors look up to her.
  - I look up to my old mentor.
  - The team looks up to him for guidance.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Colleague:** Why did you join this team?
  - **You:** I've always looked up to the lead's work on forecasting.
- **Formal alternative:** admire / respect
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Look up to" (admire) vs "look up" (search for information). Add "to [person]" for admiration.

#### put up with
- **Meaning:** to tolerate something unpleasant.
- **Examples:**
  - We put up with slow servers for months.
  - I can't put up with these delays.
  - She puts up with a lot.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The old tool crashed daily.
  - **Colleague:** How did you put up with it for so long?
- **Formal alternative:** tolerate / endure
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Put up with" (tolerate) is different from "put up" (build or host). Keep all three words together.

#### count on
- **Meaning:** to trust someone to do something.
- **Examples:**
  - I can count on her to deliver.
  - The team counts on you.
  - Can we count on the vendor?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** Can I count on you to have this ready by Friday?
  - **You:** Yes, you can count on me.
- **Formal alternative:** rely on / depend on
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Count on [someone]," not "count upon." Both exist, but "count on" is normal in speech.

#### rely on
- **Meaning:** to depend on someone or something.
- **Examples:**
  - We rely on this pipeline daily.
  - Don't rely on manual checks.
  - I rely on her feedback.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Colleague:** Should we automate this?
  - **You:** Yes — we can't rely on someone remembering to run it.
- **Formal alternative:** depend on
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Rely on," never "rely upon" in casual speech, and never "rely to."

#### team up
- **Meaning:** to join with others to work together.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's team up on this feature.
  - We teamed up with marketing.
  - Two of us teamed up to fix it.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** This is a big task for one person.
  - **Colleague:** Let's team up and split it.
- **Formal alternative:** collaborate / partner
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Team up with [someone]." Don't say "team up together" — "up" already means together.

#### bring in
- **Meaning:** to involve a new person or resource.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's bring in a specialist.
  - We brought in extra help for the launch.
  - They brought in a consultant.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** This needs deeper stats knowledge.
  - **You:** Should we bring in someone from the research team?
- **Formal alternative:** engage / involve
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Bring in" (involve someone) vs "bring" (carry). "Bring in" adds a person or skill to a group.

#### take on (hire)
- **Meaning:** to hire a new person.
- **Examples:**
  - We took on two data scientists.
  - The team is taking on interns this summer.
  - They took her on last year.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** We're stretched thin.
  - **Manager:** We're taking on two new hires next month.
- **Formal alternative:** hire / recruit
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Take on" can mean both "accept work" and "hire." Context makes it clear. Don't say "take in" for hiring.

#### let go
- **Meaning:** to end someone's employment (a gentle way to say "fire").
- **Examples:**
  - They had to let two people go.
  - Sadly, she was let go.
  - The company let go of 5% of staff.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Colleague:** What happened to the contractor?
  - **You:** His contract ended and they let him go.
- **Formal alternative:** dismiss / terminate employment
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Let [someone] go" is softer than "fire." "Let go of" also means to release your grip — context decides.

#### lay off
- **Meaning:** to end jobs because of business reasons, not performance.
- **Examples:**
  - The firm laid off 100 people.
  - He was laid off during the cuts.
  - They're laying off staff to save costs.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Why did so many leave?
  - **Colleague:** There were layoffs after the budget cut.
- **Formal alternative:** make redundant (British) / downsize
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Lay off" (job cuts) is different from "let go" (can be for any reason). Note the noun is "layoffs."

#### burn out
- **Meaning:** to become exhausted from too much work.
- **Examples:**
  - He burned out after months of overtime.
  - Don't burn yourself out.
  - The team is close to burning out.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** You've worked three weekends.
  - **You:** I know — I need a break before I burn out.
- **Formal alternative:** become exhausted / overwork
- **Indian-English pitfall:** The noun is "burnout" (one word). The verb is "burn out" (two words). In Europe, saying you're near burnout is taken seriously.

#### get ahead
- **Meaning:** to make progress in your career.
- **Examples:**
  - She works hard to get ahead.
  - Networking helps you get ahead.
  - He got ahead by learning new skills.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Colleague:** How did you become a lead so fast?
  - **You:** I took on hard projects to get ahead.
- **Formal alternative:** advance / progress
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Get ahead" (make progress) vs "go ahead" (proceed). Different meanings — don't mix them.

#### move up
- **Meaning:** to get promoted or rise in rank.
- **Examples:**
  - She moved up to senior analyst.
  - There's room to move up here.
  - He moved up quickly.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** What's the growth path?
  - **Manager:** You can move up to lead within two years.
- **Formal alternative:** get promoted / advance
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Move up," not "move upward" in speech. Say "move up to [role]."

#### branch out
- **Meaning:** to try something new, beyond your usual area.
- **Examples:**
  - I want to branch out into engineering.
  - She branched out from reporting to modelling.
  - We're branching out into new markets.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** You've only done dashboards so far.
  - **You:** I'd like to branch out into predictive models.
- **Formal alternative:** diversify / expand into
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Branch out into [area]." Don't say "branch out to." It means to widen your skills or scope.

#### settle in
- **Meaning:** to get comfortable in a new place or job.
- **Examples:**
  - Take a week to settle in.
  - She settled in quickly.
  - How are you settling in?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** It's your second week — how are you settling in?
  - **You:** Really well, thanks. The team's been very helpful.
- **Formal alternative:** adjust / acclimatise
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Settle in" (get comfortable) vs "settle down" (calm down or start a family). At work, use "settle in."

#### fit in
- **Meaning:** to feel like you belong in a group.
- **Examples:**
  - He fits in well with the team.
  - It took time to fit in.
  - She didn't quite fit in at first.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The new hire seems quiet.
  - **Colleague:** Give her time to fit in.
- **Formal alternative:** integrate / belong
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Fit in" (belong) vs "fit in" a task (find time for it). Both exist; context decides.

#### show up
- **Meaning:** to arrive or appear.
- **Examples:**
  - Half the team showed up late.
  - She always shows up prepared.
  - He didn't show up to the call.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** Was the client on the call?
  - **You:** No, they didn't show up.
- **Formal alternative:** attend / appear
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Show up" is a little informal. In formal writing use "attend." Don't say "show off," which means to boast.

#### turn up
- **Meaning:** to arrive, often unexpectedly; also to be found.
- **Examples:**
  - He turned up an hour late.
  - The missing file turned up in the archive.
  - Nobody turned up to the optional session.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Did we lose that report?
  - **Colleague:** No, it turned up in the shared drive.
- **Formal alternative:** arrive / appear
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Turn up" (arrive/appear) is close to "show up." "Turn up" can also mean to increase volume — context decides.

#### stand out
- **Meaning:** to be clearly better or more noticeable.
- **Examples:**
  - Her analysis really stood out.
  - This candidate stands out.
  - One result stood out from the rest.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** Why did we pick this vendor?
  - **You:** Their support really stood out.
- **Formal alternative:** be exceptional / be notable
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Stand out" (be noticeable) is positive at work. Don't confuse with "stand up," which means to rise.

#### reach out to
- **Meaning:** to contact someone.
- **Examples:**
  - Reach out to me if you need help.
  - I reached out to the vendor.
  - She reached out to a mentor.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** We need input from finance.
  - **You:** I'll reach out to them today.
- **Formal alternative:** contact / get in touch with
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Reach out to [someone]," not "reach out [someone]." Common and polite in European work culture.

## More career & people phrasal verbs

#### bring on
- **Meaning:** to add someone to a team.
- **Examples:**
  - We brought on a new analyst.
  - They're bringing on more staff.
  - She was brought on to lead hiring.
- **Formal alternative:** onboard / recruit
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Bring on" (add to team) vs "bring in" — very close; both are fine.

#### take after
- **Meaning:** to be similar to someone (usually a mentor or family).
- **Examples:**
  - She takes after her old manager's style.
  - He takes after his mentor.
- **Formal alternative:** resemble
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Take after" means to resemble, not to follow behind.

#### work under
- **Meaning:** to be managed by someone.
- **Examples:**
  - I worked under a great leader.
  - She works under the CTO.
- **Formal alternative:** be supervised by
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Work under [person]" is acceptable, but "report to" sounds more modern and less hierarchical in Europe.

#### fall out (with)
- **Meaning:** to have an argument and stop being friendly.
- **Examples:**
  - The two leads fell out over the budget.
  - I don't want to fall out with a colleague.
- **Formal alternative:** have a disagreement
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Fall out with [someone]." Don't confuse with "fall out" of a bag (drop out).

#### win over
- **Meaning:** to get someone's support or trust.
- **Examples:**
  - She won over the whole team.
  - We need to win over the stakeholders.
- **Formal alternative:** persuade / gain support
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Win over [someone]," not "win on." It means to gain trust, not to defeat.

#### settle down
- **Meaning:** to become calm and stable.
- **Examples:**
  - Things settled down after the reorg.
  - Let the team settle down before big changes.
- **Formal alternative:** stabilise
- **Indian-English pitfall:** At work, "settle down" means to become calm — different from "settle in" (get comfortable).

#### hit it off
- **Meaning:** to get along well immediately.
- **Examples:**
  - We hit it off at the first meeting.
  - They really hit it off.
- **Formal alternative:** build instant rapport
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Fixed phrase — always "hit it off," never "hit off."

#### bow out
- **Meaning:** to withdraw or leave gracefully.
- **Examples:**
  - He bowed out of the race for the lead role.
  - She bowed out of the project.
- **Formal alternative:** withdraw / step aside
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Bow out" is graceful and voluntary, unlike being pushed out.
