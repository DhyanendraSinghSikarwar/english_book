# Chapter 9: Verbs of Communication

These verbs describe how you share information, explain ideas, and work with others through words. Choosing the right one makes your emails and meetings clearer and more professional.

**How to read each entry:** the verb, a simple meaning, common word partners, two examples, and a workplace scenario line.

---

### convey
- **Meaning:** to get a message or feeling across.
- **Collocations:** convey a message, meaning, an idea, urgency.
- **Examples:** The chart conveys the trend clearly. / I want to convey how important this is.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "This slide conveys the key result in one glance."

### articulate
- **Meaning:** to express an idea clearly in words.
- **Collocations:** articulate a vision, a plan, concerns, a point.
- **Examples:** She articulated the strategy well. / Let me articulate my concern.
- **Scenario:** To leadership: "Let me articulate the trade-off clearly: speed versus cost."

### clarify
- **Meaning:** to make something clearer.
- **Collocations:** clarify a point, a question, expectations, a doubt.
- **Examples:** Let me clarify what I mean. / Can you clarify the deadline?
- **Scenario:** In an email: "Just to clarify, we need the data by Thursday, not Friday."

### elaborate
- **Meaning:** to give more detail.
- **Collocations:** elaborate on a point, an idea, a plan.
- **Examples:** Could you elaborate on that? / Let me elaborate on the second point.
- **Scenario:** In a meeting: "That's interesting — could you elaborate on the risk you mentioned?"

### emphasise
- **Meaning:** to give special importance to something.
- **Collocations:** emphasise a point, the importance, a risk, a benefit.
- **Examples:** I want to emphasise the deadline. / She emphasised data quality.
- **Scenario:** In a presentation: "I'd like to emphasise that this result is preliminary."

### highlight
- **Meaning:** to draw attention to something.
- **Collocations:** highlight a finding, a risk, a change, an issue.
- **Examples:** Let me highlight the key numbers. / The report highlights three risks.
- **Scenario:** To stakeholders: "I'll highlight the main takeaway before we dive in."

### outline
- **Meaning:** to give the main points without detail.
- **Collocations:** outline a plan, an approach, the steps, an agenda.
- **Examples:** Let me outline the plan. / She outlined the next steps.
- **Scenario:** At the start of a meeting: "First, let me outline what we'll cover today."

### summarise
- **Meaning:** to give a short version of the main points.
- **Collocations:** summarise findings, a discussion, results, a report.
- **Examples:** Let me summarise the key points. / To summarise, we're on track.
- **Scenario:** At the end of a meeting: "To summarise, we agreed on three action points."

### address
- **Meaning:** to deal with a topic, question, or problem.
- **Collocations:** address a concern, a question, an issue, feedback.
- **Examples:** Let me address your concern. / We addressed all the feedback.
- **Scenario:** In a Q&A: "Let me address that question in two parts."

### acknowledge
- **Meaning:** to accept or recognise something.
- **Collocations:** acknowledge a point, a mistake, feedback, receipt.
- **Examples:** I acknowledge the delay was my fault. / She acknowledged the good work.
- **Scenario:** In an email: "I acknowledge there was a mistake, and here's how we'll fix it."

### advise
- **Meaning:** to recommend or inform (formal).
- **Collocations:** advise a course of action, caution, someone that.
- **Examples:** I'd advise waiting for more data. / Please advise on the next steps.
- **Scenario:** In an email: "I'd advise we run one more test before launching."

### inform
- **Meaning:** to tell someone officially.
- **Collocations:** inform a team, stakeholders, someone of/about.
- **Examples:** I'll inform the team of the change. / We were informed about the delay.
- **Scenario:** In an email: "I'm writing to inform you that the report will be a day late."

### notify
- **Meaning:** to formally tell someone about something.
- **Collocations:** notify a team, users, someone of a change.
- **Examples:** We notified users of the outage. / Please notify me when it's done.
- **Scenario:** To your team: "The system will notify us if a job fails."

### propose
- **Meaning:** to suggest a plan or idea formally.
- **Collocations:** propose a solution, a change, an approach, a plan.
- **Examples:** I propose we use a simpler model. / She proposed a new process.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "I propose we run a two-week pilot before a full launch."

### recommend
- **Meaning:** to suggest something as the best option.
- **Collocations:** recommend an approach, a tool, an action.
- **Examples:** I recommend the second option. / We recommend delaying the launch.
- **Scenario:** In a report: "Based on the results, we recommend option B."

### suggest
- **Meaning:** to put forward an idea for consideration.
- **Collocations:** suggest an idea, an approach, an alternative.
- **Examples:** May I suggest a different approach? / The data suggests a seasonal effect.
- **Scenario:** In a meeting: "I'd suggest we test this on a small group first."

### negotiate
- **Meaning:** to discuss to reach an agreement.
- **Collocations:** negotiate a deal, terms, a deadline, a price.
- **Examples:** We negotiated a better rate. / Let's negotiate the timeline.
- **Scenario:** With a stakeholder: "Can we negotiate the deadline? Two more days would help quality."

### persuade
- **Meaning:** to convince someone to agree or act.
- **Collocations:** persuade someone to, of the value, a team.
- **Examples:** The data persuaded the board. / I persuaded them to fund the pilot.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "The results should persuade leadership to invest more."

### present
- **Meaning:** to show and explain something to an audience.
- **Collocations:** present findings, results, a plan, a proposal.
- **Examples:** I'll present the results on Friday. / She presented to the whole company.
- **Scenario:** To your team: "I'm presenting our findings to the leadership team next week."

### brief
- **Meaning:** to give someone the key information quickly.
- **Collocations:** brief a team, a manager, stakeholders, someone on.
- **Examples:** Let me brief you on the status. / She briefed the CEO before the call.
- **Scenario:** Before a meeting: "Let me briefly brief you on where we stand."

### explain
- **Meaning:** to make something clear by describing it.
- **Collocations:** explain a concept, a result, a decision, a process.
- **Examples:** Let me explain how the model works. / She explained the delay.
- **Scenario:** In a presentation: "Let me explain this in simple terms for the business team."

### describe
- **Meaning:** to say what something is like.
- **Collocations:** describe a process, an approach, results.
- **Examples:** Let me describe the approach. / He described the problem clearly.
- **Scenario:** In an interview: "Describe a project where you used machine learning."

### discuss
- **Meaning:** to talk about something with others.
- **Collocations:** discuss options, a plan, results, an issue.
- **Examples:** Let's discuss this in the meeting. / We discussed three options.
- **Scenario:** In an email: "I'd like to discuss the results with you this week."

### report
- **Meaning:** to give an account of something officially.
- **Collocations:** report progress, results, an issue, findings.
- **Examples:** I'll report the progress weekly. / She reported the bug to the team.
- **Scenario:** To your manager: "I'll report back once the tests are done."

### confirm
- **Meaning:** to say that something is definitely true.
- **Collocations:** confirm a time, a booking, a figure, receipt.
- **Examples:** Can you confirm the meeting time? / I confirm the numbers are correct.
- **Scenario:** In an email: "I confirm I'll attend the 3 p.m. call."

### respond
- **Meaning:** to answer or react.
- **Collocations:** respond to a query, an email, feedback, a request.
- **Examples:** I'll respond by tomorrow. / She responded to all the comments.
- **Scenario:** In an email: "I'll respond to your questions point by point."

### raise
- **Meaning:** to bring up a topic or concern.
- **Collocations:** raise a concern, a question, an issue, a point.
- **Examples:** I'd like to raise a concern. / She raised a good point.
- **Scenario:** In a meeting: "Before we close, I'd like to raise one issue."

### stress
- **Meaning:** to emphasise strongly.
- **Collocations:** stress the importance, a point, the need.
- **Examples:** I must stress how urgent this is. / She stressed the need for testing.
- **Scenario:** In a presentation: "I want to stress that these numbers are still estimates."

### reiterate
- **Meaning:** to say something again for emphasis.
- **Collocations:** reiterate a point, a message, the importance.
- **Examples:** Let me reiterate the key deadline. / She reiterated the plan.
- **Scenario:** At the end of a meeting: "To reiterate, the report is due Friday at noon."

### relay
- **Meaning:** to pass a message from one person to another.
- **Collocations:** relay a message, information, feedback.
- **Examples:** I'll relay your feedback to the team. / She relayed the update to us.
- **Scenario:** In an email: "I'll relay this to the engineering team and get back to you."

### update
- **Meaning:** to give the latest information.
- **Collocations:** update a team, someone on, a status.
- **Examples:** I'll update you tomorrow. / She updated the team on progress.
- **Scenario:** Stand-up: "Quick update: the pipeline is fixed and running again."

### consult
- **Meaning:** to ask someone for advice or information.
- **Collocations:** consult an expert, a team, data, a document.
- **Examples:** Let's consult the legal team. / I consulted the documentation.
- **Scenario:** In an email: "I'll consult the security team before we proceed."

### liaise
- **Meaning:** to work with another team to share information (British/formal).
- **Collocations:** liaise with a team, a department, a partner.
- **Examples:** I'll liaise with marketing. / She liaises between the teams.
- **Scenario:** In a report: "I'm liaising with the finance team on the cost figures."

### escalate
- **Meaning:** to raise an issue to a higher level.
- **Collocations:** escalate an issue, a concern, to a manager.
- **Examples:** We escalated the bug to the vendor. / I'll escalate this if it isn't fixed.
- **Scenario:** Stand-up: "The vendor isn't responding, so I'm escalating to their support lead."

### remind
- **Meaning:** to help someone remember.
- **Collocations:** remind a team, someone of/about, a deadline.
- **Examples:** Just a reminder about tomorrow's call. / She reminded us of the deadline.
- **Scenario:** In a message: "A quick reminder that reports are due end of day."

### announce
- **Meaning:** to make something known publicly.
- **Collocations:** announce a change, a launch, results, a decision.
- **Examples:** We announced the new feature today. / She announced the results at the all-hands.
- **Scenario:** To the team: "We'll announce the launch date next week."

### caution
- **Meaning:** to warn someone gently.
- **Collocations:** caution against, that, someone about.
- **Examples:** I'd caution against rushing this. / She cautioned that the data was small.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "I'd caution that these results come from a small sample."

### comment
- **Meaning:** to give an opinion or remark.
- **Collocations:** comment on a report, a result, a plan.
- **Examples:** Could you comment on the draft? / She commented on the findings.
- **Scenario:** In an email: "Please add any comments on the document by Friday."

### seek
- **Meaning:** to look for or ask for something.
- **Collocations:** seek feedback, approval, input, clarification.
- **Examples:** I'm seeking feedback on the design. / We sought approval from legal.
- **Scenario:** In an email: "I'm seeking your input before I finalise the approach."

### walk (someone) through
- **Meaning:** to explain something step by step.
- **Collocations:** walk through a process, a report, the steps.
- **Examples:** Let me walk you through the results. / She walked us through the plan.
- **Scenario:** In a meeting: "I'll walk you through the analysis one step at a time."

---

**Chapter 9 count: 40 power verbs.** In Europe, clear and direct communication is respected. Use "propose," "recommend," and "advise" to sound decisive, and "clarify" or "elaborate" to keep conversations clear.
