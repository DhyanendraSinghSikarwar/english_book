# Chapter 21: Before the Meeting

Good meetings start before anyone joins the call. This chapter covers scheduling across time zones, writing agendas, and rescheduling politely — all with ready-to-use language and templates.

**Format:** the exact sentence to use, when to use it, and the tone.

---

## 1. Scheduling a meeting

**Suggesting a time:**
- "Would Tuesday at 2 p.m. CET work for you?"
- "Are you free for 30 minutes on Thursday morning?"
- "Could we find 30 minutes next week to go over the results?"
- "What does your calendar look like on Wednesday?"

**Offering options (polite and efficient):**
- "I'm free Tuesday afternoon or Wednesday morning — what suits you better?"
- "Here are a few slots that work for me: [list]. Let me know which is easiest."

**Time-zone etiquette with Europe (important):**
- **Always state the time zone.** Say "2 p.m. CET" not just "2 p.m." Europe mostly uses CET/CEST; the UK and Ireland use GMT/BST.
- **Be aware of the gap.** India is 3.5–4.5 hours ahead of Central Europe. If you're coordinating with India, spell out both times: "3 p.m. CET, which is 6:30 p.m. IST."
- **Respect working hours.** Europeans protect their evenings. Avoid proposing meetings after ~5–6 p.m. their time unless truly necessary. (More on work-life boundaries in Chapter 33.)
- **Use a shared calendar or scheduling tool** when possible — it avoids back-and-forth.

**Confirming:**
- "Great — I'll send a calendar invite for Tuesday at 2 p.m. CET."
- "Confirmed. You'll get an invite shortly with the video link."

## 2. Writing an agenda email

An agenda tells people why the meeting exists and what to prepare. In Europe, sending an agenda in advance is expected and respected — it shows you value everyone's time.

**Full agenda email template:**

> **Subject:** Agenda — Churn model review, Tue 8 July, 14:00 CET
>
> Hi all,
>
> Thanks for making time on Tuesday. Here's what we'll cover in 30 minutes:
>
> 1. **Progress update** (5 min) — where the model stands.
> 2. **Key findings** (10 min) — what's driving churn.
> 3. **Decision needed** (10 min) — approve a pilot? (owner: [Name])
> 4. **Next steps and actions** (5 min).
>
> **To prepare:** please take a look at the attached one-page summary beforehand.
>
> **Goal of the meeting:** to decide whether we move ahead with a pilot.
>
> See you Tuesday,
> [Name]

**Why this works:**
- It states the goal clearly.
- It gives times per item, so the meeting stays on track.
- It marks the decision needed, so people come ready.
- It asks people to prepare, so you don't waste time explaining basics.

**Shorter version for a quick sync:**
> "Hi — quick 15-min sync tomorrow at 11:00 CET to align on the data issue. Goal: agree who fixes what. No prep needed."

## 3. Rescheduling or cancelling politely

Things change. The key is to give notice, apologise briefly, and offer an alternative.

**Rescheduling:**
- "I'm afraid I need to move our 3 o'clock — would tomorrow morning suit you instead?"
- "Something urgent has come up. Could we push our meeting to Thursday? Apologies for the short notice."
- "Would it be possible to shift our call by an hour? I have a clash. Sorry about that."

**Cancelling:**
- "I need to cancel today's sync, unfortunately. Let's pick it up next week — I'll send a new invite."
- "Let's cancel tomorrow's meeting; I don't think we'll have the data ready in time. I'll reschedule once we do."

**If you're running late:**
- "Running about five minutes late — please start without me if needed."
- "Apologies, I'm caught in another meeting. I'll join as soon as it wraps up."

**Tone note:** Apologise once, briefly, and move to the solution (a new time). Don't over-apologise with three sentences of sorry — one clear "apologies for the short notice" is enough. (See Chapter 14 on avoiding "revert" and "do the needful" in these emails.)

## 4. Declining a meeting politely

You don't have to accept every invite. In Europe, declining with a reason is normal and professional.

- "I don't think I'll add much value here — happy to read the notes instead. Feel free to go ahead without me."
- "My calendar's full that day. Could you share the outcome, or shall we find another time for just the two of us?"
- "This looks like an engineering topic — I'll skip unless you specifically need the data view."

## 5. Preparing yourself

Before an important meeting, prepare a few lines so you're not caught off guard:
- Your **one-sentence update** (what's the status).
- Your **one ask or point** (what you want from this meeting).
- Answers to the **two questions** you expect to be asked.

Even five minutes of prep makes you sound sharp and confident.

## Common Indian-English pitfalls before meetings

- **"Please revert with your availability."** → "Please let me know when you're free."
- **"Kindly confirm the same."** → "Please confirm."
- **"Preponing our meeting to 2 p.m."** → "Moving our meeting earlier, to 2 p.m."
- **"I want to fix a meeting with you."** → "I'd like to set up a meeting with you." ("Fix a meeting" is Indian English; "set up" or "arrange" is standard.)
- **"Are you free in the today evening?"** → "Are you free this evening?"

---

**Chapter 21 summary.** Strong meetings start with clear scheduling (always state the time zone), a well-written agenda that states the goal, and polite, brief communication when plans change. A little preparation before the meeting saves everyone time during it.
