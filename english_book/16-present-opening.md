# Chapter 16: Opening a Presentation

The first two minutes of a presentation decide how people listen to the rest. A strong opening makes you look confident and makes the audience want to pay attention. This chapter gives you ready-to-use sentences for every part of an opening.

**For each part you get:** full sentences you can use, when to use them, and how to deliver them (tone and body language). Data-science examples are marked **[DS]**.

---

## 1. Greeting and self-introduction

**Full sentences:**
- "Good morning, everyone. Thank you for joining."
- "Good afternoon. For those who don't know me, I'm Dhyanendra, and I lead the data science work for the retention team."
- "Hi everyone. I'm [Name], a data scientist on the analytics team. I've been working on the churn project you'll hear about today."

**When to use:** Always start with a greeting and a short intro, even to people who know you. Keep the intro to one sentence about who you are and why you're the right person to speak.

**How to deliver:** Stand or sit up straight. Smile. Make eye contact with a few people around the room (or look at the camera on video). Speak a little slower than feels natural — this signals confidence.

**[DS] Example:** "Good morning. I'm [Name] from the data team. Over the last two months I've been building a model to predict which customers are likely to leave, and today I'll share what we found."

## 2. Hooks — grab attention in the first 30 seconds

A hook is a strong first line that makes people curious. Choose one of three types.

**Type A — A striking number (great for data science):**
- "Let me start with a number: 40% of our customers who leave do so in their first month."
- "Here's a figure that surprised me: we're losing €2 million a year to a problem we can now predict."

**Type B — A question:**
- "How much would it be worth to know, today, which customers will leave next month?"
- "What if I told you we could cut churn in half without spending a cent more on marketing?"

**Type C — A short story or scenario:**
- "Last week, a customer cancelled after five years. Our data showed the warning signs three months earlier — we just weren't looking. Today, I'll show you how we can look."

**When to use:** Use a hook when you want engagement (most presentations). Skip it for very short, routine updates. Numbers work best with senior and technical audiences; stories work best with mixed or business audiences.

**How to deliver:** Pause for one second after your hook. Let it land. Don't rush into the next sentence.

## 3. The agenda — tell them the plan

**Full sentences:**
- "I'll cover three things today: first, the problem; then, what the data shows; and finally, what I recommend we do."
- "Here's how I'll structure this: a quick background, the key findings, and next steps."
- "This talk has three parts. By the end, you'll know what's driving churn and what we should do about it."

**When to use:** Almost always. People listen better when they know the map. Keep it to three or four points — more than that is hard to remember.

**How to deliver:** Count on your fingers as you say "first, second, third." It helps the audience follow and looks natural.

## 4. Setting the rules for questions

**Option A — Take questions at the end (good for short or formal talks):**
- "I'll take questions at the end, so please hold them until then."
- "Let's keep questions for the last five minutes, so I can get through the main points first."

**Option B — Welcome interruptions (good for small, informal groups):**
- "Feel free to interrupt me at any point — this works best as a discussion."
- "If anything isn't clear, just jump in."

**When to choose which:**
- Choose **"questions at the end"** for large audiences, senior leadership, tightly-timed slots, or when your points build on each other.
- Choose **"feel free to interrupt"** for small teams, working sessions, and when you want a two-way conversation.

**How to deliver:** Say your choice clearly and early. It gives you control and sets expectations.

## 5. The timing promise

**Full sentences:**
- "This will take no more than 20 minutes of your time."
- "I'll keep this to 15 minutes, leaving 10 for discussion."
- "I know everyone's busy, so I'll be brief — about 10 minutes."

**When to use:** Almost always, especially with senior people. A timing promise shows respect for their time and makes them relax and listen. **Important: keep the promise.** If you say 20 minutes, finish in 20.

**How to deliver:** Say it warmly, as a courtesy, not an apology.

## Putting it all together — a full opening script

Here is a complete, natural opening you can adapt. It uses all five parts in about 60 seconds:

> "Good morning, everyone, and thanks for joining. For those who don't know me, I'm [Name], and I lead the data science work on customer retention.
>
> Let me start with a number that surprised me: 40% of the customers we lose leave within their first month. [pause]
>
> Today I'll cover three things: what's driving that early churn, what our new model can predict, and what I'd recommend we do next. I'll keep this to about 15 minutes and leave time for questions at the end.
>
> So let's start with the problem."

---

## Openings to avoid

- **The apology opening:** "Sorry, I didn't have much time to prepare..." Never lower expectations. It makes people trust you less before you've begun.
- **The rambling intro:** Long life stories or too much background. Get to the point.
- **The tech-dump opening [DS]:** Starting with model architecture before saying why anyone should care. Lead with the business problem, then the tech.
- **Reading your slides:** Your first slide is a backdrop, not a script. Talk to people, not to the screen.

---

## Common Indian-English pitfalls in openings

- **"Myself [Name]"** → "I'm [Name]." (See Chapter 14.)
- **"Today I am going to present about..."** → "Today I'll present..." (drop "about"; "present" doesn't need it).
- **"Respected sir/madam, ladies and gentlemen"** → "Good morning, everyone." (Too formal for a European workplace.)
- **"First of all, I would like to thank..."** (long thanks) → keep thanks to one short line.
- **"I will be discussing about..."** → "I'll discuss..." (no "about").

---

**Chapter 16 summary.** A great opening is short, confident, and clear: greet, hook, give the agenda, set question rules, and promise your timing. Practise your opening until you can say it without notes — it's the part that sets the tone for everything else.
