# Chapter 11: Verbs of Management & Leadership

As you grow, you will lead people, own projects, and make decisions. These verbs describe managing, guiding, and controlling work. They make you sound senior and in charge — useful for reviews, promotions, and stakeholder talk.

**How to read each entry:** the verb, a simple meaning, common word partners, two examples, and a workplace scenario line.

---

### oversee
- **Meaning:** to watch over and be responsible for work.
- **Collocations:** oversee a project, a team, operations, a migration.
- **Examples:** I oversee three data projects. / She oversees the whole pipeline.
- **Scenario:** In a self-review: "I oversee the analytics work for two product teams."

### supervise
- **Meaning:** to manage and check someone's work.
- **Collocations:** supervise a team, an intern, a process.
- **Examples:** I supervise two junior analysts. / She supervised the migration closely.
- **Scenario:** In a one-on-one: "I supervise the interns and review their code."

### delegate
- **Meaning:** to give a task to someone else.
- **Collocations:** delegate a task, responsibility, work, authority.
- **Examples:** I delegated the report to a junior. / Good leaders delegate.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "I learned to delegate the routine tasks so I can focus on modelling."

### allocate
- **Meaning:** to give out resources for a purpose.
- **Collocations:** allocate resources, budget, time, people.
- **Examples:** We allocated two people to the project. / Let's allocate more time to testing.
- **Scenario:** In planning: "We'll allocate one sprint to cleaning up the data."

### prioritise
- **Meaning:** to decide what is most important to do first.
- **Collocations:** prioritise tasks, work, a backlog, features.
- **Examples:** Let's prioritise the urgent bugs. / We prioritised speed over polish.
- **Scenario:** Sprint planning: "Let's prioritise the data quality fix — it blocks everything else."

### authorise
- **Meaning:** to give official permission.
- **Collocations:** authorise access, a payment, a change, a release.
- **Examples:** Only the lead can authorise access. / She authorised the extra budget.
- **Scenario:** In an email: "Once you authorise the access, I can start pulling the data."

### approve
- **Meaning:** to formally agree to something.
- **Collocations:** approve a plan, a budget, a request, a change.
- **Examples:** The manager approved the plan. / Please approve the access request.
- **Scenario:** In an email: "Could you approve the pull request when you get a moment?"

### endorse
- **Meaning:** to publicly support something.
- **Collocations:** endorse a plan, an approach, a candidate, a tool.
- **Examples:** The team endorsed the new process. / She endorsed the proposal.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "Leadership endorsed our recommendation to expand the pilot."

### mentor
- **Meaning:** to guide and teach a less experienced person.
- **Collocations:** mentor a junior, a new hire, a team member.
- **Examples:** I mentor two junior data scientists. / She mentored me early on.
- **Scenario:** In a self-review: "I mentor two juniors and help them grow their modelling skills."

### empower
- **Meaning:** to give people the power to act.
- **Collocations:** empower a team, users, someone to.
- **Examples:** The tool empowers teams to self-serve data. / We empower our juniors to decide.
- **Scenario:** To leadership: "This dashboard empowers business teams to answer their own questions."

### motivate
- **Meaning:** to make people want to work well.
- **Collocations:** motivate a team, staff, someone to.
- **Examples:** A clear goal motivates the team. / She motivates everyone around her.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "I kept the team motivated through a tough deadline."

### resolve
- **Meaning:** to settle or fix a problem.
- **Collocations:** resolve an issue, a conflict, a bug, a dispute.
- **Examples:** We resolved the data issue. / She resolved the disagreement quickly.
- **Scenario:** Stand-up: "I resolved the pipeline failure — it was a bad config."

### mediate
- **Meaning:** to help two sides reach agreement.
- **Collocations:** mediate a dispute, a conflict, a discussion.
- **Examples:** She mediated between the two teams. / I mediated the disagreement.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "I mediated a disagreement between engineering and analytics on the data model."

### enforce
- **Meaning:** to make sure rules are followed.
- **Collocations:** enforce a policy, a standard, rules, a deadline.
- **Examples:** We enforce a code review rule. / The system enforces data checks.
- **Scenario:** To your team: "We enforce a rule that no code ships without tests."

### comply
- **Meaning:** to follow rules or requirements.
- **Collocations:** comply with a policy, a regulation, GDPR, standards.
- **Examples:** We comply with data protection rules. / The process complies with the policy.
- **Scenario:** In a report: "Our data handling complies fully with GDPR."

### adhere
- **Meaning:** to stick closely to rules or a plan.
- **Collocations:** adhere to a standard, a process, guidelines, a schedule.
- **Examples:** We adhere to strict naming rules. / The team adheres to the coding standard.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "We adhere to the data governance guidelines at every step."

### monitor
- **Meaning:** to watch something over time to spot changes.
- **Collocations:** monitor performance, a system, metrics, drift.
- **Examples:** We monitor the model daily. / Let's monitor for data drift.
- **Scenario:** To your team: "We monitor the model's accuracy and alert if it drops."

### track
- **Meaning:** to follow the progress or status of something.
- **Collocations:** track progress, metrics, tasks, spending.
- **Examples:** We track key metrics weekly. / I track the project in the board.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "We track accuracy over time to catch any decline early."

### review
- **Meaning:** to check work or progress carefully.
- **Collocations:** review code, performance, a plan, results.
- **Examples:** I review all the team's code. / We review progress every Friday.
- **Scenario:** In a one-on-one: "Let's review your goals for this quarter."

### audit
- **Meaning:** to inspect something officially for accuracy.
- **Collocations:** audit data, a process, accounts, access.
- **Examples:** We audited the data pipeline. / Access is audited monthly.
- **Scenario:** In a report: "We audited the pipeline and found no data quality issues."

### lead
- **Meaning:** to guide a team or effort.
- **Collocations:** lead a team, a project, an initiative, a workshop.
- **Examples:** I lead the analytics team. / She led the migration.
- **Scenario:** In a self-review: "I led the churn project from idea to production."

### direct
- **Meaning:** to control and guide something.
- **Collocations:** direct a team, resources, efforts, attention.
- **Examples:** She directs the data strategy. / We directed our effort to the top issues.
- **Scenario:** To leadership: "I directed the team's effort toward the highest-impact fixes."

### govern
- **Meaning:** to control with rules and structure.
- **Collocations:** govern data, access, a process, usage.
- **Examples:** Clear rules govern data access. / We govern how models are deployed.
- **Scenario:** In a report: "A clear policy governs who can access customer data."

### own
- **Meaning:** to take full responsibility for something.
- **Collocations:** own a project, a metric, a decision, an outcome.
- **Examples:** I own the reporting pipeline. / She owns the churn metric.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "I own the end-to-end pipeline, from data to dashboard."

### drive (an initiative)
- **Meaning:** to lead and push an effort forward.
- **Collocations:** drive an initiative, adoption, change, a program.
- **Examples:** I drive the data quality initiative. / She drives adoption of the new tool.
- **Scenario:** In a self-review: "I drove the initiative to automate our reporting."

### coordinate
- **Meaning:** to organise people and tasks to work together.
- **Collocations:** coordinate teams, efforts, a release, schedules.
- **Examples:** I coordinate work across three teams. / We coordinated the launch.
- **Scenario:** To leadership: "I coordinate between data, engineering, and product."

### assign
- **Meaning:** to give a task or role to someone.
- **Collocations:** assign a task, a ticket, ownership, a role.
- **Examples:** I assigned the bug to Raj. / Let's assign owners to each action.
- **Scenario:** At the end of a meeting: "I'll assign each action item to an owner."

### escalate
- **Meaning:** to raise a serious issue to higher management.
- **Collocations:** escalate an issue, a risk, a blocker.
- **Examples:** I escalated the delay to my manager. / We escalated the outage.
- **Scenario:** Stand-up: "This is blocking the release, so I'm escalating it today."

### evaluate (performance)
- **Meaning:** to judge how well someone or something performs.
- **Collocations:** evaluate performance, a team member, results.
- **Examples:** We evaluate performance quarterly. / I evaluated each option carefully.
- **Scenario:** In a one-on-one: "Let's evaluate how the quarter went and set new goals."

### foster
- **Meaning:** to encourage the growth of something good.
- **Collocations:** foster collaboration, a culture, trust, learning.
- **Examples:** We foster a learning culture. / She fosters open discussion.
- **Scenario:** In a self-review: "I foster a culture where juniors feel safe to ask questions."

### champion
- **Meaning:** to strongly support and promote something.
- **Collocations:** champion a cause, an idea, a change, best practice.
- **Examples:** I champion clean code across the team. / She champions data-driven decisions.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "I championed automated testing until the whole team adopted it."

### steer
- **Meaning:** to guide the direction of something.
- **Collocations:** steer a project, a discussion, a team, strategy.
- **Examples:** I steered the project back on track. / She steered the discussion to the key point.
- **Scenario:** In a meeting: "Let me steer us back to the main decision we need to make."

### facilitate
- **Meaning:** to lead a session and help it run well.
- **Collocations:** facilitate a meeting, a workshop, a retro.
- **Examples:** I facilitate the weekly retro. / She facilitated the planning session.
- **Scenario:** In a calendar invite: "I'll facilitate the retrospective on Friday."

### coach
- **Meaning:** to help someone improve through guidance.
- **Collocations:** coach a team member, a junior, on a skill.
- **Examples:** I coach juniors on presenting. / She coached me through my first talk.
- **Scenario:** In a self-review: "I coach team members on how to explain models to business users."

### hold accountable
- **Meaning:** to make someone responsible for results.
- **Collocations:** hold someone accountable, for a target, for delivery.
- **Examples:** We hold each owner accountable for their metric. / I'm held accountable for accuracy.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "Each person owns a metric and is held accountable for it."

### set (goals/standards)
- **Meaning:** to decide targets or rules for others.
- **Collocations:** set goals, standards, expectations, a direction.
- **Examples:** I set clear goals for the team. / We set high standards for data quality.
- **Scenario:** In a one-on-one: "Let's set three clear goals for the next quarter."

### plan
- **Meaning:** to decide in advance what to do.
- **Collocations:** plan a project, a sprint, a roadmap, resources.
- **Examples:** We plan the sprint every two weeks. / She planned the whole rollout.
- **Scenario:** In planning: "Let's plan the next two sprints around the migration."

### organise
- **Meaning:** to arrange things or people in order.
- **Collocations:** organise a team, work, a meeting, resources.
- **Examples:** I organised the work into clear phases. / She organised the workshop.
- **Scenario:** To your team: "I've organised the tasks into three clear stages."

### streamline (a team)
- **Meaning:** to make a team or process leaner and more efficient.
- **Collocations:** streamline operations, a team, decision-making.
- **Examples:** We streamlined how decisions are made. / The process was streamlined.
- **Scenario:** In a review: "I streamlined our stand-ups so they take ten minutes, not thirty."

### unblock
- **Meaning:** to remove something that stops progress.
- **Collocations:** unblock a task, a team, a dependency.
- **Examples:** I unblocked the team by getting access approved. / Let's unblock this first.
- **Scenario:** Stand-up: "My goal today is to unblock the team on the data access issue."

## Part 2 wrap-up

Across Chapters 7–11 you now have **200 business power verbs**. These verbs are the difference between sounding like a beginner and sounding like a senior professional. The trick is precision: pick the verb that says exactly what you did. When you "oversee," "own," "drive," and "resolve," you show leadership without ever saying "I am a leader." Keep a short list of your ten favourite power verbs and use them in your next self-review — they make your work sound as strong as it truly is.
