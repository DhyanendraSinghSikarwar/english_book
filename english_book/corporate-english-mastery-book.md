### Chapter 1: Meetings & Discussions (Pages 9–24) — 30 verbs

**Sample entry — "wrap up":**
- **Meaning:** to finish/conclude something
- **Examples:** "Let's wrap up the meeting — we're out of time." / "I'll wrap up the analysis by Friday." / "Before we wrap up, any questions?"
- **Scenario:** You're hosting a stand-up that's running long. Instead of "Let us stop now" (too abrupt), say: "Okay everyone, let's wrap up here and take the remaining points offline."
- **Formal alternative:** conclude
- **Pitfall:** Indians often say "let us wind up the meeting" — 'wind up' is fine in India/UK but can also mean 'to annoy someone' in British slang; 'wrap up' is safer.

### Chapter 14: Words to STOP Using (Indian-English → International) (Pages 187–194)
- "do the needful" → "please take the necessary action / please proceed"
- "revert" (meaning reply) → "get back to me / respond"
- "prepone" → "bring forward / move up"
- "kindly" (overuse) → "please" (and when to drop it entirely)
- "the same" ("please confirm the same") → "please confirm it/this"
- "today morning" → "this morning" | "yesterday night" → "last night"
- "out of station" → "out of office / away" | "passed out" (graduated) → "graduated"
- "sir/madam" culture → first names in Europe (when and how to switch)

# PART 4 — PRESENTATIONS: EVERY SCENARIO SCRIPTED (Pages 201–270)

> **Format for every section:** Full sentences ready to use | When to use | How to deliver (tone, pace, body language) | Data-science example version

### Chapter 16: Opening a Presentation (Pages 201–212)
- Greeting & self-intro: "Good morning everyone, for those who don't know me, I'm..., and I lead the... "
- Hooks: question, statistic, story ("Let me start with a number: 40% of our churn...")
- Agenda: "I'll cover three things today: first..., then..., and finally..."
- Setting rules: "Feel free to interrupt" vs "I'll take questions at the end — When to choose which"
- Timing promise: "This will take no more than 20 minutes of your time."

### Chapter 17: The Body — Presenting Content (Pages 213–230)
- **Transitions:** "Moving on to... / That brings me to... / Now that we've seen X, let's look at Y"
- **Presenting data/charts (DS-critical):** "As you can see on this slide... / The x-axis represents... / Notice the spike in Q3... / The key takeaway from this chart is..."
- **Explaining models to non-technical audiences:** "In simple terms, the model learns patterns from past data... / Think of it as..."
- **Emphasising:** "I want to stress that... / What's particularly important here is..."
- **Referring back/forward:** "As I mentioned earlier... / I'll come back to this point shortly"
- **Handling uncertainty in results:** "The confidence interval suggests... / With the caveat that..."

### Chapter 18: Closing & Q&A (Pages 231–244)
- Summarising: "So, to sum up the three key points..."
- Call to action: "What I'd ask from this group is..."
- Q&A management — full scripts:
  - You know the answer: "Great question. The short answer is..."
  - You don't know: "That's a fair point — I don't have that figure to hand, but I'll follow up by email."
  - Hostile question: "I understand the concern. Let me address it in two parts..."
  - Question off-topic: "That's slightly outside today's scope, but happy to discuss it afterwards."
  - No questions coming: "One question I often get is..."

### Chapter 19: Special Presentation Scenarios (Pages 245–258)
- Presenting to senior leadership (C-suite): BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front) style + scripts
- Presenting bad results/failed model: "The experiment didn't confirm our hypothesis — here's what we learned..."
- Demo scripts (live product/model demo + what to say when the demo breaks)
- Impromptu "give us a quick update" moments — 60-second structures (PREP: Point, Reason, Example, Point)
- Conference-style talks vs internal reviews
- Co-presenting: handovers ("I'll now hand over to Priya, who'll walk you through...")

### Chapter 20: Delivery Skills (Pages 259–270)
- Pausing, pacing, stress patterns for Indian speakers (which syllables Europeans expect stressed)
- Filler-word elimination (basically, actually, like, you know)
