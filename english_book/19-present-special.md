# Chapter 19: Special Presentation Scenarios

Not every presentation is a normal slide talk. Sometimes you present to the CEO, sometimes your model failed, sometimes the demo breaks live, and sometimes you're asked for "a quick update" with no warning. This chapter gives you a playbook for each special situation.

---

## 1. Presenting to senior leadership (C-suite)

Senior leaders have little time and think about business impact, not technical detail. Use the **BLUF** method: **Bottom Line Up Front.** Say your conclusion and your ask first, then support it.

**BLUF structure:**
1. The bottom line (one sentence).
2. The ask or recommendation.
3. The two or three reasons.
4. Stop. Let them ask for more.

**Scripts:**
- "Bottom line: we can cut churn by 20%, and I'm asking for approval to run a pilot. Here's the reasoning in three points..."
- "The headline is that this project will save an estimated €2 million a year. I need two things from you to make it happen."
- "In short: the model works, it's ready, and I recommend we deploy. Let me give you the three reasons, then I'll take your questions."

**Rules for the C-suite:**
- Lead with the answer, not the journey. Never make them wait through your methodology to hear the result.
- Talk money and impact, not accuracy scores. "€2M saved" beats "AUC of 0.89."
- Keep it short. If you have 15 minutes, plan for 8 and leave 7 for discussion.
- Have the detail ready in backup slides, but don't show it unless asked.

## 2. Presenting bad results or a failed model

In data science, experiments fail often. That's normal science. How you present a failure shows your maturity.

**Scripts:**
- "The experiment didn't confirm our hypothesis. Here's what we learned and what I'd do differently."
- "The model didn't beat the baseline. That's a useful result — it tells us the signal isn't in this data, so let's look elsewhere."
- "This didn't work as hoped. Let me be clear about why, because the reason is valuable."
- "We took a calculated bet, and it didn't pay off. Here's the €X we spent, the €Y we saved by learning early, and my recommendation."

**Rules for presenting failure:**
- Be honest and direct. Don't hide it or bury it.
- Frame it as learning, not disaster.
- Show what you'll do next. A failure with a next step is progress.
- Don't over-apologise. State it once, explain, move to the lesson. (European cultures respect calm honesty about failure far more than defensiveness.)

## 3. Live demo scripts (product or model demo)

Demos are powerful but risky — things break. Prepare language for both success and failure.

**Setting up the demo:**
- "Let me show you this live. I'll walk through it as a real user would."
- "I'll demo the main flow. If anything's slow, it's my connection, not the product."

**Narrating as you go:**
- "So here I'm entering a customer ID... and the model returns a risk score of 0.82, meaning high risk."
- "Watch what happens when I click here..."

**When the demo breaks (have this ready):**
- "Well, that's live software for you! Let me try that once more."
- "It seems the connection dropped. Rather than wait, let me show you a recording of this exact flow."
- "That's not behaving as it should — I'll follow up with a working version, but let me talk you through what it does."

**Golden rule:** Always have a backup — a recorded video or screenshots. Never rely only on the live demo working. If it breaks, stay relaxed and switch to the backup. Panicking is worse than the bug.

## 4. Impromptu updates — "give us a quick update"

Sometimes you're asked to speak with zero warning. Use the **PREP** structure to sound organised instantly:

- **P**oint — your main message.
- **R**eason — why it's true.
- **E**xample — a specific fact or number.
- **P**oint — repeat the main message.

**Example (60 seconds):**
> "**Point:** The churn project is on track. **Reason:** We've finished the data work and the first model is already beating our baseline. **Example:** It's predicting churn correctly about 80% of the time on new data. **Point:** So we're on track to have it ready for a pilot next month."

**Other quick structures:**
- **Status update:** "Where we are / what's next / any blockers." (Same as a stand-up.)
- **Three-word frame:** "Good news, one risk, one ask." Then fill each in.

**Tip:** When put on the spot, take one breath before you start. A two-second pause looks thoughtful, not slow.

## 5. Conference-style talks vs internal reviews

These are different animals — adjust your style.

| | Conference talk | Internal review |
|---|---|---|
| Audience | Strangers, mixed background | Colleagues who know the context |
| Goal | Inspire, teach, share a story | Make a decision, report status |
| Detail | Less; more story and big ideas | More; specifics and numbers |
| Tone | Energetic, engaging | Direct, businesslike |
| Opening | Strong hook, more context | Brief; get to the point |

**Conference-style language:** "I want to share a story about..." / "Here's an idea that changed how we work..."

**Internal-review language:** "Here's where we are, here's the risk, here's what I need."

## 6. Co-presenting — handing over smoothly

When two people present together, clumsy handovers look unprofessional. Use clean handover lines.

**Handing over:**
- "I'll now hand over to Priya, who'll walk you through the results."
- "That's the background. Priya, over to you for the technical detail."
- "Priya knows this part best, so I'll let her take it from here."

**Taking over:**
- "Thanks, [Name]. So, building on that..."
- "Thank you. Let me pick up where [Name] left off."

**Rules for co-presenting:**
- Agree who covers what before you start.
- Use the other person's name in the handover — it's smooth and clear.
- The person not speaking should stay quiet and attentive (don't fidget or check your phone on camera).
- Agree a signal for "help me out" in case one of you gets stuck.

---

## Common Indian-English pitfalls in special scenarios

- **"I will just show you one demo."** → "Let me show you a quick demo." (Avoid "just one" as filler.)
- **"As of now, everything is fine only."** → "So far, everything's on track." (Drop "only"; "as of now" is fine but "so far" is smoother.)
- **"I will hand over to my colleague."** → correct, but add the name: "I'll hand over to Priya."
- **Over-apologising for a failure:** "I'm extremely sorry, it's completely my fault..." → State it once, calmly, then move to the lesson.

---

**Chapter 19 summary.** Match your approach to the situation: BLUF for executives, honest framing for failures, a backup plan for demos, PREP for impromptu moments, story for conferences, and clean handovers for co-presenting. Prepare these patterns in advance and no special scenario will catch you off guard.
