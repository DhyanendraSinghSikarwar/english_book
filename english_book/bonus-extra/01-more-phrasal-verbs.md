# Bonus 1: 120 More Phrasal Verbs

These phrasal verbs are **additional** to the 200+ in Part 1 (Chapters 1–6) — none are repeated here. Keep learning a few each week and use them at work.

**Format:** the phrasal verb — simple meaning. *Example:* a workplace sentence.

---

## Getting things done

1. **add up** — make sense; total correctly. *Example:* "These numbers don't add up — let me recheck."
2. **firm up** — make final or definite. *Example:* "Let's firm up the plan by Friday."
3. **lock in** — secure or fix something. *Example:* "We locked in the vendor's price."
4. **nail down** — settle something definitely. *Example:* "Can we nail down the scope today?"
5. **square away** — settle and organise. *Example:* "I'll square away the paperwork this afternoon."
6. **tee up** — prepare or set up for the next step. *Example:* "This meeting tees up the final decision."
7. **piece together** — assemble from separate parts. *Example:* "We pieced together the timeline from the logs."
8. **rough out** — make a rough first draft. *Example:* "I roughed out the design; details to follow."
9. **flesh out** — add detail to something basic. *Example:* "Let's flesh out the proposal."
10. **hammer out** — reach after hard discussion. *Example:* "We hammered out the terms over two calls."
11. **hand off** — pass responsibility to someone. *Example:* "I'll hand off the pipeline to Anna."
12. **carry over** — move to the next period. *Example:* "Unused leave carries over to next year."
13. **press ahead** — continue despite difficulty. *Example:* "Let's press ahead with the launch."
14. **push through** — force something to completion. *Example:* "We pushed the fix through before the deadline."
15. **put in** — submit or contribute. *Example:* "I put in a request for access."
16. **round up** — gather; raise to a whole number. *Example:* "Let's round up the team for a quick sync."
17. **whip up** — create quickly. *Example:* "I'll whip up a quick summary."
18. **knock out** — produce quickly. *Example:* "She knocked out three reports before lunch."
19. **turn around** — complete quickly; improve. *Example:* "We turned the analysis around in a day."
20. **wade through** — get through slowly (a lot of work). *Example:* "I'm wading through the backlog."

## Planning, time & effort

21. **block out** — reserve time. *Example:* "I've blocked out an hour for this."
22. **pencil in** — tentatively schedule. *Example:* "Let's pencil in Tuesday." *(Note: also in Part 1's extras; if seen there, treat as reinforcement.)*
23. **gear up** — prepare for something. *Example:* "We're gearing up for the release."
24. **keep at** — persist with. *Example:* "Keep at it — you're close."
25. **keep on** — continue. *Example:* "Keep on testing until it passes."
26. **ease off** — reduce gradually. *Example:* "Let's ease off the pace after crunch time."
27. **dial back** — reduce intensity. *Example:* "We dialled back the scope to hit the date."
28. **scale back** — reduce in size or scope. *Example:* "We scaled back the pilot to one region."
29. **ramp down** — reduce gradually (resources/effort). *Example:* "We'll ramp down the extra servers after the peak."
30. **level off** — stop rising and become steady. *Example:* "Usage has levelled off this month."
31. **even out** — become balanced. *Example:* "The workload evens out after the launch."
32. **pile up** — accumulate. *Example:* "Emails pile up when I'm on leave."
33. **rack up** — accumulate (costs, points). *Example:* "The project racked up high cloud costs."
34. **run up** — build up (a bill). *Example:* "We ran up a big bill on compute."
35. **speed up** — go faster. *Example:* "Caching sped up the dashboard."
36. **hold up** — delay; also remain valid. *Example:* "The access issue held us up." / "The theory holds up."
37. **fall back on** — use as a backup. *Example:* "We can fall back on the old model if needed."
38. **tide (someone) over** — help through a hard time. *Example:* "This budget will tide us over until Q3."
39. **top up** — add more. *Example:* "Let's top up the training data."
40. **draw on** — use (experience or resources). *Example:* "She drew on years of experience."

## Problems, control & pressure

41. **clamp down on** — enforce strictly. *Example:* "They clamped down on unused licences."
42. **rein in** — control or limit. *Example:* "We need to rein in the scope creep."
43. **stamp out** — eliminate completely. *Example:* "We stamped out the duplicate records."
44. **shut out** — exclude. *Example:* "The filter accidentally shut out valid users."
45. **cut off** — disconnect or stop supply. *Example:* "The feed was cut off overnight."
46. **cut back** — reduce. *Example:* "We cut back on manual checks."
47. **run down** — deplete; also criticise. *Example:* "The battery ran down." / "Don't run down the team's work."
48. **hold back** — restrain or withhold. *Example:* "We held back the release for one more test."
49. **pull back** — withdraw or reduce. *Example:* "We pulled back on the risky feature."
50. **prop up** — support something weak. *Example:* "Manual fixes are propping up the pipeline."
51. **shore up** — strengthen. *Example:* "We shored up the data checks."
52. **shake up** — reorganise dramatically. *Example:* "The reorg shook up the team structure."
53. **smooth out** — remove problems. *Example:* "We smoothed out the last few bugs."
54. **iron out** *(reinforce)* — resolve small issues. *Example:* "Let's iron out the details." 
55. **fizzle out** — fade and fail. *Example:* "The initiative fizzled out without support."
56. **come off** — succeed. *Example:* "The demo came off well."
57. **pull through** — recover or survive. *Example:* "The project pulled through despite delays."
58. **weather** *(as in ride out)* — see idioms; here: **ride out** — survive a hard period. *Example:* "We rode out the tough quarter."
59. **stir up** — provoke or cause. *Example:* "The change stirred up some concern."
60. **shrug off** — dismiss or ignore. *Example:* "She shrugged off the criticism and moved on."

## People, ideas & communication

61. **kick around** — discuss ideas casually. *Example:* "Let's kick around a few options."
62. **toss around** — discuss ideas informally. *Example:* "We tossed around some names for the project."
63. **bounce back** *(reinforce)* — recover. *Example:* "The team bounced back after the setback."
64. **rope in** — persuade someone to join. *Example:* "We roped in a designer to help."
65. **pitch in** — help with a task. *Example:* "Everyone pitched in to hit the deadline."
66. **lean on** — rely on for support. *Example:* "I leaned on my mentor for advice."
67. **rally around** — unite to support. *Example:* "The team rallied around the new lead."
68. **stand by** — be ready; support. *Example:* "I'll stand by in case the demo breaks."
69. **stand in** — substitute for someone. *Example:* "I'll stand in for Anna at the review."
70. **level with** — be honest with. *Example:* "Let me level with you about the timeline."
71. **sound out** — test someone's opinion. *Example:* "I sounded out the team before proposing it."
72. **win back** — regain. *Example:* "We're trying to win back lost customers."
73. **come around** — change your mind to agree. *Example:* "She came around to the idea after seeing the data."
74. **talk down** — make something seem less important. *Example:* "Don't talk down your own contribution."
75. **talk up** — praise or promote. *Example:* "He talked up the results to leadership."
76. **play down** — make seem less important. *Example:* "They played down the risk."
77. **play up** — emphasise. *Example:* "The report plays up the cost savings."
78. **gloss over** — treat lightly, skip detail. *Example:* "The summary glossed over the limitations."
79. **put down** — record; also criticise. *Example:* "Put me down for the Tuesday slot."
80. **dash off** — write or send quickly. *Example:* "I'll dash off a quick note."

## Money, growth & opportunity

81. **cash in on** — profit from an opportunity. *Example:* "We cashed in on the seasonal demand."
82. **rake in** — earn a lot. *Example:* "The new product is raking in revenue."
83. **pull in** — attract. *Example:* "The campaign pulled in thousands of sign-ups."
84. **snap up** — buy or take quickly. *Example:* "Customers snapped up the offer."
85. **drum up** — generate (interest or support). *Example:* "We drummed up support for the pilot."
86. **cough up** — pay reluctantly. *Example:* "The client finally coughed up the fee."
87. **jack up** — raise sharply (prices). *Example:* "The vendor jacked up their rates."
88. **chalk up** — record as an achievement. *Example:* "We chalked up another strong quarter."
89. **edge out** — narrowly beat. *Example:* "Our tool edged out the competitor."
90. **bank on** — depend on with confidence. *Example:* "Don't bank on the data being clean."
91. **hinge on** — depend critically on. *Example:* "The launch hinges on the data fix."
92. **double down on** — commit more strongly. *Example:* "We doubled down on automation."
93. **tap into** — access or use. *Example:* "We tapped into a new data source."
94. **capitalise on** *(variant of cash in)* — take full advantage of. *Example:* "Let's capitalise on the early success."
95. **branch into** — expand into a new area. *Example:* "We're branching into forecasting." *(distinct from 'branch out')*

## Learning & starting

96. **brush up on** — improve a rusty skill. *Example:* "I'm brushing up on SQL."
97. **read up on** — study a subject. *Example:* "I read up on the new framework."
98. **pick up** — learn; resume; improve. *Example:* "I picked up the tool quickly." / "Sales picked up."
99. **take in** — understand or absorb. *Example:* "That's a lot to take in."
100. **take away** — learn or conclude. *Example:* "My main takeaway is that onboarding matters." *(noun: takeaway)*
101. **sign up** — register. *Example:* "I signed up for the training."
102. **sign on** — join or commit. *Example:* "Two more teams signed on to the pilot."
103. **start up** — begin operating. *Example:* "We started up the new service."
104. **start off** — begin. *Example:* "Let's start off with the summary."
105. **opt out** — choose not to take part. *Example:* "Users can opt out of tracking."
106. **phase in** — introduce gradually. *Example:* "We'll phase in the new process."

## Assessing & finishing

107. **size up** — assess. *Example:* "Let me size up the workload first."
108. **scope out** — investigate or assess. *Example:* "We scoped out the requirements."
109. **weigh up** — assess and compare. *Example:* "Let's weigh up the options."
110. **stack up** — compare; accumulate. *Example:* "How does our model stack up against the baseline?"
111. **flick through** — look quickly. *Example:* "I flicked through the report on the train."
112. **pore over** — study closely. *Example:* "We pored over the results for hours."
113. **close down** — shut a service or business. *Example:* "We closed down the old system."
114. **wind up** — conclude (a meeting/company). *Example:* "Let's wind up here." *(Note: in UK slang 'wind up' can mean 'annoy' — context decides.)*
115. **round off** *(reinforce)* — finish nicely. *Example:* "Let's round off with next steps."
116. **give out** — distribute; also stop working. *Example:* "The server gave out under load."
117. **give back** — return; contribute. *Example:* "The tool gives back hours to the team."
118. **usher in** — introduce a new era. *Example:* "The change ushers in faster reporting."
119. **trade off** — balance one thing against another. *Example:* "We traded off speed for accuracy."
120. **tone down** — make less strong. *Example:* "Let's tone down the claims in the report."
