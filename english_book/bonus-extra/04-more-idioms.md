# Bonus 4: 110 More Professional Idioms

These idioms are **additional** to the 200 in Part 11 (Chapters 40–44) — none are repeated here. Learn a few at a time and use them lightly.

**Format:** the idiom — simple meaning. *Example:* a workplace sentence. **[Casual]** = informal. **[Careful]** = may confuse non-native speakers, so use plainly with mixed audiences.

---

## Effort, quality & getting things done

1. **the whole nine yards** — everything, the full amount. *Example:* "We tested the whole nine yards." **[Careful]**
2. **go the whole hog** — do something fully. *Example:* "Let's go the whole hog and automate it all." **[Casual]**
3. **cut the mustard** — meet the required standard. *Example:* "The old tool no longer cuts the mustard." **[Careful]**
4. **bring to the table** — offer or contribute. *Example:* "What does this vendor bring to the table?"
5. **on the ball** — alert and competent. *Example:* "She's really on the ball with details."
6. **in full swing** — at peak activity. *Example:* "The project is in full swing."
7. **off the top of my head** — from memory, without checking. *Example:* "Off the top of my head, it's about 12%."
8. **get your ducks in a row** — get organised. *Example:* "Let's get our ducks in a row before the review." **[Careful]**
9. **cover a lot of ground** — deal with a lot. *Example:* "We covered a lot of ground today."
10. **break new ground** — do something innovative. *Example:* "This research breaks new ground."
11. **crunch the numbers** — do the calculations. *Example:* "Let me crunch the numbers first."
12. **the proof is in the pudding** — results prove the value. *Example:* "The proof is in the pudding — churn dropped." **[Careful]**
13. **reinvent the wheel** — waste effort redoing something. *Example:* "Let's not reinvent the wheel."
14. **cut and dried** — clear and settled. *Example:* "The decision isn't cut and dried." **[Careful]**
15. **hit the books** — study hard. *Example:* "I hit the books to learn the new framework." **[Casual]**
16. **burn the candle at both ends** — overwork/exhaust yourself. *Example:* "Don't burn the candle at both ends." **[Careful]**

## Using these idioms wisely

- Idioms make you sound fluent, but with non-native speakers (very common in Europe) and in direct cultures (Netherlands, Germany, Nordics), use them lightly and choose the clear ones. Plain English is never wrong.
- Get the wording exact — a half-remembered idiom sounds worse than plain words.
- Learn to *understand* even the ones you'd never say, so you're never lost when a colleague uses one.

**Common Indian-English note:** avoid mixing idioms, and remember Indian-English phrases like "do the needful" and "prepone" are not international idioms (see Chapter 14).
