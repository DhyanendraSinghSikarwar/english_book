# Bonus 3: 120 More Vocabulary Words

Part 3 (Chapter 12) upgraded common *simple* words. This file adds **120 professional words** of a different kind: strong adjectives, useful nouns, and formal connectors that make your writing precise and polished. None repeat the Chapter 12 list.

**Format:** the word — simple meaning. *Example:* a workplace sentence.

---

## A. Professional adjectives (describe things precisely) — 50

1. **robust** — strong and reliable. *Example:* "We need a robust pipeline that won't break."
2. **comprehensive** — complete, covering everything. *Example:* "A comprehensive review of the data."
3. **nuanced** — with fine, subtle detail. *Example:* "It's a nuanced problem, not black and white."
4. **pragmatic** — practical, sensible. *Example:* "Let's take a pragmatic approach."
5. **scalable** — able to grow without breaking. *Example:* "Is this solution scalable?"
6. **granular** — very detailed. *Example:* "We need more granular data."
7. **holistic** — looking at the whole, not just parts. *Example:* "A holistic view of the customer."
8. **incremental** — in small steps. *Example:* "We made incremental improvements."
9. **iterative** — repeated in cycles to improve. *Example:* "It's an iterative process."
10. **cohesive** — well connected as a whole. *Example:* "A cohesive strategy."
11. **coherent** — clear and logical. *Example:* "A coherent argument."
12. **viable** — workable, able to succeed. *Example:* "Is this a viable option?"
13. **feasible** — possible to do. *Example:* "The timeline is feasible."
14. **tangible** — real and measurable. *Example:* "We need tangible results."
15. **intangible** — real but not measurable. *Example:* "Trust is an intangible benefit."
16. **salient** — most noticeable or important. *Example:* "The salient point is cost."
17. **pivotal** — very important, decisive. *Example:* "A pivotal moment for the project."
18. **marginal** — small, minor. *Example:* "Only a marginal improvement."
19. **negligible** — so small it barely matters. *Example:* "The effect was negligible."
20. **substantial** — large, significant. *Example:* "A substantial cost saving."
21. **considerable** — fairly large. *Example:* "Considerable effort went into this."
22. **prudent** — careful and wise. *Example:* "A prudent decision."
23. **judicious** — showing good judgment. *Example:* "Judicious use of resources."
24. **meticulous** — very careful about detail. *Example:* "Meticulous data cleaning."
25. **diligent** — hard-working and careful. *Example:* "A diligent analyst."
26. **rigorous** — thorough and precise. *Example:* "A rigorous test process."
27. **systematic** — done in an organised way. *Example:* "A systematic review."
28. **methodical** — orderly, step by step. *Example:* "A methodical approach."
29. **agile** — able to move and adapt quickly. *Example:* "An agile team."
30. **resilient** — able to recover from setbacks. *Example:* "A resilient system."
31. **versatile** — able to do many things. *Example:* "A versatile tool."
32. **adept** — highly skilled. *Example:* "She's adept at forecasting."
33. **proficient** — competent and skilled. *Example:* "Proficient in Python."
34. **seasoned** — very experienced. *Example:* "A seasoned data scientist."
35. **astute** — sharp and clever in judgment. *Example:* "An astute observation."
36. **succinct** — short and clear. *Example:* "A succinct summary."
37. **candid** — honest and direct. *Example:* "Thanks for the candid feedback."
38. **transparent** — open and clear. *Example:* "A transparent process."
39. **accountable** — responsible and answerable. *Example:* "Each owner is accountable."
40. **proactive** — acting ahead of problems. *Example:* "A proactive approach to risk."
41. **strategic** — about the long-term plan. *Example:* "A strategic priority."
42. **tactical** — about short-term actions. *Example:* "A tactical fix for now."
43. **actionable** — able to be acted on. *Example:* "Give me actionable insights."
44. **quantifiable** — able to be measured. *Example:* "A quantifiable benefit."
45. **ambiguous** — unclear, open to interpretation. *Example:* "The requirement is ambiguous."
46. **redundant** — unnecessary or repeated. *Example:* "This step is redundant."
47. **obsolete** — out of date, no longer used. *Example:* "The old system is obsolete."
48. **contingent** — depending on something else. *Example:* "The plan is contingent on approval."

51. **preliminary** — early, not final. *Example:* "Preliminary results look promising."

## D. Formal connectors & linking phrases — 20

Use these to link ideas smoothly in reports and emails.

101. **therefore** — for that reason. *Example:* "The test failed; therefore, we paused."
102. **consequently** — as a result. *Example:* "Costs rose; consequently, we cut scope."
103. **nevertheless** — despite that. *Example:* "It's late; nevertheless, quality is high."
104. **notwithstanding** — in spite of. *Example:* "Notwithstanding the delay, we succeeded."
105. **albeit** — although. *Example:* "A good result, albeit on small data."
106. **whereas** — in contrast. *Example:* "A grew, whereas B fell."
107. **hence** — for this reason. *Example:* "The data is noisy; hence the caution."
108. **thereby** — as a result of that. *Example:* "We automated it, thereby saving time."
109. **insofar as** — to the extent that. *Example:* "It helps insofar as the data is clean."
110. **moreover** — in addition. *Example:* "It's faster; moreover, it's cheaper."
111. **furthermore** — additionally. *Example:* "Furthermore, the results are consistent."
112. **conversely** — the opposite way. *Example:* "Conversely, low usage predicts churn."
113. **accordingly** — in a way that fits. *Example:* "We planned accordingly."
114. **in light of** — considering. *Example:* "In light of the results, we'll proceed."
115. **with respect to** — regarding. *Example:* "With respect to cost, we're on budget."
116. **going forward** — from now on. *Example:* "Going forward, we'll test earlier."
117. **by and large** — mostly. *Example:* "By and large, the data is clean."
118. **to that end** — for that purpose. *Example:* "To that end, we built a checklist."
119. **on balance** — considering everything. *Example:* "On balance, it's the right call."
120. **as such** — for that reason / in that capacity. *Example:* "It's untested; as such, treat it with care."

---

**Total: 120 vocabulary words.** Use adjectives and nouns to be precise, and connectors to make your writing flow. As always: upgrade for formal writing and senior audiences, but keep it simple and warm in chat and everyday talk (see Chapter 12). Clarity always beats showing off.
