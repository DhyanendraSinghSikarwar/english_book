# Chapter 35: Job Interviews & Career Conversations

Interviews and career conversations are high-stakes moments where the right language really matters. This chapter gives you scripts and structures for data-science interviews, the "tell me about yourself" answer, salary negotiation, asking for promotion, giving notice, and performance reviews — all tuned for a European context.

---

## 1. Describing projects with the STAR method

In interviews, you'll be asked "Tell me about a time when..." The best way to answer is the **STAR** structure:

- **S**ituation — the context.
- **T**ask — what you needed to do.
- **A**ction — what you actually did (the biggest part).
- **R**esult — the outcome, ideally with numbers.

**Worked example [DS] — "Tell me about a challenging project":**
> **Situation:** "At my last company, customer churn had jumped 15% in one quarter, and no one knew why.
> **Task:** "I was asked to find the cause and build a way to predict which customers would leave.
> **Action:** "I analysed a year of customer data, cleaned it, and tested several models. I found that most churn happened in the first month, linked to onboarding. I built a model to flag at-risk customers and worked with the product team to act on it.
> **Result:** "The model predicted churn with about 85% accuracy, and the onboarding changes we made reduced first-month churn by 20% — worth roughly €1 million a year."

**Worked example [DS] — "Tell me about a time you failed":**
> **Situation:** "I once spent two weeks building a complex model for demand forecasting.
> **Task:** "The goal was to beat our existing simple method.
> **Action:** "Despite a lot of tuning, my model barely beat the baseline. I made the call to stop and be honest with the team rather than keep polishing it.
> **Result:** "We kept the simpler method, and I learned to test a quick baseline first before investing in complexity. Now I always start simple — it's saved a lot of time since."

**Tips:**
- Spend most time on **Action** (what *you* did) and always end with a **Result**.
- Use "I" for your own contribution, but acknowledge the team ("I worked with...").
- Have three or four STAR stories ready before any interview.

## 2. "Tell me about yourself" — a 90-second script

This is usually the first question. Don't tell your life story. Use this structure: **present → past → future.**

> "I'm a data scientist with about four years' experience, currently focused on customer retention and churn modelling. [present]
>
> I started in analytics, then moved into machine learning, where I've built models that turn customer data into real business decisions — my proudest was a churn model that helped cut early churn by 20%. [past]
>
> I've recently moved to [City], and I'm looking for a role where I can keep solving business problems with data, ideally in a product-focused team like yours. [future]"

**Tips:**
- Keep it to 60–90 seconds.
- Focus on professional, relevant points — not hobbies or personal history.
- End by connecting to *this* role/company.

## 3. Salary negotiation

Talking about money feels uncomfortable, but in Europe it's a normal, expected part of hiring. Come prepared with research and stay calm.

**Researching first:**
- "Based on my research, the market range for this role in [City] is between €X and €Y."
- Use sites like Glassdoor, Levels.fyi, and local salary surveys.

**Stating your expectation:**
- "Given my experience and the market range, I'm looking for something in the region of €X."
- "My expectation is around €X, but I'm open to discussing the full package."

**Negotiating an offer:**
- "Thank you for the offer — I'm excited about the role. Based on my research and experience, I was hoping for closer to €X. Is there flexibility?"
- "I'd be ready to accept at €X. Could we get there?"
- "Beyond base salary, could we look at [bonus / more leave / learning budget]?"

**If they can't move on salary:**
- "I understand. Could we revisit compensation at my six-month review?"
- "Are there other parts of the package that are flexible — such as annual leave or remote days?"

**Tips:**
- Never give the first number if you can avoid it; ask their range first.
- Negotiate the whole package: salary, bonus, leave, remote work, learning budget, relocation.
- Stay warm and collaborative, not aggressive. "Is there flexibility?" works well.
- It's normal to ask for time: "Could I have a day or two to consider the offer?"

## 4. Asking for a promotion or pay rise (in your current job)

Do this in a one-on-one, with evidence, calmly.

- "I'd like to talk about my growth. Over the past year I've [list two or three achievements with impact]. I feel ready for [the next level / more responsibility]. What would it take to get there?"
- "Based on my contributions and the market, I'd like to discuss a pay rise. Can we explore that?"
- "I've taken on [X and Y] beyond my original role. I'd like my title and pay to reflect that."

**Tips:**
- Bring specific achievements with numbers.
- Frame it as "what would it take?" — this invites a path, not a yes/no.
- Give your manager time; decisions often need approval up the chain.

## 5. Giving notice / resigning

Keep it professional and positive — you may need these people again.

- "I wanted to let you know I've decided to move on. I've accepted a new role, and my last day would be [date], respecting my notice period."
- "This wasn't an easy decision — I've really valued my time here. I want to make the handover as smooth as possible."
- "I'm grateful for everything I've learned. I'll make sure my projects are well documented before I go."

**Tips:**
- Tell your manager first, in person or by call, before anyone else.
- Know your notice period (often one to three months in Europe).
- Don't badmouth the company. Leave on good terms.
- Offer to help with the handover.

## 6. Exit conversations / exit interviews

- "My reasons for leaving are mostly about growth — I was looking for [X]."
- "I've had a positive experience overall. One area I'd suggest improving is [constructive point]."
- Keep feedback honest but constructive; don't burn bridges.

## 7. Performance review language (self-appraisal)

In reviews, you often write a self-appraisal. Use power verbs (Chapters 7–11) and show impact.

**Self-appraisal sentence stems:**
- "This year, I led / owned / delivered..."
- "I improved [X] by [number], resulting in..."
- "A key achievement was..."
- "I took the initiative to..."
- "I grew my skills in [area] by..."
- "An area I'd like to develop further is..."
- "My goals for next year are..."

**Example:**
> "This year I owned the churn model from concept to production. It now predicts at-risk customers with 85% accuracy and has helped reduce early churn by 20%, worth around €1M annually. I also mentored two junior analysts. An area I want to develop is stakeholder communication with senior leadership, and I'd like to lead a cross-team project next year."

**Tips:**
- Be specific and quantify impact.
- Balance achievements with one or two honest growth areas — European reviews value self-awareness.
- Don't be falsely modest (undersells you) or arrogant (overclaims). Aim for confident and factual.

## 8. Receiving feedback in a review

- "Thank you — that's helpful. Could you give me an example so I can work on it?"
- "I appreciate the honest feedback. Here's how I'll approach that."
- "That's fair. I've noticed it too, and here's my plan."

Take feedback graciously, even if it stings. In direct cultures (NL, Germany) it may be blunt — remember it's about the work, not you.

## Common Indian-English pitfalls in career conversations

- **"I am having 4 years experience."** → "I have four years of experience."
- **"My current CTC is..."** → "My current salary/package is..." ("CTC" — cost to company — is an Indian term; Europeans say "salary" or "total package.")
- **"I want salary hike."** → "I'd like to discuss a pay rise."
- **"Kindly consider my candidature."** → "I'd be grateful to be considered."
- **"I passed out in 2020."** → "I graduated in 2020."
- **"Myself [Name], I am from..."** → "I'm [Name], a data scientist..."
- **"I did my graduation in..."** → "I did my degree in..." / "I studied..."
- **"Notice period is 3 months from my side."** → "My notice period is three months."
