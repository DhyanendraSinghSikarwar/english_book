# Chapter 26: Email Mastery

Email is where much of your professional impression is formed. A clear, well-judged email builds trust; a confusing or over-formal one costs you credibility. This chapter covers the anatomy of a good email, openers for every relationship, 20 full templates, tone calibration, and European email norms.

---

## 1. The anatomy of a good email

**Subject line — get it opened and understood:**
- Be specific: "Churn model — approval needed by Fri" beats "Update."
- Signal the action: "[Action needed]", "[FYI]", "[Decision]".
- Keep it short — it may be read on a phone.

Good subject lines:
- "Approval needed: pilot budget (by 10 July)"
- "Notes & actions — data sync, 8 July"
- "Quick question on the sales data"
- "Heads up: pipeline delay and plan"

**Structure (most emails):**
1. Greeting.
2. One-line purpose (why you're writing).
3. The detail (short paragraphs or bullets).
4. The clear ask (what you want, by when).
5. Sign-off.

**Golden rule for Europe:** shorter is better. Get to the point in the first two lines. Busy European colleagues appreciate brevity far more than long, formal build-ups.

## 2. Openers by relationship

**Neutral / first contact:**
- "I hope you're well."
- "I'm writing to..."
- "I hope this finds you well." (a bit formal, fine for external contacts)

**Following up:**
- "Further to our conversation earlier,..."
- "As discussed,..."
- "Following up on my last email,..."

**Warm / colleague you know:**
- "Hope your week's going well."
- "Thanks for your help yesterday."
- "Quick one for you —"

**Cold / someone you don't know:**
- "We haven't met, but I lead the data science work on retention."
- "[Name] suggested I get in touch."

**Note:** In Europe you can often skip the pleasantries entirely for internal emails and go straight to the point. "Hi Anna, quick question —" is perfectly polite.

## 3. Sign-offs

- **Neutral/safe:** "Best regards," / "Kind regards," / "Best,"
- **Warm:** "Thanks," / "Cheers," (informal, common in UK/NL) / "Many thanks,"
- **Formal:** "Yours sincerely," (when you know the name) / "Yours faithfully," (British, when you don't — rare now)
- **Avoid:** "Thanking you," / "Yours truly," / "Warm regards" repeated everywhere — these sound dated or over-formal.

## 4. Twenty full email templates

### Template 1 — Making a request
> **Subject:** Access request: payments table
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Could you grant me read access to the payments table? I need it for the churn model — it should be the last thing blocking the data work.
>
> If it's easier to hop on a quick call, let me know.
>
> Thanks,
> [You]

### Template 2 — Following up (no reply yet)
> **Subject:** Following up: access request
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Just following up on the access request below — no rush, but it's blocking the next step, so I wanted to keep it on your radar.
>
> Thanks,
> [You]

### Template 3 — Escalating an issue
> **Subject:** Escalating: vendor unresponsive, blocking the launch
>
> Hi [Manager],
>
> I want to flag an issue early. The vendor hasn't responded in four days, and it's now blocking the launch planned for the 22nd.
>
> I've tried email and their support line. Could you help push on your side, or advise how you'd like to proceed?
>
> Thanks,
> [You]

### Template 4 — Apology for a mistake
> **Subject:** Correction to yesterday's figures
>
> Hi all,
>
> Apologies — I found an error in yesterday's report. The churn figure should read 12%, not 21%. I've attached the corrected version.
>
> The cause was a filter I've now fixed, and I've added a check to catch it in future.
>
> Sorry for any confusion.
> [You]

### Template 5 — Saying no politely
> **Subject:** Re: extra dashboard request
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Thanks for thinking of me. I'd like to help, but I'm at capacity with the churn pilot this sprint, so I can't take this on without risking that deadline.
>
> Could it wait until the following sprint, or shall we ask [Name] if they have bandwidth?
>
> Best,
> [You]

### Template 6 — Delivering bad news
> **Subject:** Pipeline delay — new timeline inside
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I want to be upfront: the data pipeline will be a few days late because of a data quality issue we found in testing.
>
> Here's the plan: we fix it by Wednesday, and the model work resumes Thursday, putting us back on track for the pilot.
>
> Happy to talk it through if useful.
>
> Best,
> [You]

### Template 7 — Introducing yourself to a new team

> [You]

>
> Thanks for clarifying,
> [You]

>

>
> Headline: most churn happens in the first month, and onboarding is the main driver.

>
> Best,
> [You]

> Thanks,
> [You]

## 5. Tone calibration — the same email in three tones

**Casual (teammate):**
> "Hey — can you send the sales file when you get a sec? Thanks!"

**Neutral-professional (most emails):**
> "Hi Anna, could you send over the sales file when you have a moment? Thanks."

**Formal (client / senior / external):**
> "Dear Ms. Berg, I would be grateful if you could share the sales file at your earliest convenience. Many thanks in advance."

**Which to use:** Neutral-professional works for 90% of work email. Use casual with close teammates, and formal only for external clients, very senior people, or sensitive matters.

## 6. European email norms (important shifts)

- **Shorter is better.** Two or three sentences is often plenty. Long emails may go unread.
- **Get to the point fast.** State your purpose in line one or two. Don't bury the ask at the bottom.
- **Direct asks are polite.** "Could you send X by Friday?" is not rude — it's clear and helpful.
- **Less "sir/madam."** Use first names. "Hi Anna" is standard even to senior people.
- **Drop the flowery closings.** "Best regards" or "Thanks" beats "Thanking you and awaiting your kind response."
- **Don't over-apologise.** One "apologies for the delay" is enough.
- **Reply times.** A same-day or next-day reply is normal. If you need longer, a one-line "I'll get back to you by Thursday" is appreciated.
- **Respect off-hours.** Avoid expecting replies in the evening or at weekends. (More in Chapter 33.)

## Common Indian-English pitfalls in email

- **"Please do the needful."** → "Please take care of this." / "Please proceed."
- **"Please revert back at the earliest."** → "Please get back to me as soon as you can."
- **"Kindly find the attached herewith."** → "Please find the attachment." / "I've attached..."
- **"This is to inform you that..."** → "I'm writing to let you know..." / just say it.
- **"Awaiting your revert."** → "Looking forward to your reply."
- **"Please confirm the same."** → "Please confirm."
- **"Thanking you"** → "Thank you." / "Thanks."
- **"Myself [Name], working as data scientist."** → "I'm [Name], a data scientist on the retention team."

---

**Chapter 26 summary.** Great work email is short, clear, and warm: a specific subject, a one-line purpose, the detail, and a clear ask. Use the neutral-professional tone by default, adapt for close teammates or formal clients, and follow European norms — brief, direct, first-name, no flowery closings. Keep these 20 templates handy and adapt them to your situation.
