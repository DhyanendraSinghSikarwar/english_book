# Chapter 14: Words to STOP Using (Indian English → International English)

Indian English is a real and rich form of English. There is nothing wrong with it. But some words and phrases that are normal in India are not used — or mean something different — in European and international workplaces. Using them can confuse your colleagues or make you sound less polished than you are.

This chapter lists the most common ones, with the international version and why it matters. Learning these gives you one of the fastest upgrades to your professional image.

**Format:** Indian-English phrase → International version | Why it matters.

---

## The top offenders (learn these first)

**"do the needful"** → "please take the necessary action" / "please proceed" / "please handle this"
*Why:* This phrase is almost unknown outside South Asia. To a European colleague it sounds strange and old-fashioned. Just say what you actually want done.

**"revert"** (meaning "reply") → "get back to me" / "reply" / "respond"
*Why:* In international English, "revert" means to go back to a previous state (like "revert the code"). "Please revert" makes people think you want them to undo something, not answer you.

**"prepone"** → "bring forward" / "move up" / "reschedule earlier"
*Why:* "Prepone" is an Indian coinage. It's logical, but nobody outside India uses it. Say "Can we bring the meeting forward to Tuesday?"

**"kindly"** (overused) → "please" (or drop it)
*Why:* "Kindly do this" sounds overly formal and slightly old-fashioned in Europe. "Please" is warmer and more modern. Often you can drop it entirely: "Could you send the file?" is perfect.

**"the same"** ("please confirm the same") → "please confirm it" / "please confirm this"
*Why:* "Kindly confirm the same" is very common in Indian business writing but sounds robotic internationally. Use a normal pronoun: "Please confirm this."

**"revert back"** → "reply" / "get back to me"
*Why:* Double problem: "revert" is wrong (see above), and "back" is redundant. Just "get back to me."

## Time and date phrases

**"today morning"** → "this morning"
**"today evening"** → "this evening"
**"yesterday night"** → "last night"
**"tomorrow morning"** → "tomorrow morning" (this one is correct — keep it)
*Why:* "Today morning" and "yesterday night" are not standard. Use "this morning" and "last night."

**"since 3 years"** → "for 3 years"
*Why:* Use "for" with a length of time, and "since" with a starting point. "I've worked here for three years" or "since 2021."

**"by tomorrow itself"** → "by tomorrow" / "by tomorrow at the latest"
*Why:* "Itself" as emphasis ("today itself," "here itself") is Indian English. Drop it or use "at the latest."

## Office and status phrases

**"out of station"** → "out of office" / "away" / "travelling"
*Why:* "Out of station" is a railway-era phrase unknown abroad. Say "I'll be out of office next week."

**"passed out"** (graduated) → "graduated"
*Why:* Internationally, "passed out" means fainted! "I passed out of college in 2018" sounds alarming. Say "I graduated in 2018."

**"on leave" / "took leave"** → "on holiday" (UK) / "on vacation" (US) / "taking annual leave"
*Why:* "On leave" is understood in the UK but "annual leave" or "holiday" is more common. In Europe, "I'm on holiday next week" is normal.

**"cabin"** (office room) → "office" / "meeting room"
*Why:* A "cabin" abroad is a small hut or a plane section. Say "Can we talk in your office?"

**"intimate"** (meaning inform) → "inform" / "let (someone) know" / "notify"
*Why:* "Please intimate me" means something very different internationally! "Intimate" relates to closeness/privacy. Say "Please let me know" or "Please inform me."

**"needful"** → (see "do the needful" above) "what's needed"

## Grammar habits to drop

**"discuss about"** → "discuss"
*Why:* "Discuss" already includes "about." Say "Let's discuss the plan," not "discuss about the plan."

**"return back"** → "return"
*Why:* "Return" means to go back. "Back" is redundant. Same with "revert back," "repeat again," "reply back."

**"cope up with"** → "cope with"
*Why:* No "up." Say "I can cope with the workload."

**"myself Rahul"** → "I'm Rahul" / "My name is Rahul"
*Why:* "Myself Rahul" is a common introduction in India but is grammatically off. Say "Hi, I'm Rahul."

**"good name"** ("What is your good name?") → "What's your name?" / "May I ask your name?"
*Why:* "Good name" is a polite Hindi/Urdu translation (shubh naam). It sounds unusual in English. Just "your name."

**"only" (as emphasis)** ("I told you only") → drop it or rephrase ("That's exactly what I told you")
*Why:* Using "only" for emphasis ("He is like that only") is Indian English. Rephrase for clarity.

**"na?" / "no?" / "isn't it?" (as a tag on everything)** → use the correct tag or drop it
*Why:* "You sent it, no?" or "It's ready, isn't it?" overused sounds off. Use varied, correct tags: "You sent it, didn't you?" — or just ask directly: "Did you send it?"

**"informations" / "feedbacks" / "trainings"** → "information" / "feedback" / "training sessions"
*Why:* These are uncountable nouns — no plural "s." Say "some information," "useful feedback," "three training sessions."

**"a doubt"** (meaning a question) → "a question" / "a query"
*Why:* "I have a doubt" internationally suggests you distrust something. In class or meetings, say "I have a question."

**"updation" / "upgradation"** → "update" / "upgrade"
*Why:* These noun forms don't exist in standard English. Use "update" and "upgrade" as nouns too.

**"revert" / "advise" as noun** ("awaiting your revert / advise") → "reply" / "advice"
*Why:* "Advise" is a verb; "advice" is the noun. "Await your reply," not "await your revert."

## Politeness and culture

**"sir" / "madam" (constant use)** → first names
*Why:* This is a big cultural shift. In most of Europe, you call your manager — even your CEO — by their first name. Constant "sir/madam" sounds overly formal and creates distance. **How to switch:** on day one, listen to how people address each other. If everyone uses first names, do the same. If unsure, ask: "Do you prefer I call you [first name]?" People will almost always say yes. Using "sir" repeatedly can even make senior Europeans uncomfortable.

**"please do the honours"** → "please go ahead" / "would you like to start?"
**"revert at the earliest"** → "get back to me as soon as you can" / "when you have a chance"
**"as per our discussion"** → "following our discussion" / "as we discussed" (this one is acceptable but "as we discussed" is more natural)
**"prepone the call"** → "move the call earlier"
**"do one thing"** ("Do one thing, send me the file") → just give the instruction: "Could you send me the file?"
*Why:* "Do one thing" is a direct translation of a Hindi phrase. Skip it and state the request.

**"same to same"** → "identical" / "exactly the same"
**"prepone/postpone"** — "postpone" is correct and international; only "prepone" is the problem.
**"good name," "good self"** ("your good self") → "you" / "your name"
*Why:* "Your good self" is very formal old English, still common in Indian letters. Modern writing just says "you."

**"anyways"** → "anyway"
*Why:* "Anyways" is casual/nonstandard. Use "anyway."

**"backside"** ("park at the backside") → "at the back" / "behind the building"
*Why:* "Backside" means buttocks in British/international English! Say "at the back" or "the rear."

**"prepone," "eve-teasing," "hotel" (restaurant), "tempo," "cent per cent"** → move up, harassment, restaurant, small truck, 100 percent
*Why:* These are region-specific. Use the international word so everyone understands.
