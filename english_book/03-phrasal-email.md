# Chapter 3: Email & Communication

Written communication is where small language mistakes stand out the most. This chapter gives you the phrasal verbs that make your emails and chat messages clear, polite, and professional. Many of these fix common Indian-English habits like "revert back" and "do the needful."

---

#### reach out
- **Meaning:** to contact someone.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll reach out to the vendor today.
  - Feel free to reach out anytime.
  - She reached out to the support team.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** We need the API docs.
  - **You:** I'll reach out to their engineering team and ask.
- **Formal alternative:** contact / get in touch
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Reach out to" needs "to." Don't say "reach out the team"; say "reach out to the team."

#### get back to
- **Meaning:** to reply to someone later.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll get back to you by tomorrow.
  - Can you get back to me on this?
  - She hasn't got back to me yet.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Client:** When will the report be ready?
  - **You:** Let me check and get back to you within the hour.
- **Formal alternative:** reply / respond
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Use "get back to you," not "revert back to you." "Revert" alone means "return to a previous state," so "revert back" is wrong. Prefer "get back to you" or "reply."

#### loop in
- **Meaning:** to add someone to a conversation so they know what's happening.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me loop in the design team.
  - I'll loop you in on the thread.
  - Please loop in finance here.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** This affects security too.
  - **Anna:** Good point, loop in the security lead.
- **Formal alternative:** include / involve
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Loop in" (add to a conversation) is different from "copy in" (add to email CC), though they overlap. "Loop in" is broader.

#### keep (someone) posted
- **Meaning:** to keep giving someone updates.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll keep you posted on progress.
  - Keep me posted if anything changes.
  - She kept us posted all week.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** How's the training going?
  - **You:** Running now. I'll keep you posted on the results.
- **Formal alternative:** keep informed / update
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "I will keep you updating." Correct is "keep you posted" or "keep you updated."

#### pass on
- **Meaning:** to give information or a message to someone else.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll pass on your feedback.
  - Can you pass this on to the team?
  - She passed on the message.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Client:** Please thank the whole team.
  - **You:** I will. I'll pass on your thanks to them.
- **Formal alternative:** relay / forward
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Pass on" (relay) vs "pass away" (die). Keep them separate; don't shorten "pass on the message" to just "pass on" without an object.

#### forward on
- **Meaning:** to send a received message to someone else.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll forward on the email.
  - Can you forward that on to me?
  - She forwarded the thread on.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Did you get the client's spec?
  - **Anna:** Yes, I'll forward it on to you now.
- **Formal alternative:** forward / send on
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Forward on" and "forward" mean the same; the "on" is optional. Don't write "forwarding the same" — say "forwarding it" or "forwarding the email."

#### chase up
- **Meaning:** to remind someone to do or send something.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll chase up the approval.
  - Can you chase up the vendor?
  - She's chasing up the invoice.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Any word on the access request?
  - **You:** Not yet, I'll chase it up with IT.
- **Formal alternative:** follow up on / remind
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Chase up" (remind politely) is normal and not rude in Europe. Don't over-apologise when you chase up; a simple reminder is fine.

#### hear back
- **Meaning:** to get a reply from someone.
- **Examples:**
  - I haven't heard back yet.
  - Did you hear back from the client?
  - I'll let you know when I hear back.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I emailed the vendor on Monday.
  - **Lead:** Have you heard back?
  - **You:** Not yet, I'll chase them up.
- **Formal alternative:** receive a reply
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Hear back from," not "hear back of." And don't say "I didn't get any revert." Say "I haven't heard back."

#### point out
- **Meaning:** to make someone notice something.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me point out one risk.
  - She pointed out an error in the code.
  - Thanks for pointing that out.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I want to point out that the data is a month old.
  - **Anna:** Good catch, we should refresh it.
- **Formal alternative:** highlight / note
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "point out about the issue." Just "point out the issue."

#### spell out
- **Meaning:** to explain something in very clear, simple detail.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me spell out the steps.
  - The email spells out the process.
  - Can you spell out what you need?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **New hire:** I'm not sure what's expected.
  - **You:** Let me spell it out: clean the data, train, then send me the metrics.
- **Formal alternative:** clarify / detail
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Spell out" (explain clearly) has nothing to do with spelling letters. It means to make fully clear.

#### flag up
- **Meaning:** to bring attention to a problem or point.
- **Examples:**
  - I want to flag up a concern.
  - She flagged up the risk early.
  - Let me flag this up to the team.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I want to flag up that the deadline may slip.
  - **Lead:** Thanks for flagging it early.
- **Formal alternative:** raise / highlight
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Flag up" or just "flag" both work. In email, "I want to flag an issue" is clean and professional.

#### note down
- **Meaning:** to write something so you remember it.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me note down the action points.
  - She noted down the decisions.
  - I'll note down your number.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** The deadline is the 15th.
  - **You:** Noted down. I'll add it to the tracker.
- **Formal alternative:** record / write down
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Note down" and "jot down" mean the same. Don't say "note it in a paper"; say "note it down" or "write it down."

#### jot down
- **Meaning:** to write something quickly.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me jot down your idea.
  - I jotted down a few notes.
  - Jot it down before you forget.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Give me a second to jot down the key numbers.
  - **Anna:** Sure, take your time.
- **Formal alternative:** note / record quickly
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Jot down" is informal but perfectly fine in the office. It implies quick, rough notes.

#### print out
- **Meaning:** to produce a paper copy.
- **Examples:**
  - Can you print out the report?
  - I printed out the agenda.
  - Let's print out the results.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Bring the numbers to the room.
  - **You:** I'll print out a copy for everyone.
- **Formal alternative:** produce a hard copy
- **Indian-English pitfall:** The noun is "printout" (one word); the verb is "print out" (two words). Don't say "take a printout of it" — say "print it out."

#### fill in / fill out
- **Meaning:** to complete a form with information.
- **Examples:**
  - Please fill in the form.
  - I filled out the survey.
  - Can you fill in your details?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **HR:** We need your onboarding form.
  - **You:** I'll fill it in and send it back today.
- **Formal alternative:** complete
- **Indian-English pitfall:** UK says "fill in," US says "fill out"; both are correct. Also "fill in" can mean to substitute for someone (see Chapter 5).

#### send over
- **Meaning:** to send something to someone.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll send over the file.
  - Can you send the data over?
  - She sent over the draft.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Client:** Do you have the report?
  - **You:** Yes, I'll send it over now.
- **Formal alternative:** send / forward
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Send over" is friendly and normal. Avoid "kindly send the same"; say "I'll send it over."

#### copy in
- **Meaning:** to add someone to the CC line of an email.
- **Examples:**
  - Please copy in my manager.
  - I've copied in the team.
  - Can you copy me in on that?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Should the lead see this?
  - **Anna:** Yes, copy her in.
- **Formal alternative:** CC / include on the email
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Say "copy me in," not "put me in CC" (though that's understood) or "mark me in the CC." "Copy me in" is the natural phrase.

#### respond to
- **Meaning:** to reply to a message or request.
- **Examples:**
  - Please respond to the client by noon.
  - I responded to all the comments.
  - She hasn't responded to my email.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Did you answer the reviewer?
  - **You:** Yes, I responded to every point.
- **Formal alternative:** reply to / answer
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Respond to," not "respond on" or "respond for." Also avoid "do the reply"; just "respond" or "reply."

#### refer to
- **Meaning:** to mention or direct attention to something.
- **Examples:**
  - Please refer to the attached file.
  - I referred to the earlier email.
  - As I referred to before.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The details are in the doc. Please refer to section 3.
  - **Anna:** Found it, thanks.
- **Formal alternative:** see / consult
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Refer to," not "refer." Don't say "please refer the attached." Say "please refer to the attached" or "please see the attached."

#### clarify
- **Meaning:** to make something clearer or ask for clearer information. (Not a phrasal verb, but a key email verb.)
- **Examples:**
  - Let me clarify the requirement.
  - Can you clarify the deadline?
  - I want to clarify one point.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Just to clarify, do you need the raw data or the summary?
  - **Client:** The summary, please.
- **Formal alternative:** confirm / make clear
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "please do the clarification" or "kindly clarify me." Say "please clarify" or "could you clarify this for me?"

#### follow up on
- **Meaning:** to take further action about something mentioned earlier.
- **Examples:**
  - I'm following up on my last email.
  - Can you follow up on the request?
  - Just following up on our chat.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You (email):** Hi, I'm following up on the access request from Monday. Any update?
  - **IT:** Approved this morning.
- **Formal alternative:** pursue / check on
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Following up on" (a topic) needs "on." Also avoid the redundant "gentle reminder" opening; "I'm following up on..." is enough.

#### check in
- **Meaning:** to make brief contact to see how things are going.
- **Examples:**
  - Just checking in on the project.
  - Let's check in tomorrow.
  - I'll check in with the team.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You (chat):** Checking in — how's the model training going?
  - **Anna:** Almost done, results by 4.
- **Formal alternative:** touch base / follow up
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Check in" (contact briefly) is different from "check in" at a hotel or "check into" a problem. Context makes it clear.

#### touch base
- **Meaning:** to make brief contact to align.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's touch base on Friday.
  - I'll touch base with the client.
  - We touched base before the demo.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Before the sprint ends, let's touch base on priorities.
  - **You:** Sure, 10 minutes tomorrow?
- **Formal alternative:** connect / align
- **Indian-English pitfall:** It's "touch base" (from baseball), not "touch base with the topic" — you touch base "with a person" or "on a topic."

#### get across
- **Meaning:** to make someone understand your message.
- **Examples:**
  - I need to get the point across.
  - She got the idea across clearly.
  - It's hard to get this across in email.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I don't think my email got the urgency across.
  - **Anna:** Then call them; it's clearer by voice.
- **Formal alternative:** communicate / convey
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Get across" needs an object between: "get the point across" or "get it across." Don't say "get across the point" (word order matters here).

#### fire off
- **Meaning:** to send a message quickly.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll fire off a quick email.
  - She fired off a reply.
  - Let me fire off a message to them.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Can you ask the vendor now?
  - **You:** Sure, I'll fire off an email right away.
- **Formal alternative:** send promptly
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Informal but common. Suggests speed, not anger. Fine for quick internal notes, not formal letters.

#### drop (someone) a line
- **Meaning:** to send someone a short message.
- **Examples:**
  - Drop me a line when you're free.
  - I'll drop her a line about it.
  - Just dropping you a line to say thanks.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** If you need anything, drop me a line.
  - **New hire:** Thank you, I will.
- **Formal alternative:** send a message / write
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Idiomatic and friendly. Don't take it literally; it just means "send a short message."

#### run (something) by
- **Meaning:** to show something to someone for their opinion before acting.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me run this by my manager.
  - Can I run the plan by you?
  - She ran the draft by legal.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Before I send it to the client, can I run the email by you?
  - **Lead:** Sure, share it.
- **Formal alternative:** review with / seek approval from
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Run by" (get an opinion) vs "run through" (review the content). "Run by" implies you want their view or sign-off.

#### type up
- **Meaning:** to write notes into a clean, finished document.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll type up the minutes.
  - She typed up the notes after the call.
  - Can you type up the summary?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Who's taking notes?
  - **You:** I am. I'll type them up and share after the meeting.
- **Formal alternative:** write up / document
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Type up" implies turning rough notes into a clean version. Don't confuse with "write up" (which can be from scratch).

#### write up
- **Meaning:** to write a full report or document about something.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll write up the findings.
  - She wrote up the incident.
  - Let's write up the results.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** The experiment is done.
  - **You:** I'll write up the results and share the doc.
- **Formal alternative:** document / report
- **Indian-English pitfall:** The noun is "write-up" (hyphenated). Verb is "write up" (two words).

#### bounce back
- **Meaning:** (of email) to be returned undelivered; also to recover.
- **Examples:**
  - The email bounced back.
  - Her message bounced back twice.
  - The address must be wrong; it keeps bouncing back.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Did the client get the report?
  - **Anna:** No, the email bounced back. The address is wrong.
- **Formal alternative:** be returned undelivered
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Bounce back" for undelivered email is standard. Don't say "the mail is bounced"; say "the email bounced back."

#### key in
- **Meaning:** to type information into a system.
- **Examples:**
  - Please key in the values.
  - I keyed in the data manually.
  - Can you key in the codes?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The numbers aren't in the system yet.
  - **Anna:** I'll key them in this afternoon.
- **Formal alternative:** enter / input
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Key in" (type in) is fine but slightly technical. "Enter the data" is more common in everyday email.

#### pencil in
- **Meaning:** to tentatively schedule something (not final).
- **Examples:**
  - Let's pencil in Thursday for now.
  - I've pencilled you in for 3pm.
  - Pencil it in and confirm later.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Can we meet next week?
  - **Anna:** Let's pencil in Tuesday and confirm on Monday.
- **Formal alternative:** tentatively schedule
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Useful phrase to show a plan is not final. Don't say "book tentatively"; native speakers say "pencil in."
