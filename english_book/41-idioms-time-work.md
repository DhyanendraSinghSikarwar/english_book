# Chapter 41: Professional Idioms — Time, Deadlines & Getting Things Done

This chapter covers 40 idioms about time, deadlines, effort, and productivity — the language of daily work life. You'll hear these constantly in stand-ups, planning, and status updates.

**How to read each entry:** the idiom, a simple meaning, an example, and a note where useful. **[Casual]** = informal. **[Careful]** = may confuse non-native speakers.

---

1. **against the clock** — rushing to finish before a deadline.
*Example:* "We're working against the clock to ship by Friday."

2. **beat the clock** — finish before time runs out.
*Example:* "We beat the clock and delivered a day early."

3. **crunch time** — a period of intense pressure near a deadline.
*Example:* "It's crunch time before the launch."

4. **down to the wire** — very close to the deadline.
*Example:* "It went down to the wire, but we made it." **[Careful]**

5. **in the nick of time** — just before it was too late.
*Example:* "We caught the bug in the nick of time."

6. **buy some time** — get extra time.
*Example:* "Let's buy some time by shipping a smaller first version."

7. **race against time** — a rush to finish quickly.
*Example:* "It's a race against time to fix this before the demo."

8. **around the clock** — all day and night, without stopping.
*Example:* "The system runs around the clock."

9. **call it a day** — stop working for the day.
*Example:* "It's late — let's call it a day." **[Casual]**

10. **burn the midnight oil** — work late into the night.
*Example:* "I burned the midnight oil to finish the report." **[Careful]**

11. **put in the hours** — work hard over time.
*Example:* "She put in the hours to master the new tool."

12. **hit the ground running** — start a new task fast and effectively.
*Example:* "The new hire hit the ground running."

13. **get the ball rolling** — start something (also in Ch 40).
*Example:* "Let's get the ball rolling on the analysis."

14. **take the ball and run with it** — take an idea and develop it independently.
*Example:* "I gave her the concept and she ran with it." **[Casual]**

15. **on the back burner** — a low priority for now.
*Example:* "We've put that feature on the back burner."

16. **front burner** — a high priority.
*Example:* "This is on the front burner now." **[Careful]**

17. **fall behind** — be slower than the schedule.
*Example:* "We fell behind because of the data issue."

18. **catch up** — reach the level or point expected.
*Example:* "I'll catch up on the emails this afternoon."

19. **ahead of the curve** — more advanced than others.
*Example:* "Our team is ahead of the curve on automation."

20. **behind the curve** — slower to adopt or progress than others.
*Example:* "We're a bit behind the curve on cloud tools."

21. **keep the ball rolling** — keep progress going.
*Example:* "Let's keep the ball rolling and book the next review."

22. **drop the ball** — fail to do something you were responsible for.
*Example:* "I dropped the ball on the follow-up — my apologies." **[Careful]**

23. **pick up the slack** — do the extra work someone else didn't.
*Example:* "She picked up the slack while I was on leave."

24. **pull your weight** — do your fair share of the work.
*Example:* "Everyone pulled their weight during crunch time."

25. **go the extra mile** — make more effort than expected.
*Example:* "The team went the extra mile to hit the date."

26. **cut corners** — do something quickly by skipping important steps (usually bad).
*Example:* "We shouldn't cut corners on testing."

27. **bite off more than you can chew** — take on more than you can handle.
*Example:* "I bit off more than I could chew this sprint." **[Careful]**

28. **spread yourself too thin** — try to do too many things at once.
*Example:* "I'm spread too thin across three projects."

29. **have a lot on your plate** — have a lot of work to do.
*Example:* "She has a lot on her plate right now."

30. **take a rain check** — postpone to another time.
*Example:* "Can I take a rain check on that meeting?" **[Careful]**

31. **put something on ice** — pause a project for later.
*Example:* "We've put the redesign on ice until Q4." **[Casual]**

32. **kill two birds with one stone** — solve two problems with one action.
*Example:* "This fix kills two birds with one stone." **[Careful]**

33. **hit a milestone** — reach an important point in a project.
*Example:* "We hit our first milestone on time."

34. **the eleventh hour** — the last possible moment.
*Example:* "The change came at the eleventh hour." **[Careful]**

35. **time is money** — wasted time costs the business.
*Example:* "Let's decide quickly — time is money."

36. **on the clock** — during paid working hours.
*Example:* "Let's keep personal chats off the clock." **[Casual]**

37. **a quick turnaround** — finishing something in a short time.
*Example:* "Thanks for the quick turnaround on the report."

38. **back-to-back** — meetings/tasks with no gap between them.
*Example:* "I'm back-to-back until three."

39. **get a head start** — begin earlier than others for an advantage.
*Example:* "Let's get a head start on next quarter's planning."

40. **in the pipeline** — planned or being prepared.
*Example:* "We have two more features in the pipeline."

---

## Using these idioms wisely

- These are very common in day-to-day work, so they're safe to use, but keep the **[Careful]** ones plain with non-native speakers.
- "Drop the ball" is useful for owning a mistake lightly, but in a formal report write "I missed this" instead.
- Avoid stacking them: "It's crunch time and we're down to the wire against the clock" is too much — pick one.

**Common Indian-English note:** Say "postpone" or "move earlier," not "prepone." Say "I have a lot on my plate," not "I am having too much work load only" (see Chapters 14 and 36).

---

**Chapter 41 summary.** These 40 idioms are the language of pace and productivity — deadlines, effort, priorities, and progress. They come up daily, especially in stand-ups and planning. Learn them, keep the trickier ones for people who'll understand, and you'll talk about time and work like a natural.
