# How To Use This Book

## Welcome

Hello, and welcome to **Corporate English Mastery**.

This book is for one kind of reader: an Indian professional who works with data — a data scientist, data analyst, ML engineer, data engineer, or someone close to that work — who is moving into a European English-speaking workplace. Maybe you just joined a team in Amsterdam, Dublin, Berlin, Zurich, or London. Maybe you work remotely for a European company. Maybe you are getting ready for that move. In all these cases, your **technical English is already good**. You can read papers, write code comments, and explain a model. What you need now is the **workplace English** — the way people speak in stand-ups, write emails, disagree politely, and sound natural in meetings.

Your English is not "bad." It is Indian English, which is a full, rich, correct version of the language. But some habits from Indian English sound strange or too formal to European colleagues. This book helps you notice those habits and shift them. The goal is not to erase your voice. The goal is to give you **choices** — so you can sound casual, neutral, or formal, whenever you want.

### How the book is organised

The book moves from small to big:

1. **Front matter** (this chapter) — how to use the book, a self-test, the idea of registers, key mindset shifts, and a 90-day plan.
2. **Core chapters** — one skill area each: emails, meetings, small talk, giving updates, disagreeing, feedback, and so on.
3. **Data-science scenarios** — real situations from your job: explaining a model to a non-technical manager, defending a result, handling a code review, running a sprint demo.
4. **Reference** — word lists, phrase banks, and quick-fix tables.

### The entry format

Most teaching points in this book use the same simple format, so you always know what you are looking at:

> **Situation** — where you would use this.
> **Indian-English version** — what you might say now.
> **International version** — a more natural option for a European workplace.
> **Why** — a short, plain reason for the change.
> **Ready-to-use lines** — copy these straight into your next meeting or email.

You do not need to read the book in order. But please read this chapter first. It sets up ideas — like **registers** and **mindset shifts** — that the rest of the book uses again and again.

Be honest. Nobody sees this but you. A wrong self-score wastes your time.

### Scoring guide

Count your ticks.

| Ticks | Your level | What to do |

## The 3 Registers: Casual, Neutral-Professional, Formal

This is the most important idea in the whole book. If you learn only one thing, learn this.

A **register** is the *level of formality* of your language. The same idea can be said in a relaxed way, a normal work way, or a very formal way. Good speakers switch between them without thinking. Many learners get stuck on **one** register — usually the formal one — and use it everywhere. That is the single biggest reason Indian professionals can sound "off" to European colleagues: too formal for the moment.

Here are the three registers.

### 1. Casual

Used with close teammates, in Slack/Teams chat, in quick hallway talk, at lunch. Short. Relaxed. Contractions (*I'm, don't, we've*). Sometimes no full sentences.

> *"Sure, on it."*
> *"Can you take a look when you get a sec?"*

### 2. Neutral-professional

Your **default** at work. Use this 80% of the time — most emails, most meetings, most messages with people you don't know well. Polite but not stiff. Clear. Friendly. This is the register most Indian professionals need to practise, because they jump straight from casual to formal and skip the middle.

> *"Sure, I'll get started on that."*
> *"Could you take a look when you have a moment?"*

### 3. Formal

Used with senior leaders you don't know, external clients, legal or HR matters, and official documents. Full sentences. No contractions. Careful, complete, polite.

> *"Certainly, I will begin working on this right away."*
> *"Would you be able to review this at your earliest convenience?"*

### The register table

Read across each row. Same meaning, three levels. Notice how the **neutral** column is usually the safest for daily work.

| Meaning | Casual | Neutral-Professional | Formal |
|---------|--------|----------------------|--------|
| Agreeing to do a task | "Sure, on it." | "Sure, I'll take care of it." | "Certainly, I will handle this straight away." |
| Asking for a review | "Can you check this?" | "Could you review this when you get a chance?" | "Would you be able to review this at your convenience?" |
| Saying you don't understand | "Wait, I'm lost." | "Sorry, I didn't quite follow that." | "I'm afraid I don't fully understand — could you clarify?" |
| Disagreeing | "Hmm, not sure about that." | "I see it a bit differently." | "I would respectfully like to offer a different view." |
| Asking for more time | "Need a bit more time." | "Could I have until tomorrow for this?" | "Would it be possible to extend the deadline to tomorrow?" |
| Reporting a problem | "Something's broken." | "We've hit an issue with the pipeline." | "I would like to flag an issue we have encountered with the pipeline." |
| Saying thank you | "Thanks a lot!" | "Thanks, that really helps." | "Thank you very much, I appreciate your support." |
| Following up | "Any update on this?" | "Just checking in on this — any progress?" | "I wanted to follow up regarding the status of this item." |
| Declining a request | "Can't do it today, sorry." | "I won't be able to get to this today, sorry." | "Unfortunately, I will not be able to accommodate this today." |
| Asking someone to repeat | "Sorry, say that again?" | "Could you repeat that, please?" | "I'm sorry, would you mind repeating that?" |
| Suggesting an idea | "What if we just cached it?" | "Maybe we could cache the results?" | "I would suggest that we consider caching the results." |
| Ending a message | "Cheers!" | "Thanks, and let me know." | "Thank you, and please let me know if you need anything further." |
| Admitting a mistake | "My bad, I'll fix it." | "That was my mistake — I'll fix it now." | "I apologise for the error; I will correct it immediately." |
| Praising a colleague | "Nice work!" | "Great job on this, really solid." | "Excellent work — this is very well done." |

**The rule of thumb:** when in doubt, go **neutral**. If you use casual with the wrong person, you seem too relaxed. If you use formal everywhere, you seem stiff and distant. Neutral almost never fails.

## Indian English vs British English vs International Business English

Indian English is a real, valid form of English. But some of its habits do not travel well. European colleagues — even in Britain — often find certain Indian-English phrases old-fashioned, too formal, or simply confusing.

"International Business English" is not a fixed dialect. It is a **middle style**: clear, simple, friendly, and neutral. It works with an Irish manager, a Dutch teammate, a German client, and a British stakeholder all at once. That is what you are aiming for.

Below are the key mindset shifts. Each one is a small habit change with a big effect.

### Shift 1: Drop the very formal filler words

Words like *kindly, revert, do the needful, please find attached, herewith* feel polite in India but sound stiff or dated abroad.

> **Before:** "Kindly do the needful and revert at the earliest."
> **After:** "Could you take care of this and let me know? Thanks."

### Shift 2: "Doubt" → "question"

In Indian English, "doubt" means "question." In international English, "doubt" means "I am not sure I believe it." This causes real confusion.

> **Before:** "Sir, I have a doubt about the model."
> **After:** "I have a question about the model."

### Shift 3: Stop saying "sir" and "ma'am" at work

In most European workplaces, people use first names — even with senior leaders. "Sir" and "ma'am" sound overly formal and can even feel uncomfortable.

> **Before:** "Yes sir, I will send it, sir."
> **After:** "Sure, Thomas — I'll send it over."

### Shift 4: "Prepone" is not a word abroad

"Prepone" (to move earlier) is Indian English. Nobody else uses it.

> **Before:** "Can we prepone the meeting to 10?"
> **After:** "Can we move the meeting earlier, to 10?"

### Shift 5: Numbers — lakh and crore don't work

European colleagues do not know lakhs and crores. Use millions and thousands, and use commas the international way.

> **Before:** "The dataset has 5 lakh rows."
> **After:** "The dataset has 500,000 rows" (or "half a million rows").

### Shift 6: Soften commands with "could/would"

Indian English often uses direct forms that can sound like orders. Add "could you" or "would you" to soften.

> **Before:** "Send me the report."
> **After:** "Could you send me the report?"

### Shift 7: Less over-apologising and less over-thanking

Long chains of "sorry to disturb you, kindly excuse me" feel heavy. One clear, polite line is better.

> **Before:** "Sorry sir, so sorry to disturb, kindly excuse me for the trouble..."
> **After:** "Sorry to interrupt — quick question."

### Shift 8: "The same" as a pronoun sounds odd

"Please send the same" is common in India but strange abroad. Use "it" or "them."

> **Before:** "I received the files. Will review the same."
> **After:** "I received the files. I'll review them."

### Shift 9: Continuous tense overuse

Indian English uses "-ing" forms where international English uses the simple present.

> **Before:** "I am having a question." / "I am understanding now."
> **After:** "I have a question." / "I understand now."

### Shift 10: Update on progress, don't announce completion formally

Small, natural updates beat formal declarations.

> **Before:** "I am pleased to inform you that the task has been completed."
> **After:** "Done — the task is finished. Let me know if you need anything else."

**Summary table:**

| Habit | Indian English | International Business English |
|-------|----------------|--------------------------------|
| Politeness fillers | kindly, revert, do the needful | could you, let me know, thanks |
| Question | "I have a doubt" | "I have a question" |
| Titles | sir, ma'am | first names |
| Timing | prepone | move earlier |
| Big numbers | lakh, crore | thousand, million |
| Commands | "Send me X" | "Could you send me X?" |
| Apologies | long chains of sorry | one short, clear line |
| Pronoun | "the same" | "it / them" |
| Tense | "I am having / understanding" | "I have / understand" |
| Completion | "pleased to inform you" | "Done — finished" |

## The 90-Day Study Plan

Twenty minutes a day. Ninety days. That is it. Small and steady beats long and rare. Do not study for three hours on Sunday and nothing else. Do a little **every day**.

Keep a simple notebook (paper or a note app). Write down phrases you like and mistakes you catch. Reviewing your own notes is the fastest way to improve.

### Phase 1 — Days 1–30: Foundation and awareness

**Goal:** notice your own habits and fix the biggest, most visible ones.

Each day (20 minutes):
- **10 min** — Read one section of this book (start with registers, then mindset shifts, then emails).
- **5 min** — Rewrite one of your own recent emails or Slack messages into the neutral register.
- **5 min** — Copy 3 ready-to-use lines into your notebook and say them out loud.

By the end of Phase 1 you should have dropped *kindly, revert, do the needful, prepone,* and "*doubt*." You should be writing most emails in the neutral register.

### Phase 2 — Days 31–60: Speaking and meetings

**Goal:** sound natural when you talk, not just when you write.

Each day (20 minutes):
- **7 min** — Read a meetings/small-talk/updates chapter.
- **8 min** — Speak out loud: practise a stand-up update, or record yourself giving a 60-second project summary. Listen back once.
- **5 min** — Watch or listen to 5 minutes of real English (a team meeting recording, a podcast, a conference talk). Note 2 useful phrases.

By the end of Phase 2 you should be able to give a clear stand-up update, make short small talk, and ask people to repeat things without stress.

### Phase 3 — Days 61–90: Polish and register control

**Goal:** switch registers on purpose and handle harder situations.

Each day (20 minutes):
- **7 min** — Read a chapter on disagreeing, feedback, or a data-science scenario.
- **8 min** — Practise switching: take one sentence and say it in all three registers (casual, neutral, formal).
- **5 min** — Review your notebook. Pick one old mistake and use the correct version in a real message today.

By the end of Phase 3 you should be able to disagree politely, present and take questions, and choose your register to match any person or situation.

### Weekly rhythm

Inside each phase, spread the skills across the week so you never get bored:

| Day | Focus | Sample 20-minute activity |
|-----|-------|---------------------------|
| **Monday** | Writing | Rewrite one real email into neutral register. |
| **Tuesday** | Speaking | Record a 60-second update; listen back once. |
| **Wednesday** | Listening | Listen to 5 min of real English; note 2 phrases. |
| **Thursday** | Vocabulary | Learn 5 phrase-bank lines; say them aloud. |
| **Friday** | Registers | Take 2 sentences; write all 3 register versions. |
| **Saturday** | Review | Read your notebook; fix your most common mistake. |
| **Sunday** | Rest / light | Watch one short talk in English, just for fun. No pressure. |

**A few rules for the whole 90 days:**

1. **Speak out loud.** Reading silently is not enough. Your mouth needs practice.
2. **Use it the same day.** If you learn a phrase in the morning, use it at work before evening.
3. **Miss a day? Don't quit.** Just start again tomorrow. Streaks are nice, but the habit matters more than a perfect record.
4. **Track one win a week.** "I disagreed politely in the sprint review." Small wins keep you going.

That's it. Turn the page, take the self-test if you haven't, and let's begin.
