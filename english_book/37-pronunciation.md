# Chapter 37: Pronunciation & Clarity

You don't need to lose your accent — accents are fine and you should keep yours with pride. The goal of this chapter is **clarity**, not sounding "British" or "American." A few specific sounds and rhythm patterns, when adjusted, make a big difference to how easily European colleagues understand you. That's what matters.

---

## 1. Sounds that matter most for clarity

Some sounds, if unclear, cause real misunderstandings. Focus your effort here.

### /v/ vs /w/
Many Indian speakers merge these two sounds, so "vine" and "wine," or "vest" and "west," sound the same.
- **/v/** — top teeth touch the bottom lip, and you make a buzzing sound (like "vvv"). Words: **v**ery, **v**alue, deli**v**er, **v**ersion.
- **/w/** — round your lips, no teeth (like "www"). Words: **w**e, **w**ork, **w**ould, soft**w**are.
- **Practice pairs:** vine/wine, vest/west, vary/wary, vet/wet.
- **Practice sentence:** "We value every version we deliver."

### The "th" sounds (/θ/ and /ð/)
Indian English often replaces "th" with a "t" or "d" sound, so "think" becomes "tink" and "this" becomes "dis."
- **/θ/ (voiceless)** — put your tongue lightly between your teeth and blow: **th**ink, **th**ree, **th**anks, me**th**od.
- **/ð/ (voiced)** — same tongue position, but with voice: **th**is, **th**at, **th**e, wi**th**, o**th**er.
- **Practice:** "I think this method is the best one." / "Thanks for the three things."
- **Note:** This is a small thing; being understood matters more than perfection. But the tongue-between-teeth habit is easy to build.

### Ending consonants
Indian speakers sometimes soften or drop the final consonant of a word, which can blur meaning. Pronounce the ending clearly.
- "cost" (not "cos"), "list" (not "lis"), "helped" (say the "t"), "asked" (say the "kt").
- Plurals and past tenses matter: "models" (with the "s"), "worked" (with the "t"), "used" (with the "d").
- **Practice:** "The tests we asked for helped a lot." / "We fixed the models and checked the results."

### /s/ vs /ʃ/ ("s" vs "sh")
Some speakers swap these.
- **/s/** — "sip," "see," "sink," "sales."
- **/ʃ/** — "ship," "she," "shrink," "shales."
- **Practice:** "She sells six ships." / "The sales team saw the ship."

## 2. Word stress that changes meaning

English words can change from a noun to a verb depending on which syllable you stress. Getting this right adds polish. (First introduced in Chapter 20.)

| Word | Noun (stress 1st) | Verb (stress 2nd) |
|---|---|---|
| record | **RE**cord | re**CORD** |
| present | **PRE**sent | pre**SENT** |
| object | **OB**ject | ob**JECT** |
| increase | **IN**crease | in**CREASE** |
| decrease | **DE**crease | de**CREASE** |
| project | **PRO**ject | pro**JECT** |
| conduct | **CON**duct | con**DUCT** |
| contract | **CON**tract | con**TRACT** |
| permit | **PER**mit | per**MIT** |
| export | **EX**port | ex**PORT** |

**Examples:**
- "Let me **pre**sent the **PRE**sent." → "Let me pre**SENT** the **PRE**sent." (verb, then noun)
- "We saw an **IN**crease." (noun) vs "Sales will in**CREASE**." (verb)

## 3. Sentence stress and rhythm (the biggest clarity win)

This is more important than any single sound. English stresses the **meaning-carrying words** (nouns, main verbs, adjectives, key numbers) and rushes through the small words (a, the, is, of, to, and). Many Indian languages give each syllable more equal time, which is why the rhythm can sound different.

- **Stress:** the important words. **Rush:** the small words.
- Flat (each word equal): "The-mo-del-will-be-rea-dy-by-Fri-day." (harder to follow)
- Natural: "The **model** will be **ready** by **Friday**." (stress model, ready, Friday)

**Practice:** Take a sentence and tap the table on the stressed words:
> "We **found** that most **churn** happens in the **first month**."
(Tap on: found, churn, first month.)

Working on this rhythm does more for your clarity than fixing any individual sound.

## 4. Intonation: statements vs questions

- **Statements** — your voice goes **down** at the end. "The report is ready." (falling)
- **Yes/no questions** — your voice goes **up** at the end. "Is the report ready?" (rising)
- **Wh- questions** (what, when, why) — usually go **down** at the end. "When is it ready?" (falling)

**Watch out:** if statements end with a rising tone, they can sound like questions or sound uncertain. Practise ending your statements with a confident, falling tone. This alone can make you sound more sure of yourself.

## 5. Speaking speed — slowing down = sounding senior

The most common issue for many speakers, especially when nervous, is speaking too fast. Fast speech blurs sounds and is hard to follow, especially on video calls.

- **Slow down deliberately.** It feels unnatural at first, but to listeners it sounds clear and confident.
- **Pause at commas and full stops.** Give listeners time to process.
- **Slow down even more for numbers, names, and key points.** "The saving is... two point three... million euros."
- Senior people often speak slowly and pause. Speed signals nervousness; calm pace signals authority.

## 6. Names — pronouncing European names, and helping others with yours

**Learning European names:** Some common ones and rough pronunciations:
- Dutch: "Sjoerd" (roughly "Shoort"), "Guus" ("Hhoos"), "Joris" ("Yoris"). The Dutch "g" is a throaty sound.
- German: "Jürgen" ("Yur-gen"), "Matthias" ("Mat-tee-as"), "-ei-" sounds like "eye" (Klein = "Kline").
- French: "Guillaume" ("Ghee-yohm"), silent final consonants ("Renault" = "Ren-oh").
- Nordic: "Bjørn" ("Byorn"), "Sigrid" ("Seeg-rid").

**Tip:** If you're unsure, just ask: "Sorry, how do you pronounce your name?" People appreciate the effort and never mind being asked.

**Helping others with your name:** Many Europeans will struggle with Indian names. Make it easy:
- Offer a simple guide: "It's Dhyanendra — you can think of it as 'Dhya-nen-dra'."
- If you're happy with a short form, offer it: "Feel free to call me Dhyan."
- Say it slowly and clearly when you introduce yourself, and don't be shy about correcting people kindly: "Almost — it's 'Dhya-nen-dra'."
- Never feel you must change your name — just make it easy to say.

## 7. Practical ways to improve

- **Listen and copy (shadowing):** play a short clip of a clear English speaker (podcast, talk), pause after each sentence, and repeat it copying the rhythm and intonation. Ten minutes a day works wonders.
- **Record yourself** reading a paragraph, then compare to a native clip. You'll hear the differences.
- **Read aloud** for a few minutes daily, focusing on clear endings and natural stress.
- **Ask a trusted colleague** which words of yours are hardest to catch — then focus there.
- **Don't chase a perfect accent.** Chase clarity. Being clearly understood, at a calm pace, is the whole goal.

---

## Mindset: keep your accent, gain clarity

Your accent is part of who you are, and English is a global language with countless accents. European colleagues work with accents from all over the world every day. Nobody expects or needs you to sound British or American. What helps everyone — including you — is being **clear**: distinct key sounds, natural rhythm, confident falling intonation on statements, and an unhurried pace. Aim for that, and you'll be understood easily while keeping your own voice.
