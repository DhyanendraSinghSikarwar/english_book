# Chapter 13: Upgrading Full Sentences

Single words matter, but whole sentences matter more. This chapter shows how to turn simple or awkward sentences into confident, professional ones. Each entry gives the simple version, the upgraded version, and a note on when to use it.

The goal is not to sound fancy. It is to sound clear, calm, and in control — especially when the topic is hard (bad news, disagreement, or not knowing an answer).

---

## 1. Softening opinions and being diplomatic

**"I think..."**
→ "In my assessment..." / "I'd argue that..." / "My understanding is..." / "From what I can see..."
*When to use:* In meetings and reports, these sound more considered than "I think." Use "I'd argue that..." when you want to state a position with confidence but stay open to debate.

**"This is wrong."**
→ "This may need revisiting." / "There appears to be a discrepancy here." / "I'm not sure this is quite right."
*When to use:* When correcting someone senior or a client. Direct "this is wrong" can feel harsh. The softer version fixes the problem without bruising egos. (Note: in very direct cultures like the Netherlands or Germany, "I think this is incorrect" is perfectly fine and even respected.)

**"I don't agree."**
→ "I see it slightly differently." / "I have a different view on this." / "I'm not sure I agree — my concern is..."
*When to use:* Disagreeing politely in a meeting. Naming your concern makes it a discussion, not a fight.

**"You are wrong."**
→ "I think there may be a misunderstanding." / "Can we double-check that together?"
*When to use:* Never say "you are wrong" directly at work. Focus on the issue, not the person.

## 2. Handling "I don't know"

**"I don't know."**
→ "I'll need to confirm that." / "Let me look into it and get back to you." / "I don't have that figure to hand, but I'll follow up."
*When to use:* Saying "I don't know" alone can sound unprepared. Adding a next step shows you're on top of it. This is one of the most useful upgrades in this book — practise it.

**"I'm not sure."**
→ "I'd want to verify before committing to a number." / "My initial view is X, but let me confirm."
*When to use:* When you have a rough idea but need to check. It shows honesty and care for accuracy.

## 3. Delivering bad news

**"The project is late."**
→ "We're tracking behind on the timeline, and here's the plan to recover." / "The delivery date has slipped; let me explain why and what we're doing about it."
*When to use:* Always pair bad news with a plan. Europeans value honesty, but they value a solution even more.

**"The model failed."**
→ "The experiment didn't confirm our hypothesis — here's what we learned." / "The results weren't what we expected, which tells us something useful."
*When to use:* In data science, failed experiments are normal. Frame them as learning, not disaster.

**"We have a big problem."**
→ "We've hit a significant issue, and I want to walk you through it and the options." / "There's a risk I need to flag early."
*When to use:* Stay calm. "Big problem" sounds panicked. "Significant issue" plus options sounds like leadership.

## 4. Making requests

**"Do this by Friday."**
→ "Could you have this ready by Friday?" / "Would it be possible to complete this by Friday?" / "It would be a great help if you could finish this by Friday."
*When to use:* Direct orders sound rude to peers. Questions are polite and still clear.

**"Send me the file."**
→ "Could you send over the file when you have a moment?" / "Would you mind sharing the file?"
*When to use:* Everyday requests. "When you have a moment" adds politeness without losing the ask.

**"I need this now."**
→ "This is fairly urgent — could you prioritise it today?" / "I'd appreciate it if this could be treated as a priority."
*When to use:* When something truly is urgent. Naming urgency clearly is fine; being rude is not.

## 5. Giving and receiving feedback

**"Your work is bad."**
→ "I think there's room to strengthen a few areas — shall we go through them?" / "This is a solid start; here's how we can take it further."
*When to use:* Feedback should be about growth, not blame. Start with something positive, then be specific.

**"You made a mistake."**
→ "I spotted a small issue here — easy fix." / "There's a discrepancy in this section; let's sort it out."
*When to use:* Focus on the issue, not the person. Keep it light for small things.

**"That's a good point, but..."**
→ "That's a fair point. Building on that..." / "I agree with much of that. One thing I'd add is..."
*When to use:* "Yes, but" feels like disagreement. "Yes, and" feels like collaboration.

## 6. Sounding confident and senior

**"Maybe we can try this."**
→ "I'd recommend we try this." / "My suggestion would be to test this approach."
*When to use:* "Maybe" sounds unsure. "I recommend" sounds like an expert giving advice.

**"I did the analysis and I think it's okay."**
→ "I completed the analysis, and the results are solid." / "The analysis is done and the findings are clear."
*When to use:* Own your work. "It's okay" undersells good work. Say it's "solid" or "clear."

**"It's not my fault."**
→ "Let me explain what happened and how we'll prevent it." / "Here's the root cause, and here's the fix."
*When to use:* Blaming sounds defensive. Explaining and fixing sounds mature.

## 7. In presentations and reports

**"Here is the data."**
→ "Let me walk you through the key findings." / "Here's what the data is telling us."
*When to use:* Frame data as a story, not a dump of numbers.

**"The number went up a lot."**
→ "We saw a substantial increase of 30%." / "There was a marked rise this quarter."
*When to use:* Be specific with numbers, and use strong words like "substantial" or "marked."

**"I'm almost done."**
→ "I'm on track to complete this by [date]." / "This is in the final stages and will be ready by [date]."
*When to use:* Give a date, not a vague "almost." Dates build trust.

## 8. Meetings and collaboration

**"Let's stop now."**
→ "Let's wrap up here and take the rest offline." / "In the interest of time, let's pause here."
*When to use:* Ending a meeting politely without sounding abrupt.

**"That's not the topic."**
→ "That's an important point, but let's park it for now and stay on the agenda." / "Good question — can we take that separately?"
*When to use:* Keeping a meeting on track without shutting people down.

**"Repeat what you said."**
→ "Could you say a bit more about that?" / "Sorry, could you run that by me again?"
*When to use:* Asking someone to repeat or clarify, politely.

## 9. Committing and following up

**"I'll do it."**
→ "I'll take that on and have it ready by [date]." / "Consider it done — I'll update you by Thursday."
*When to use:* Adding a date or clear commitment sounds reliable.

**"I forgot."**
→ "This slipped through — my apologies. I'll sort it today." / "I dropped the ball on this; here's my plan to catch up."
*When to use:* Owning a miss honestly, without over-apologising. Say it once, then move to the fix.

**"I'll try."**
→ "I'll do my best and let you know by [date] if there's any risk." / "I'm confident I can, but I'll flag early if anything changes."
*When to use:* "I'll try" sounds weak. Commit, but be honest about risk.

## The 15-sentence starter kit (memorise these)

These are the highest-value sentence upgrades. Learn them by heart:

1. "Let me look into it and get back to you." (for "I don't know")
2. "I'd recommend..." (for stating a view)
3. "Could you have this ready by [date]?" (for requests)
4. "The experiment didn't confirm our hypothesis — here's what we learned." (for failures)
5. "That's a fair point. Building on that..." (for agreement + adding)
6. "I see it slightly differently — my concern is..." (for polite disagreement)
7. "Let me walk you through the key findings." (for presenting)
8. "We're tracking behind, and here's the recovery plan." (for delays)
9. "Just to clarify, do you mean X or Y?" (for confirming)
10. "I'll take that on and update you by [date]." (for commitment)
11. "Apologies for the delay." (for a genuine sorry, said once)
12. "Let's park that and stay on the agenda." (for meeting control)
13. "There appears to be a discrepancy here." (for corrections)
14. "This is fairly urgent — could you prioritise it today?" (for urgency)
15. "In my assessment, the data suggests..." (for confident analysis)
