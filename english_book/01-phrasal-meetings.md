# Chapter 1: Meetings & Discussions

Meetings are where much of your work becomes visible. In European English-speaking offices, people use a lot of phrasal verbs to steer discussions, manage time, and invite others to speak. This chapter gives you the exact phrases you need to sound natural and confident when you talk in meetings.

---

#### bring up
- **Meaning:** to start talking about a topic.
- **Examples:**
  - I want to bring up the data quality issue before we finish.
  - She brought up a good point about the deadline.
  - Can we bring this up in the next stand-up?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Priya:** Before we close, I'd like to bring up the missing labels in the dataset.
  - **Lars:** Good catch. Let's put it on the agenda for tomorrow.
- **Formal alternative:** raise / mention
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Indian speakers often say "I want to *put up* this topic." Use *bring up* instead. "Put up" sounds like you are displaying a poster.

#### follow up
- **Meaning:** to check on something again after the first contact.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll follow up with the client next week.
  - Can you follow up on the pending approval?
  - Thanks for following up so quickly.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** Did the vendor reply about the API keys?
  - **You:** Not yet. I'll follow up with them this afternoon.
- **Formal alternative:** pursue / check on
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Many Indians say "I will do the needful follow-up." Drop "do the needful." Just say "I'll follow up."

#### go over
- **Meaning:** to review or examine something carefully.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's go over the results one more time.
  - Can we go over the model assumptions together?
  - I went over your report last night.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Anna:** The numbers look strange to me.
  - **You:** Let's go over the query together and find the error.
- **Formal alternative:** review / examine
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Avoid "let us go through *thoroughly over* it." Pick one: *go over* or *go through*, not both.

#### run through
- **Meaning:** to quickly go over the main points.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me run through the agenda quickly.
  - Can you run through the steps again?
  - We ran through the demo before the client call.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I'll run through the pipeline in three minutes, then take questions.
  - **Tom:** Perfect, keep it short.
- **Formal alternative:** summarise / outline
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't confuse with "run out." "Run through" means review; "run out" means finish supply.

#### wrap up
- **Meaning:** to finish or bring to an end.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's wrap up here; we're out of time.
  - We wrapped up the sprint yesterday.
  - I'll wrap up my part in two minutes.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Chair:** We have five minutes left.
  - **You:** Let me wrap up. The key action is to retrain the model by Friday.
- **Formal alternative:** conclude / finalise
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Avoid "let us *wind up* the meeting" when you mean finish neatly. "Wind up" can sound abrupt; "wrap up" is smoother.

#### kick off
- **Meaning:** to start something.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's kick off with a quick update.
  - We kicked off the project on Monday.
  - Who wants to kick off the discussion?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Shall we begin?
  - **You:** Yes, I'll kick off with the model results.
- **Formal alternative:** commence / begin
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Do not say "let us *start off* the meeting kickoff." One phrase is enough: "let's kick off."

#### move on
- **Meaning:** to go to the next topic.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's move on to the next item.
  - Can we move on? We're short on time.
  - We've covered this, so let's move on.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I think we agree on the metric. Shall we move on to deployment?
  - **Sara:** Yes, let's move on.
- **Formal alternative:** proceed / continue
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Avoid "let us *move forward on* the next point" when you simply mean the next topic. "Move on" is cleaner.

#### touch on
- **Meaning:** to mention briefly.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll just touch on the budget quickly.
  - She touched on the risks but didn't go deep.
  - Let me touch on one more thing.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I'll touch on data privacy, then hand over to Ravi for the details.
  - **Ravi:** Sounds good.
- **Formal alternative:** mention / address briefly
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Do not say "touch upon *about* it." Just "touch on it" or "touch upon it."

#### weigh in
- **Meaning:** to give your opinion on a discussion.
- **Examples:**
  - I'd like to weigh in on the choice of database.
  - Can the design team weigh in here?
  - Feel free to weigh in anytime.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Chair:** Any thoughts on the vendor?
  - **You:** Let me weigh in. I think the cost is too high.
- **Formal alternative:** contribute / comment
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Indians sometimes say "let me *give my inputs*." Native usage prefers "weigh in" or "share my view."

#### chip in
- **Meaning:** to add a small comment to a discussion.
- **Examples:**
  - Can I chip in with a quick point?
  - Everyone chipped in with ideas.
  - Feel free to chip in if you disagree.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** May I chip in? The API also has a rate limit we should note.
  - **Lars:** Good point, thanks.
- **Formal alternative:** contribute / add
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Chip in" is informal and friendly. Don't use it in a very formal written report; use "add" there.

#### cut in
- **Meaning:** to interrupt someone who is speaking.
- **Examples:**
  - Sorry to cut in, but we need to decide now.
  - He kept cutting in during my update.
  - May I cut in for one second?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Sorry to cut in, but the client just joined the call.
  - **Anna:** No problem, go ahead.
- **Formal alternative:** interrupt / interject
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Always soften "cut in" with "sorry" or "may I." Cutting in without a softener sounds rude in European offices.

#### speak up
- **Meaning:** to say your opinion openly, or to talk more loudly.
- **Examples:**
  - Please speak up if you disagree.
  - Could you speak up? I can't hear you.
  - She's not afraid to speak up in meetings.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** If anyone sees a risk, please speak up now.
  - **You:** I have one concern about the timeline.
- **Formal alternative:** voice concerns / state openly
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't confuse "speak up" (give opinion / louder) with "speak out" (public protest). In meetings you "speak up."

#### sum up
- **Meaning:** to give a short summary.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me sum up the main points.
  - To sum up, we're on track.
  - Can you sum up the findings?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** To sum up: the model works, but we need more data for edge cases.
  - **Chair:** Clear, thank you.
- **Formal alternative:** summarise / recap
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Avoid "let me *sum up* in short in brief." Choose one phrase; "to sum up" is enough.

#### round off
- **Meaning:** to finish something in a pleasant, complete way.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's round off with the next steps.
  - She rounded off the talk with a demo.
  - We rounded off the meeting on a positive note.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** To round off, I'll share the slides and the recording after this.
  - **Team:** Great, thanks.
- **Formal alternative:** conclude / finish
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Round off" also means rounding numbers. In meetings the sense is "finish nicely." Context makes it clear, but say "round off the meeting" to avoid confusion.

#### put forward
- **Meaning:** to suggest an idea or plan.
- **Examples:**
  - I'd like to put forward a new approach.
  - She put forward three options.
  - Can you put forward your proposal in writing?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I'd like to put forward a simpler model as a baseline.
  - **Lars:** Interesting, tell us more.
- **Formal alternative:** propose / suggest
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "I want to *put forth* my idea forward." Say either "put forward" or "put forth" (put forward is more common in speech).

#### back up
- **Meaning:** to support someone or something with evidence.
- **Examples:**
  - Can you back up that claim with data?
  - I'll back you up in the meeting.
  - The results back up our theory.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I think the drift is real.
  - **Anna:** I'll back you up; my logs show the same thing.
- **Formal alternative:** support / substantiate
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Back up" (support) is different from "back up" (copy data). In discussions, the sense is "support." Use tone and context.

#### come up
- **Meaning:** to arise or happen, often unexpectedly.
- **Examples:**
  - A problem came up during testing.
  - Something urgent came up, so I'll be late.
  - This question comes up a lot.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Sorry, an issue came up with the server, so I need five minutes.
  - **Chair:** No problem, we'll wait.
- **Formal alternative:** arise / occur
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "one doubt is coming up." Use "a question came up" or "I have a question." Avoid "doubt" for "question."

#### call off
- **Meaning:** to cancel something.
- **Examples:**
  - We had to call off the demo.
  - They called off the meeting at the last minute.
  - Let's not call it off; let's reschedule.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Manager:** The client isn't ready.
  - **You:** Should we call off today's review and move it to Thursday?
- **Formal alternative:** cancel
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "the meeting is *cancelled off*." Just "cancelled" or "called off," not both.

#### put off
- **Meaning:** to postpone to a later time.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's put off the release until next week.
  - The meeting was put off twice.
  - Don't put off the hard tasks.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The data isn't ready. Can we put off the review by two days?
  - **Lead:** Fine, Thursday then.
- **Formal alternative:** postpone / defer
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Put off" also means "to disgust." In work context it means postpone, but avoid "I am put off from this task" when you mean postpone.

#### push back
- **Meaning:** to move a deadline later, OR to disagree politely.
- **Examples:**
  - Can we push back the deadline by a week?
  - She pushed back on the scope.
  - I'd like to push back on that estimate.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I'd like to push back on the timeline. Two days isn't realistic.
  - **Lead:** Understood. Let's discuss a new date.
- **Formal alternative:** postpone (for dates) / object (for disagreeing)
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Push back" to disagree is common and accepted in Europe. Don't fear it; it is polite, not aggressive, when said calmly.

#### circle back
- **Meaning:** to return to a topic later.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's circle back to this after lunch.
  - I'll circle back with an answer tomorrow.
  - Can we circle back on the budget?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I don't have the number now. Can I circle back after I check the logs?
  - **Anna:** Sure, circle back by end of day.
- **Formal alternative:** revisit / return to
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Circle back" is business slang. It is fine in meetings, but in formal writing use "revisit."

#### drill down
- **Meaning:** to look at something in more detail.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's drill down into the failed cases.
  - Can we drill down on the churn numbers?
  - I want to drill down before we decide.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The overall accuracy is fine, but let's drill down into the minority class.
  - **Lars:** Yes, that's where it fails.
- **Formal alternative:** examine in detail / analyse
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "drill down *into deeper*." "Drill down into" already means going deeper.

#### zoom in / zoom out
- **Meaning:** zoom in = focus on detail; zoom out = look at the big picture.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's zoom in on the last quarter.
  - Zooming out, the trend is upward.
  - Can we zoom in on that chart?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** If we zoom out, the model is fine. If we zoom in on new users, it struggles.
  - **Anna:** Good framing.
- **Formal alternative:** focus (zoom in) / take a broader view (zoom out)
- **Indian-English pitfall:** These are figurative in meetings, not literal screen zoom. Make clear you mean focus level, not the actual zoom button.

#### hash out
- **Meaning:** to discuss something in detail until you agree.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's hash out the details offline.
  - We hashed out the plan yesterday.
  - We need to hash out who owns what.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** There's disagreement on the schema. Let's hash it out in a short call.
  - **Tom:** Agreed, book 30 minutes.
- **Formal alternative:** discuss thoroughly / negotiate
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Hash out" is informal. Don't put it in a formal proposal; use "discuss and agree."

#### iron out
- **Meaning:** to solve small problems or remove difficulties.
- **Examples:**
  - We still need to iron out a few bugs.
  - Let's iron out the details before the demo.
  - The plan is good; just iron out the timing.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The pipeline works, but we need to iron out the retry logic.
  - **Anna:** Let's do that today.
- **Formal alternative:** resolve / smooth out
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't confuse with the clothes iron. It means "remove small problems," not "press flat."

#### sort out
- **Meaning:** to solve a problem or organise something.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll sort out the access issue.
  - Let's sort this out quickly.
  - Can you sort out the meeting room?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The permissions are broken.
  - **Lars:** Don't worry, I'll sort it out this morning.
- **Formal alternative:** resolve / arrange
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Sort out" is very common and useful. Avoid the heavier "do the needful to solve"; just say "I'll sort it out."

#### talk through
- **Meaning:** to discuss something step by step.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me talk you through the process.
  - We talked through the risks.
  - Can we talk through the plan tomorrow?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I'll talk you through the deployment steps so nothing is missed.
  - **Sara:** Please do.
- **Formal alternative:** explain / discuss in detail
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Talk through" needs an object or "through." Don't just say "let me talk"; say "let me talk you through it."

#### walk through
- **Meaning:** to guide someone through steps carefully.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll walk you through the code.
  - She walked us through the dashboard.
  - Can you walk me through the setup?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **New hire:** I don't understand the pipeline.
  - **You:** No problem, let me walk you through it screen by screen.
- **Formal alternative:** guide / demonstrate
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Walk through" (guide) vs "walk out" (leave). Use "walk you through" when demonstrating.

#### sign off
- **Meaning:** to give final approval, OR to end a message/call.
- **Examples:**
  - The manager needs to sign off on the budget.
  - Once you sign off, we'll deploy.
  - I'll sign off now; talk tomorrow.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The tests pass. Can you sign off so we can release?
  - **Lead:** Approved. Sign off given.
- **Formal alternative:** approve / authorise
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "kindly do the sign off." Say "please sign off on it."

#### tick off
- **Meaning:** (UK) to mark an item as done on a list.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's tick off the completed tasks.
  - I ticked off three items today.
  - Tick it off once it's tested.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Data cleaned, model trained. Two items ticked off.
  - **Anna:** Great progress.
- **Formal alternative:** mark as complete / check off
- **Indian-English pitfall:** In British English "tick off" also means "to scold." Context matters; in a task list it means "mark done." Americans say "check off."

#### open up (a discussion)
- **Meaning:** to start allowing others to speak or contribute.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's open up the floor for questions.
  - I'll open up the discussion now.
  - She opened up the topic for debate.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** That's my update. Let me open it up for questions.
  - **Tom:** I have one on scaling.
- **Formal alternative:** invite / begin discussion
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Say "open up the floor," not "open the floor for anybody who wants." Keep it short.

#### call on
- **Meaning:** to ask a specific person to speak.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me call on Priya for the numbers.
  - The chair called on each team in turn.
  - I'll call on you if I need details.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Chair:** For the data update, I'll call on Ravi.
  - **Ravi:** Thanks, here are the results.
- **Formal alternative:** invite / ask
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't confuse with "call up" (phone). "Call on someone" in a meeting means to invite them to speak.

#### chime in
- **Meaning:** to join a conversation with a comment (like chip in).
- **Examples:**
  - Feel free to chime in.
  - He chimed in with a useful point.
  - Others chimed in to agree.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** May I chime in? We tried that last quarter and it failed.
  - **Lars:** Good to know.
- **Formal alternative:** contribute / add
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Very informal. Use "add" in written minutes.

#### bring in
- **Meaning:** to invite someone into the discussion.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's bring in the design team here.
  - I'll bring in Anna for the security view.
  - Should we bring in legal on this?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** For the privacy angle, let me bring in our data protection lead.
  - **Chair:** Yes, please.
- **Formal alternative:** involve / include
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Bring in" (involve a person) vs "bring up" (start a topic). Keep them separate.

#### wind down
- **Meaning:** to gradually bring a meeting toward the end.
- **Examples:**
  - We're starting to wind down.
  - Let's wind down and note the actions.
  - The session is winding down now.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Chair:** We're at time, so let's wind down.
  - **You:** I'll capture the action points.
- **Formal alternative:** conclude gradually
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Wind down" (slow toward end) vs "wind up" (finish/close a company). Don't mix them.

#### set out
- **Meaning:** to explain or present your plan clearly.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me set out the three options.
  - She set out the goals at the start.
  - I'll set out the timeline now.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Let me set out the plan: build, test, then deploy.
  - **Lead:** Clear structure, thanks.
- **Formal alternative:** outline / present
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Set out" (explain) vs "set up" (arrange/install). Don't swap them.

#### go ahead
- **Meaning:** to proceed, or to give permission to proceed.
- **Examples:**
  - Please go ahead with your point.
  - We got the go-ahead to deploy.
  - Go ahead, I'm listening.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** May I share the results?
  - **Chair:** Yes, go ahead.
- **Formal alternative:** proceed / permission granted
- **Indian-English pitfall:** As a noun it's "the go-ahead" (with a hyphen). Don't write "the go ahead" in reports.
