# Chapter 4: Problems & Solutions

Things go wrong at work: jobs fail, plans collapse, and people need help. This chapter gives you the phrasal verbs to describe problems clearly and talk about fixing them. Using the right phrase here helps you sound calm and in control during difficult moments.

---

#### run into
- **Meaning:** to meet a problem unexpectedly.
- **Examples:**
  - We ran into a bug during testing.
  - I ran into a memory error.
  - They ran into trouble with the API.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** How's the deployment?
  - **You:** We ran into a permissions error, but I'm fixing it now.
- **Formal alternative:** encounter / face
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Run into a problem," not "run into with a problem." Also "run into" can mean to meet a person by chance.

#### come across
- **Meaning:** to find something by chance.
- **Examples:**
  - I came across a strange record.
  - We came across duplicate rows.
  - She came across a useful library.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** While cleaning the data, I came across some negative ages.
  - **Anna:** Those are errors; drop them.
- **Formal alternative:** discover / find
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Come across" (find by chance) also means "to seem" ("she comes across as confident"). Context decides.

#### deal with
- **Meaning:** to handle a problem.
- **Examples:**
  - We'll deal with the errors first.
  - Can you deal with this ticket?
  - I dealt with the complaint.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** There's a data quality issue.
  - **Lead:** Deal with it before we train the model.
- **Formal alternative:** handle / address
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Deal with," not "deal to." For problems, always "deal with."

#### get around
- **Meaning:** to find a way to avoid a problem.
- **Examples:**
  - We got around the limit with caching.
  - There's no way to get around it.
  - Let's get around the API restriction.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The API only allows 100 calls a minute.
  - **Anna:** Let's get around it by batching the requests.
- **Formal alternative:** circumvent / work around
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Get around" (avoid a problem) vs "get around to" (finally do). Without "to," it means to bypass an obstacle.

#### narrow down
- **Meaning:** to reduce options to the most likely ones.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's narrow down the causes.
  - We narrowed it down to two files.
  - Can you narrow down the search?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The bug could be anywhere.
  - **Lead:** Add logging to narrow it down.
- **Formal alternative:** reduce / focus
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Narrow down," not "narrow down to less." "Narrow down to X" states the final options; don't add extra words.

#### rule out
- **Meaning:** to remove something as a possibility.
- **Examples:**
  - We can rule out the network.
  - Let's rule out data corruption.
  - I've ruled out the obvious causes.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Could it be the database?
  - **Anna:** No, I checked the logs and ruled that out.
- **Formal alternative:** eliminate / exclude
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Rule out" (eliminate) vs "rule over" (govern). In debugging, "rule out" means to confirm something is not the cause.

#### single out
- **Meaning:** to pick one thing or person from a group.
- **Examples:**
  - The report singled out one team.
  - Let's single out the worst case.
  - She singled out the failing test.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Most classes work, but let me single out the one that fails.
  - **Lead:** Yes, focus there.
- **Formal alternative:** identify / isolate
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Single out" can sound like unfair blame if used about people. For analysis it's neutral; for people, use it carefully.

#### zero in on
- **Meaning:** to focus closely on one thing.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's zero in on the root cause.
  - We zeroed in on the slow query.
  - Can you zero in on the error?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** After adding logs, I zeroed in on the join step.
  - **Anna:** That's where the slowdown is.
- **Formal alternative:** focus on / pinpoint
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Zero in on," not "zero on" or "zero into." The full phrase is "zero in on."

#### boil down to
- **Meaning:** to be, in the end, a matter of one main thing.
- **Examples:**
  - It all boils down to data quality.
  - The issue boils down to cost.
  - It boils down to one bad assumption.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Why is the model wrong?
  - **You:** It boils down to biased training data.
- **Formal alternative:** amount to / come down to
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Boil down to" needs "to." Don't say "it boils down that." Say "it boils down to X."

#### stem from
- **Meaning:** to be caused by something.
- **Examples:**
  - The errors stem from bad input.
  - This stems from a config change.
  - The delay stems from missing data.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The crashes stem from a memory leak in the loader.
  - **Anna:** Then let's fix the loader.
- **Formal alternative:** originate from / result from
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Stem from" (be caused by) is more formal and precise than "come from." Good for written incident reports.

#### account for
- **Meaning:** to explain or be the cause of something.
- **Examples:**
  - What accounts for the drop?
  - This accounts for 80% of errors.
  - Can you account for the gap?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Why did accuracy fall?
  - **You:** The new data accounts for most of the drop.
- **Formal alternative:** explain / be responsible for
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Account for" (explain/be the cause) also means "to make up a share." Both senses are useful in data talk; context decides.

#### make up for
- **Meaning:** to compensate for something.
- **Examples:**
  - We'll make up for the delay.
  - This speed makes up for the extra cost.
  - How can we make up for the downtime?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** We lost a day to the outage.
  - **Lead:** Let's make up for it with a focused sprint.
- **Formal alternative:** compensate for / offset
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Make up for" needs "for." Don't say "make up the delay." Say "make up for the delay."

#### patch up
- **Meaning:** to fix something temporarily or roughly.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's patch it up for now.
  - We patched up the script.
  - It's a quick patch-up, not a real fix.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The job keeps failing.
  - **Anna:** Patch it up so the demo works, then fix it properly.
- **Formal alternative:** repair temporarily
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Patch up" suggests a temporary fix. For a proper solution, say "fix" or "resolve," not "patch up."

#### smooth over
- **Meaning:** to make a problem or disagreement less serious.
- **Examples:**
  - She smoothed over the tension.
  - Let's smooth things over with the client.
  - He smoothed over the mistake.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The client is upset about the delay.
  - **Lead:** I'll call them and smooth things over.
- **Formal alternative:** ease / defuse
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Smooth over" is about relationships or feelings, not technical fixes. For code, use "iron out" or "fix."

#### head off
- **Meaning:** to prevent a problem before it happens.
- **Examples:**
  - We headed off the outage.
  - Let's head off any confusion early.
  - Good monitoring heads off issues.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Disk usage is rising fast.
  - **Anna:** Add an alert to head off a crash.
- **Formal alternative:** prevent / avert
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Head off" (prevent) is proactive. Don't confuse with "head out" (leave). Use it when you stop trouble in advance.

#### ward off
- **Meaning:** to keep a problem or danger away.
- **Examples:**
  - Backups ward off data loss.
  - We warded off the attack.
  - Good tests ward off regressions.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** How do we protect against bad input?
  - **Lead:** Strong validation wards off most issues.
- **Formal alternative:** prevent / guard against
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Slightly formal. Fine in writing about security or risk. Don't overuse in casual chat.

#### fall through
- **Meaning:** (of a plan) to fail to happen.
- **Examples:**
  - The deal fell through.
  - Our plan fell through.
  - The partnership fell through.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Are we still getting the extra data?
  - **Lead:** No, that arrangement fell through. We'll use what we have.
- **Formal alternative:** collapse / fail
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Fall through" (a plan fails) vs "follow through" (complete a plan). Opposite meanings; don't swap them.

#### break down
- **Meaning:** (of a system) to stop working.
- **Examples:**
  - The pipeline broke down overnight.
  - The server broke down again.
  - Communication broke down.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The nightly job broke down at 2am.
  - **Anna:** Check the logs; probably out of memory.
- **Formal alternative:** fail / stop working
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Break down" (stop working) also means "split into parts" (Chapter 2). In problem context, it means failure.

#### act up
- **Meaning:** to behave badly or work unreliably.
- **Examples:**
  - The server is acting up.
  - My laptop is acting up today.
  - The API keeps acting up.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The dashboard is acting up — it loads slowly and freezes.
  - **Ops:** I'll restart the service.
- **Formal alternative:** malfunction / behave erratically
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Informal but very useful for flaky systems. In a formal report, say "the system is behaving unreliably" instead.

#### give up on
- **Meaning:** to stop trying with something or someone.
- **Examples:**
  - Don't give up on the model yet.
  - We gave up on that approach.
  - I nearly gave up on the bug.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I've tried five fixes and nothing works.
  - **Anna:** Don't give up on it; let's pair for 30 minutes.
- **Formal alternative:** abandon
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Give up on" (stop trying) needs "on" for a target. "Give up" alone means to quit generally.

#### back out
- **Meaning:** to withdraw from a commitment.
- **Examples:**
  - The vendor backed out.
  - Don't back out now.
  - She backed out of the plan.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Is the partner still helping with data?
  - **Lead:** No, they backed out last week.
- **Formal alternative:** withdraw / pull out
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Back out" (withdraw) also means to reverse a change in code ("back out the commit"). Context decides.

#### bail out
- **Meaning:** to rescue someone from trouble, OR to quit suddenly.
- **Examples:**
  - He bailed us out with a quick fix.
  - The script bailed out with an error.
  - Don't rely on someone to bail you out.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I was stuck all morning, but Ravi bailed me out.
  - **Anna:** Great teamwork.
- **Formal alternative:** rescue / assist (or exit, for code)
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Two senses: rescue a person, or a program exiting on error. Be clear which you mean.

#### step in
- **Meaning:** to get involved to help or take control.
- **Examples:**
  - The manager stepped in to help.
  - Can you step in for me?
  - She stepped in during the crisis.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The situation was getting tense.
  - **Lead:** That's when I stepped in and reset priorities.
- **Formal alternative:** intervene / assist
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Step in" (help/intervene) vs "step up" (take more responsibility). Slightly different; "step in" is often for a specific moment.

#### escalate to
- **Meaning:** to raise an issue to a higher level.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's escalate to the manager.
  - I escalated the ticket to support.
  - Escalate it if it's not fixed today.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** IT hasn't fixed the access issue in three days.
  - **Lead:** Escalate it to their team lead.
- **Formal alternative:** raise / refer upward
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Escalate to" (a person/level), not "escalate on." Also, escalation is normal in Europe and not seen as complaining; use it when needed.

#### look after
- **Meaning:** to take care of or be responsible for something.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll look after the deployment.
  - She looks after the data pipeline.
  - Can you look after the servers?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Who owns the monitoring?
  - **You:** I look after that. I'll add the new alerts.
- **Formal alternative:** manage / be responsible for
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Look after" (take care of) vs "look into" (investigate). "Look after" means ongoing responsibility.

#### track down
- **Meaning:** to find something after searching hard.
- **Examples:**
  - I tracked down the bug.
  - Let's track down the missing file.
  - She tracked down the root cause.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** After two hours, I tracked down the error to a null value.
  - **Anna:** Nice detective work.
- **Formal alternative:** locate / identify
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Track down" implies effort and searching. For an easy find, just say "found."

#### pin down
- **Meaning:** to identify something exactly.
- **Examples:**
  - It's hard to pin down the cause.
  - Let's pin down the requirements.
  - I can't pin down when it started.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The error is intermittent, so it's hard to pin down.
  - **Lead:** Add timestamps to the logs.
- **Formal alternative:** determine exactly / specify
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Pin down" means to make something exact and certain. Use it for vague problems or loose requirements.

#### sort through
- **Meaning:** to examine many things to find or organise them.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me sort through the logs.
  - We sorted through the tickets.
  - She sorted through the data.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** There are thousands of log lines.
  - **Anna:** Sort through them and find the errors first.
- **Formal alternative:** review / examine
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Sort through" (examine many) vs "sort out" (solve). Sorting through is the searching step.

#### clear up
- **Meaning:** to resolve confusion or a problem.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's clear up the confusion.
  - That clears up my doubt.
  - We cleared up the misunderstanding.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I wasn't sure which dataset to use.
  - **Anna:** Let me clear that up — always use the cleaned version.
- **Formal alternative:** resolve / clarify
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Clear up" (resolve confusion) vs "clean up" (tidy data). Different verbs, different meanings.

#### own up to
- **Meaning:** to admit a mistake.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll own up to my error.
  - She owned up to the bug.
  - It's better to own up early.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I pushed the wrong config, and that caused the outage.
  - **Lead:** Thanks for owning up to it. Let's fix it together.
- **Formal alternative:** admit / take responsibility
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Owning up early is respected in European offices; it's not weakness. Don't hide mistakes; "own up to it" builds trust.

#### sort out (a problem)
- **Meaning:** to fix or resolve a problem.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll sort out the login issue.
  - Let's sort this out today.
  - She sorted out the whole mess.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The permissions are broken again.
  - **Anna:** I'll sort it out this morning.
- **Formal alternative:** resolve / fix
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Very common and natural. Avoid the heavy "do the needful to resolve"; "I'll sort it out" is enough.

#### throw off
- **Meaning:** to cause a result or plan to be wrong.
- **Examples:**
  - One outlier threw off the average.
  - The bug threw off the numbers.
  - A late start threw off the schedule.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The metric looks too high.
  - **Anna:** A few duplicate rows probably threw it off.
- **Formal alternative:** distort / skew
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Throw off" (distort a result) is figurative. Don't confuse with "throw out" (discard).

#### weed out
- **Meaning:** to remove unwanted or bad items.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's weed out the bad records.
  - We weeded out the weak candidates.
  - The filter weeds out spam.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Half the rows have missing values.
  - **Anna:** Weed those out before training.
- **Formal alternative:** remove / eliminate
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Weed out" (remove bad ones from a group) vs "filter out" (remove by a rule). They overlap but "weed out" implies removing the undesirable.
