# Chapter 25: Meeting Types — Complete Playbooks

Different meetings need different language. This chapter gives you a ready playbook for each common meeting type, with scripts you can use straight away. Data-science examples are marked **[DS]**.

---

## 1. The daily stand-up (data team)

A stand-up is a short daily meeting (10–15 min) where each person shares three things. Keep it fast.

**The three-part structure:**
1. What I did yesterday.
2. What I'm doing today.
3. Any blockers.

**[DS] Example script:**
> "Yesterday I finished cleaning the churn dataset and started feature engineering. Today I'll train the first model and check its accuracy. One blocker: I'm still waiting on access to the payments table — could someone help unblock that?"

**Useful stand-up phrases:**
- "No blockers from me."
- "I'm blocked on [X] — I need help from [person]."
- "Carrying over from yesterday: still working on the pipeline fix."
- "Quick one today: mostly meetings, but I'll review Priya's code."
- "Let's take that offline — it's just the two of us who need it."

**Tip:** Keep it to your three points. Detailed problem-solving happens after the stand-up, not during it.

## 2. Sprint planning

Sprint planning is where the team decides what to work on for the next one or two weeks.

**Useful phrases:**
- "What should we prioritise this sprint?"
- "How many story points is this? / How big is this task?"
- "I think that's too much for one sprint — let's move some to the next one."
- "This depends on the data work being done first."
- "Let's pull this into the sprint and drop the lower-priority item."
- "I can commit to the model work, but not the dashboard too."

## 3. Sprint retrospective ("retro")

A retro is where the team reflects on what went well and what to improve.

**Common structure — three questions:**
- What went well?
- What didn't go well?
- What will we do differently?

**Useful phrases:**
- "What went well this sprint was the fast turnaround on data fixes."
- "One thing that didn't go so well: we underestimated the cleaning effort."
- "For next time, I'd suggest we add a buffer for data issues."
- "I want to give credit to Priya for unblocking us quickly."
- "Let's turn that into an action so it doesn't happen again."

**Tone note:** Retros should be blameless — focus on the process, not the person. Say "the estimate was off," not "you got the estimate wrong."

## 4. One-on-one with your manager

The one-on-one is your regular private meeting with your manager. Use it well — it's your chance to raise problems, get feedback, and grow.

**Raising a problem:**
- "I wanted to flag something that's been on my mind."
- "I'm finding [X] challenging — can we talk through it?"
- "I feel a bit stretched across three projects. Can we prioritise together?"

**Asking for feedback:**
- "How do you think the presentation went? Any feedback for me?"
- "What's one thing I could do better?"
- "Where do you see me growing over the next six months?"

**Discussing career growth:**
- "I'd like to work toward a senior role. What would I need to demonstrate?"
- "I'm keen to take on more ownership — is there a project I could lead?"

**Salary conversations (approach calmly, with evidence):**
- "I'd like to discuss my compensation. Based on my research, the market range for this role is [X]."
- "Given my contributions this year — [list two or three] — I'd like to revisit my salary."
- "Could we talk about a path to a raise, and what I'd need to achieve?"

## 5. Client / stakeholder meetings

Stakeholders care about outcomes and timelines, not technical detail. Manage expectations honestly.

**Managing expectations [DS]:**
- "Realistically, the model will be production-ready by the end of Q3, not sooner."
- "I want to be honest about the uncertainty — this is an estimate, not a promise."
- "We can deliver a first version quickly, but a robust one takes longer. Which do you need?"
- "The data quality will affect the timeline. Let me explain the risk."

**Building trust:**
- "Let me set out what's realistic and what's ambitious."
- "I'd rather under-promise and over-deliver."
- "Here's what we know, and here's what we're still figuring out."

**Tip:** Never promise a specific accuracy number before you've built the model. Say "our aim is X, and we'll know more after testing."

## 6. Brainstorming sessions

Brainstorming is about generating ideas freely, before judging them.

**Useful phrases:**
- "Let's not evaluate ideas yet — just get everything on the table."
- "There are no bad ideas at this stage."
- "Building on [Name]'s idea..."
- "Let me throw out a wild one..."
- "Let's group these and then pick the most promising."

**Tip:** Separate idea-generation from idea-selection. Judging too early kills creativity.

## 7. All-hands / large meetings

An all-hands is a big company meeting, sometimes with hundreds of people. Asking a question in front of everyone feels scary — here's how.

**Asking a question in a large meeting:**
- "Thanks for the update. I had a quick question about the data strategy."
- "Could you say a bit more about how this affects the analytics team?"
- "One question from the data side:..."

**Tips for large meetings:**
- Keep your question short and clear — everyone's listening.
- State your name and team first if people don't know you: "Hi, I'm [Name] from data science."
- If nervous, type your question in the chat instead — many all-hands take chat questions.
- It's fine to say "Thanks, that answers it" and sit down. You don't need a follow-up.

## Quick reference: which meeting, which mindset

| Meeting | Your goal | Key phrase |
|---|---|---|
| Stand-up | Share status fast | "Yesterday / today / blockers" |
| Sprint planning | Commit to realistic work | "Let's prioritise..." |
| Retro | Improve the process | "What could we do differently?" |
| One-on-one | Grow and raise issues | "I wanted to flag..." |
| Stakeholder | Manage expectations | "Realistically, by..." |
| Brainstorm | Generate freely | "No bad ideas yet" |
| All-hands | Ask clearly, briefly | "One quick question:..." |

## Common Indian-English pitfalls across meeting types

- **"I am having doubt on the data."** → "I have a question about the data." (See Chapter 14.)
- **"Yesterday I was doing the cleaning."** → "Yesterday I cleaned the data." (Simple past — see Chapter 36.)
- **"Kindly prioritise my request."** → "Could you prioritise this?"
- **"Please revert on the timeline."** → "Let me know about the timeline."
- **"I will update you the status."** → "I'll update you on the status." (Add "on.")
