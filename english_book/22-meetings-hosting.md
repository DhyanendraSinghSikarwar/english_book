# Chapter 22: Opening & Hosting a Meeting

When you host a meeting, you set the tone and keep it on track. This chapter gives you the language to open, welcome people, assign roles, and set ground rules — for both online and in-room meetings.

**Format:** exact sentences, when to use them, and online vs offline variations.

---

## 1. Online-specific opening (the first two minutes)

Most European meetings are on video. The start is often messy — people joining, audio issues. Handle it smoothly.

**Waiting for people to join:**
- "Let's give it two more minutes for people to join."
- "I see a few people are still joining — we'll start at [time] sharp."
- "While we wait, how's everyone's week going?"

**Checking audio and video:**
- "Can everyone hear me okay?"
- "You're on mute, Anna." (very common — say it kindly)
- "Could you turn your camera on for the introductions? No worries if not."
- "I can't quite hear you — could you check your mic?"
- "Let me share my screen. Can you all see the slides?"

**Tone note:** Keep it light and friendly. Audio problems happen to everyone. "You're on mute" is said a hundred times a day in Europe — no one minds.

## 2. Starting the meeting

**Opening lines:**
- "Right, let's get started. Thanks, everyone, for joining."
- "Okay, I think we're all here. Let's dive in."
- "Thanks for making the time. The purpose of today's meeting is to decide on the pilot."
- "Let's kick off. We've got 30 minutes, and here's what we'll cover..."

**Stating the goal (do this every time):**
- "The purpose of today's meeting is to agree on the next steps for the churn model."
- "By the end of this, I'd like us to have a clear decision on X."
- "The goal is simple: align on who does what by Friday."

**Walking through the agenda:**
- "We'll cover three things: the update, the findings, and the decision."
- "I'll keep us to time — feel free to keep me honest on that."

## 3. Assigning roles

For longer meetings, assign a note-taker and sometimes a timekeeper.

- "Marco has kindly agreed to take the minutes today."
- "Could someone volunteer to take notes? Thank you, Sofia."
- "I'll keep an eye on time so we finish on schedule."
- "Let's have Anna watch the chat for questions."

## 4. Setting ground rules

Ground rules keep meetings efficient and fair.

- "Let's keep each update to two minutes so we finish on time."
- "One conversation at a time, please — it's hard to follow otherwise online."
- "Let's hold detailed questions until the end of each section."
- "If we can't decide something today, we'll park it and follow up — let's not get stuck."
- "Please drop questions in the chat as we go, and I'll pick them up."

**For decision meetings:**
- "The aim today is a decision, not a discussion of every detail — let's keep that in mind."

## 5. In-room (offline) specifics

When you're all in a room together, a few things differ.

**Room etiquette:**
- "Grab a seat, everyone. We'll start in a minute."
- "Can everyone at the back see the screen okay?"
- "Let's make sure the people dialling in can hear us — speak up toward the mic."

**Whiteboard language:**
- "Let me sketch this out on the board."
- "Bear with me while I draw the flow."
- "So this box is the data source, and this arrow is the pipeline..."
- "Let's map this out together — shout out the steps."

**Hybrid meetings (some in-room, some remote):**
- "For those joining online, I'll describe what's on the whiteboard."
- "Let's make sure our remote colleagues get a chance to speak too."
- "[Remote person], I want to check in with you — anything to add?"

**Tip for hybrid:** Remote people are easily forgotten. As host, deliberately bring them in by name.

## 6. Introductions (when people don't know each other)

- "Before we start, let's do a quick round of introductions. Just your name and role, keep it short."
- "For those who haven't met, this is Priya from the data team."
- "Let's go around quickly — say your name and one line on what you do."

**Tip:** Keep intro rounds short and time-boxed, or they eat the whole meeting. "One line each" is your friend.

## 7. Handling early technical difficulties

- "We seem to have a connection issue — bear with me for a moment."
- "I'll stop sharing and try again; the screen froze."
- "If you can't hear me, please drop a note in the chat."
- "Let's give [Name] a moment to sort out their audio, then we'll begin."

---

## A full hosting opening — example script

> "Right, let's get started — thanks, everyone, for joining. Can you all hear me okay? Great.
>
> The purpose of today's meeting is to decide whether we move the churn model into a pilot. We've got 30 minutes.
>
> Here's the plan: I'll give a quick update, then Priya will walk through the findings, and we'll leave 10 minutes for the decision and next steps. Marco's kindly taking the minutes.
>
> One ask: let's keep updates short so we protect time for the decision. Sound good? Let's dive in."

---

## Common Indian-English pitfalls when hosting

- **"Let us start the meeting."** → "Let's get started." (More natural.)
- **"I would like to welcome you all to this meeting."** (too formal) → "Thanks, everyone, for joining."
- **"Kindly introduce yourselves."** → "Let's do a quick round of introductions."
- **"Shall we begin?"** is fine, but "Let's get started" is warmer and more common.
- **"Please mute your mikes."** → "Please mute if you're not speaking." (And it's "mics," not "mikes," in writing.)

---

**Chapter 22 summary.** As host, open by welcoming people, stating the goal, walking the agenda, assigning a note-taker, and setting one or two ground rules. Handle audio hiccups lightly online, use the whiteboard well offline, and always bring remote colleagues into the conversation by name. A confident, friendly opening makes the whole meeting run better.
