# Chapter 32: Corporate Jargon Decoder

Every workplace is full of jargon — phrases that mean something specific but sound strange at first. You'll hear these in meetings and emails. This chapter decodes 100 common ones so you understand them, know when to use them, and know when they sound fake.

**Format:** the phrase → what it means. Then guidance on using jargon wisely, with country notes.

---

## Meeting and communication jargon

- **take it offline** → discuss it separately, after the meeting.
- **circle back** → return to a topic later.
- **touch base** → check in briefly with someone.
- **loop (someone) in** → include them in the conversation.
- **ping me** → send me a quick message.
- **give me a shout / give me a heads up** → let me know / warn me in advance.
- **sync (up)** → have a quick meeting to align.
- **align** → get everyone to agree on the same thing.
- **on the same page** → in agreement / sharing understanding.
- **socialise an idea** → share an idea to get feedback before deciding.
- **park it** → set a topic aside for later.
- **table it** → (UK) discuss it; (US) postpone it — meanings differ, so be careful!
- **double-click on that** → go into more detail on something.
- **drill down** → look at the detail beneath a summary.
- **zoom out** → look at the bigger picture.

## Time and deadline jargon

- **EOD** → end of day.
- **COB** → close of business (similar to EOD).
- **EOW** → end of week.
- **ASAP** → as soon as possible.
- **by the close of play (COP)** → (UK) by end of the working day.
- **tight timeline** → not much time.
- **crunch time** → a period of intense pressure near a deadline.
- **slip (the timeline)** → fall behind schedule.
- **fast-track** → speed something up.
- **on the back burner** → low priority for now.

## Priority and status jargon

- **low-hanging fruit** → easy wins that take little effort.
- **quick win** → a fast, visible success.
- **move the needle** → make a real, measurable difference.
- **boil the ocean** → try to do too much at once (a warning against this).
- **bandwidth** → time/capacity to take on work.
- **at capacity** → fully busy, can't take more.
- **firefighting** → dealing with urgent problems instead of planned work.
- **blocker** → something stopping progress.
- **critical path** → the sequence of tasks that determines the deadline.
- **table stakes** → the minimum needed just to compete.
- **north star** → the guiding goal or metric.
- **MVP** → Minimum Viable Product: the simplest version worth releasing.

## Analysis and decision jargon

- **deep dive** → a detailed investigation.
- **ballpark figure** → a rough estimate.
- **back of the envelope** → a quick, rough calculation.
- **gut feel** → intuition, not data.
- **data-driven** → based on data, not opinion.
- **sanity check** → a quick check that something is reasonable.
- **due diligence** → careful checking before a decision.
- **red flag** → a warning sign.
- **elephant in the room** → an obvious problem nobody is mentioning.
- **apples to apples** → a fair, like-for-like comparison.
- **directionally correct** → roughly right, even if not exact.
- **rule of thumb** → a rough, practical guideline.

## Strategy and business jargon

- **synergy** → benefits from things working together (often overused).
- **value-add** → the extra value something provides.
- **win-win** → good for both sides.
- **game changer** → something that transforms the situation.
- **secret sauce** → the special thing that makes you succeed.
- **scale (up)** → grow to handle more.
- **pivot** → change strategy/direction.
- **disrupt** → change an industry dramatically.
- **best practice** → the accepted best way to do something.
- **low-hanging fruit** (again, very common).
- **moving parts** → the many components involved.
- **moonshot** → a very ambitious, risky goal.
- **land and expand** → win a small deal, then grow it.
- **future-proof** → make it last as things change.

## People and organisation jargon

- **stakeholder** → anyone with an interest in the project.
- **decision-maker** → the person who says yes or no.
- **point person / POC (point of contact)** → the go-to person.
- **DRI (Directly Responsible Individual)** → the one owner of a task.
- **cross-functional** → involving several teams.
- **silo** → a team/system that doesn't share with others (usually bad).
- **headcount** → number of employees.
- **onboarding** → getting a new hire (or customer) started.
- **offboarding** → the process when someone leaves.
- **upskill / reskill** → learn new skills.
- **wear many hats** → do many different roles.
- **dogfooding** → using your own product internally to test it.

## Action jargon

- **action (something)** → do it. "I'll action that." (very common in the UK)
- **circle up** → gather to discuss.
- **reach out** → contact someone.
- **follow up** → check back on something.
- **flag** → raise attention to something.
- **escalate** → raise to a higher level.
- **unpack** → break down and examine.
- **operationalise** → turn a plan into day-to-day practice.
- **execute** → carry out the plan.
- **iterate** → improve step by step.
- **ship (it)** → release/deliver it.
- **spin up** → quickly start something.
- **noodle on it** → think about it casually.
- **run it up the flagpole** → test an idea with others/leadership.

## Abbreviations you'll see written

- **FYI** → for your information.
- **FYA** → for your action / for your attention.
- **TL;DR** → too long; didn't read (a short summary).
- **OOO** → out of office.
- **WFH** → work from home.
- **1:1** → a one-on-one meeting.
- **AOB** → any other business (end of a meeting agenda).
- **TBD** → to be decided/determined.
- **TBC** → to be confirmed.
- **WIP** → work in progress.
- **ETA** → estimated time of arrival (when something will be ready).
- **PTO** → paid time off (US) / annual leave (UK).
- **NDA** → non-disclosure agreement.
- **SLA** → service level agreement (a promise on response/quality).
- **ROI, KPI, MRR, CAC, LTV** → see Chapters 30–31.

## When to use jargon vs when it sounds fake

Jargon can help you fit in and communicate quickly with people who share it. But too much sounds hollow, and some people dislike it. Follow these rules:

- **Use jargon that's genuinely useful** — "blocker," "EOD," "stakeholder," "take it offline" are clear and save time.
- **Avoid stacking buzzwords** — "Let's synergise our value-add to move the needle on our north star" is meaningless noise. It makes you sound like you're hiding a lack of substance.
- **Never use jargon you don't understand** — if you're unsure what "table it" means in your office (it differs between UK and US!), ask.
- **When in doubt, say it plainly** — "let's discuss this after the meeting" is always fine instead of "let's take this offline."
- **Match your audience** — use more plain language with non-technical people and clients.

## Jargon tolerance by country

- **UK / Ireland:** high tolerance for jargon and idioms, but also love plain speaking and irony. "Action that by COP" is normal.
- **Netherlands / Nordics:** prefer direct, plain language. Heavy jargon can seem like waffle or hiding. Say what you mean simply.
- **Germany:** value precision and clarity. Vague buzzwords are not appreciated; concrete language is.
- **Global tech companies:** more American-style jargon (ping, ship, sync, DRI). This is common across borders in tech.

**General advice:** Learn to *understand* all the jargon (so you're never lost in a meeting), but *use* only the clear, useful pieces. Plain, direct English is respected everywhere in Europe — jargon is optional seasoning, not the meal.

## Common Indian-English notes on jargon

- Some Indian offices use "revert," "do the needful," "prepone," and "same" (see Chapter 14) — these are Indian-English, not international jargon. Replace them.
- "Updation," "prepone," "avail (the offer)" are not used internationally — see Chapter 14.
- "Kindly revert on this at the earliest" is not corporate jargon; it's Indian-English formality. Say "let me know as soon as you can."
