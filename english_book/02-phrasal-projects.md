# Chapter 2: Projects, Tasks & Deadlines

Projects run on clear communication about who does what and by when. This chapter covers the phrasal verbs you need to accept work, track progress, manage delays, and plan tasks. These phrases appear every day in stand-ups, sprint planning, and status updates.

---

#### take on
- **Meaning:** to accept a task or responsibility.
- **Examples:**
  - I can take on the new dashboard.
  - She took on too much this sprint.
  - Who wants to take this on?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** We need someone for the data cleaning task.
  - **You:** I'll take it on. I have some capacity this week.
- **Formal alternative:** accept / undertake
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "I will take up this task on me." Just "I'll take it on" or "I'll take it up."

#### carry out
- **Meaning:** to do or perform a task.
- **Examples:**
  - We carried out the tests overnight.
  - Please carry out the migration carefully.
  - The team carried out a full review.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I've carried out the validation on all three datasets.
  - **Anna:** Any surprises?
- **Formal alternative:** perform / execute
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Avoid "do the carrying out of tests." Just "carry out the tests."

#### work on
- **Meaning:** to spend effort on a task.
- **Examples:**
  - I'm working on the feature engineering.
  - She's been working on this bug all day.
  - Let's work on the report together.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** What are you on today?
  - **You:** I'm working on the model retraining.
- **Formal alternative:** develop / handle
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "I am doing work on it" or "working over it." Correct is "working on it."

#### deal with
- **Meaning:** to handle a task or problem.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll deal with the client emails.
  - Can you deal with the failed job?
  - We dealt with the outage quickly.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The queue is backing up.
  - **Tom:** I'll deal with it now.
- **Formal alternative:** handle / manage
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Deal with" not "deal in" for tasks. "Deal in" means to trade goods.

#### get on with
- **Meaning:** to continue doing a task without delay.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's get on with the work.
  - I'll get on with the analysis now.
  - Stop talking and get on with it.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Any blockers?
  - **You:** No, I'll get on with the pipeline right away.
- **Formal alternative:** proceed / continue
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Get on with" (a task) is different from "get along with" (a person). Don't mix them.

#### catch up
- **Meaning:** to reach the same level after falling behind, OR to exchange updates.
- **Examples:**
  - I need to catch up on my emails.
  - Let's catch up tomorrow.
  - She caught up on the missed work.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I was on leave, so I need to catch up on the sprint tickets.
  - **Anna:** Let's catch up at 3 and I'll fill you in.
- **Formal alternative:** get up to date / meet
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "I will catch up you." It's "catch up with you."

#### keep up
- **Meaning:** to stay at the same pace; not fall behind.
- **Examples:**
  - It's hard to keep up with the changes.
  - Keep up the good work.
  - Can you keep up this pace?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** The requirements keep changing.
  - **You:** I'm struggling to keep up. Can we freeze the scope?
- **Formal alternative:** maintain pace / stay current
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Keep up with" (stay level) vs "keep up" (maintain). Add "with" when comparing to something.

#### fall behind
- **Meaning:** to be slower than the schedule.
- **Examples:**
  - We fell behind on testing.
  - Don't let the project fall behind.
  - I've fallen behind this week.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I've fallen behind because of the data issues.
  - **Lead:** Thanks for flagging. Let's re-plan.
- **Formal alternative:** lag / be delayed
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Say "fall behind on/with the schedule," not "fall back on the schedule." "Fall back on" means to rely on as backup.

#### hand over
- **Meaning:** to give responsibility or work to someone else.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll hand over my tasks before leave.
  - She handed over the project cleanly.
  - Let's do a proper handover.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I'm off next week, so I'll hand over the on-call duties to Ravi.
  - **Ravi:** Please share the runbook.
- **Formal alternative:** transfer / delegate
- **Indian-English pitfall:** The noun is "handover" (one word). Don't write "hand over" as a noun.

#### hand in
- **Meaning:** to submit work to someone in charge.
- **Examples:**
  - Please hand in your report by Friday.
  - I handed in the draft yesterday.
  - When do we hand this in?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Deadline for the analysis?
  - **You:** I'll hand it in by end of day Thursday.
- **Formal alternative:** submit
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Hand in" (submit) vs "hand over" (transfer duty). Submitting a document is "hand in."

#### take over
- **Meaning:** to take control of a task from someone.
- **Examples:**
  - Can you take over while I'm out?
  - She took over the project last month.
  - I'll take over the deployment.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I have a conflict. Can someone take over the client call?
  - **Anna:** I'll take over.
- **Formal alternative:** assume control / succeed
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Take over" (control) vs "takeover" (noun, one word). Also don't confuse with "take on" (accept new work).

#### set up
- **Meaning:** to arrange, install, or create something.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's set up a meeting.
  - I set up the environment yesterday.
  - Can you set up the database?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I'll set up the staging server this morning.
  - **Lead:** Great, then we can test.
- **Formal alternative:** arrange / configure / establish
- **Indian-English pitfall:** The noun is "setup" (one word); the verb is "set up" (two words). Keep them separate.

#### roll out
- **Meaning:** to release or launch something gradually.
- **Examples:**
  - We'll roll out the feature next week.
  - The update was rolled out to all users.
  - Let's roll out in phases.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The model passed tests. Can we roll it out to 10% of traffic first?
  - **Lead:** Yes, start small.
- **Formal alternative:** launch / deploy
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Roll out" (launch) vs "roll back" (undo). One goes forward, one goes back.

#### phase out
- **Meaning:** to stop using something gradually.
- **Examples:**
  - We're phasing out the old system.
  - The legacy model was phased out.
  - Let's phase out manual reports.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The v1 API is unstable. Shall we phase it out over the quarter?
  - **Lead:** Yes, warn users first.
- **Formal alternative:** discontinue / retire
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Phase out" (stop gradually) vs "phase in" (introduce gradually). Opposite directions.

#### scale up / scale down
- **Meaning:** scale up = increase size/capacity; scale down = reduce.
- **Examples:**
  - We need to scale up the cluster.
  - Let's scale down the scope for now.
  - The team scaled up for the launch.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Traffic tripled overnight. We should scale up the servers.
  - **Ops:** Adding nodes now.
- **Formal alternative:** expand (up) / reduce (down)
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Scale up" (add resources) vs "scale out" (add more machines). In infra, "up" often means bigger machine, "out" means more machines. Know the difference.

#### ramp up
- **Meaning:** to increase effort, speed, or volume over time.
- **Examples:**
  - We're ramping up hiring.
  - Let's ramp up testing before launch.
  - Production ramped up quickly.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** The deadline moved up.
  - **You:** Then we need to ramp up the annotation work now.
- **Formal alternative:** increase / accelerate
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Ramp up" is gradual increase. Don't use it for a one-time jump; use "increase" there.

#### pull off
- **Meaning:** to succeed at something difficult.
- **Examples:**
  - We pulled off the launch on time.
  - Can we pull this off in two days?
  - She pulled off a great demo.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Everything worked and the client was happy.
  - **Lead:** Well done, you pulled it off.
- **Formal alternative:** achieve / accomplish
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Pull off" (succeed) vs "pull out" (withdraw). Very different meanings.

#### get through
- **Meaning:** to finish an amount of work, or to survive a hard period.
- **Examples:**
  - I got through the backlog today.
  - We got through the busy week.
  - Let's get through these tickets.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I got through half the review comments; I'll finish tomorrow.
  - **Anna:** No rush.
- **Formal alternative:** complete / finish
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Get through" (finish) vs "go through" (review). Getting through means completing.

#### put together
- **Meaning:** to create or assemble something.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll put together a quick report.
  - She put together the slides overnight.
  - Let's put together a plan.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Can we get a summary for the client?
  - **You:** Yes, I'll put together a one-pager by noon.
- **Formal alternative:** compile / prepare
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "make together." Correct is "put together."

#### break down
- **Meaning:** to split something into smaller parts.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's break down the task into steps.
  - Break the cost down by team.
  - The plan is broken down into phases.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The epic is too big. Let me break it down into three tickets.
  - **Lead:** Good idea.
- **Formal alternative:** divide / itemise
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Break down" (split) also means "stop working" (a machine). Context decides; in planning it means split into parts.

#### figure out
- **Meaning:** to find the answer or understand something.
- **Examples:**
  - I need to figure out why it fails.
  - We figured out the root cause.
  - Can you figure out the pattern?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The accuracy dropped and I can't figure out why yet.
  - **Anna:** Let's pair on it.
- **Formal alternative:** determine / work out
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "figure out about it." Just "figure it out."

#### work out
- **Meaning:** to solve a problem or calculate something.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's work out the timeline.
  - I worked out the cost per query.
  - It'll work out fine in the end.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** We need to work out how many GPUs the job needs.
  - **Ops:** I'll calculate it.
- **Formal alternative:** calculate / resolve
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Work out" (solve) vs "workout" (exercise). Also don't confuse with "figure out"; they overlap but "work out" often involves calculation.

#### map out
- **Meaning:** to plan something in detail.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's map out the next sprint.
  - She mapped out the whole process.
  - We need to map out the dependencies.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Before coding, let me map out the data flow on the whiteboard.
  - **Lead:** Yes, show the steps.
- **Formal alternative:** plan / outline
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Map out" (plan) is figurative. You don't need an actual map. Don't overexplain it.

#### lay out
- **Meaning:** to present or arrange something clearly.
- **Examples:**
  - Let me lay out the options.
  - The doc lays out the plan well.
  - She laid out the steps clearly.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I'll lay out the three approaches with pros and cons.
  - **Lead:** Perfect, that helps us decide.
- **Formal alternative:** present / arrange
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Lay out" (present) vs "layout" (noun, the design). Verb is two words.

#### draw up
- **Meaning:** to prepare a written plan or document.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's draw up a project plan.
  - I'll draw up the contract terms.
  - She drew up a schedule.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** We need a formal plan for the client.
  - **You:** I'll draw one up by tomorrow.
- **Formal alternative:** prepare / draft
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Draw up" (prepare a document) has nothing to do with drawing pictures. It means to write formally.

#### look into
- **Meaning:** to investigate something.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll look into the error.
  - Can you look into the delay?
  - We're looking into new tools.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The job failed silently.
  - **Anna:** I'll look into the logs and get back to you.
- **Formal alternative:** investigate / examine
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "look into about it." Just "look into it."

#### dig into
- **Meaning:** to investigate deeply.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's dig into the data.
  - I dug into the customer complaints.
  - We need to dig into why users churn.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The metric looks off. I'll dig into the raw events.
  - **Lead:** Good, find the source.
- **Formal alternative:** investigate thoroughly / analyse
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Dig into" is stronger than "look into." Use "dig into" when you mean deep, careful analysis.

#### follow through
- **Meaning:** to complete something you started or promised.
- **Examples:**
  - She always follows through on tasks.
  - We need to follow through on the plan.
  - Don't just plan; follow through.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** You promised the fix last week.
  - **You:** You're right, I didn't follow through. I'll finish it today.
- **Formal alternative:** complete / see through
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Follow through" (finish) vs "follow up" (check again). Following through means you complete your own commitment.

#### hold off
- **Meaning:** to delay or wait before doing something.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's hold off on the release.
  - Hold off until we have the data.
  - Can we hold off deciding?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** Should I deploy now?
  - **Lead:** Hold off until the tests are green.
- **Formal alternative:** delay / postpone
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Hold off" (wait) vs "hold on" (wait a moment / grip). "Hold off on doing X" means don't do it yet.

#### get around to
- **Meaning:** to finally do something you delayed.
- **Examples:**
  - I finally got around to the docs.
  - I haven't got around to it yet.
  - When will you get around to testing?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Any update on the documentation?
  - **You:** Not yet. I'll get around to it once the release is out.
- **Formal alternative:** find time for
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Note the extra "to": "get around to it," not "get around it." "Get around" (without to) means to avoid a problem.

#### crack on
- **Meaning:** (UK) to start working hard and keep going.
- **Examples:**
  - Let's crack on with the analysis.
  - We cracked on and finished early.
  - Time to crack on.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** Everyone clear on tasks?
  - **You:** Yes, let's crack on.
- **Formal alternative:** proceed / press on
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Common in UK/Ireland offices. Informal; avoid in written status reports.

#### knuckle down
- **Meaning:** to start working seriously and with focus.
- **Examples:**
  - I need to knuckle down and finish.
  - Let's knuckle down before the deadline.
  - She knuckled down for the final week.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** The demo is Friday.
  - **Anna:** Then we knuckle down from now.
- **Formal alternative:** focus / concentrate
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Informal but useful in speech. Don't confuse with "buckle down," which means the same thing.

#### wrap up (a task)
- **Meaning:** to finish a task or piece of work.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll wrap up the report tonight.
  - Let's wrap up this ticket.
  - She wrapped up the analysis early.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** How's the feature?
  - **You:** Almost done, I'll wrap it up by lunch.
- **Formal alternative:** complete / finalise
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Same phrase as in meetings; here it applies to tasks. Fine in speech and email.

#### farm out
- **Meaning:** to give work to another team or external people.
- **Examples:**
  - We farmed out the annotation.
  - Let's farm out the testing.
  - The labelling was farmed out.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** We don't have time to label all this.
  - **Lead:** Let's farm it out to the vendor.
- **Formal alternative:** outsource / delegate
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Informal. In formal writing use "outsource."

#### plough through / plow through
- **Meaning:** to work slowly and steadily through a lot of work.
- **Examples:**
  - I ploughed through the backlog.
  - We have to plough through the reviews.
  - She ploughed through the data.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** There are 200 review comments.
  - **Anna:** Let's just plough through them together.
- **Formal alternative:** work steadily through
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Spelling differs: "plough" (UK) vs "plow" (US). Pick one and stay consistent.

#### line up
- **Meaning:** to arrange or organise things in advance.
- **Examples:**
  - I've lined up three interviews.
  - Let's line up the tasks for next week.
  - We have a lot lined up.
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **Lead:** What's planned for the sprint?
  - **You:** I've lined up the data prep, then training, then eval.
- **Formal alternative:** arrange / schedule
- **Indian-English pitfall:** "Line up" (arrange) vs "queue up" (wait in line). In planning it means to organise in order.

#### free up
- **Meaning:** to make time or resources available.
- **Examples:**
  - I'll free up some time tomorrow.
  - This automation frees up two hours.
  - Can you free up the server?
- **Mini-scenario:**
  - **You:** I'm blocked on the review.
  - **Lead:** I'll free up 30 minutes this afternoon to help.
- **Formal alternative:** make available / release
- **Indian-English pitfall:** Don't say "free up my time from this." Just "free up time" or "free up some capacity."
