## Appendix A: Master Vocabulary List (A–Z)

A quick-glance list of high-value professional words with simple meanings. (For fuller upgrades, see Chapter 12.)

**A** — accomplish (complete successfully) · acknowledge (accept/recognise) · align (agree/match) · allocate (give out) · articulate (express clearly) · assess (judge) · attribute (say what caused) · authorise (give permission)

**B** — bandwidth (capacity/time) · benchmark (compare to a standard) · brief (give key info) · boost (increase)

**C** — clarify (make clear) · collaborate (work together) · commence (start) · consolidate (combine) · convey (get a message across) · coordinate (organise together) · counterpart (person in the same role elsewhere)

**D** — delegate (give a task to someone) · deliver (provide a result) · deploy (release) · determine (find out/decide) · diligence (careful checking) · discrepancy (difference/error)

**E** — elaborate (give detail) · eliminate (remove) · endorse (support publicly) · escalate (raise higher) · establish (set up/prove) · evaluate (judge value) · expedite (speed up)

**F** — facilitate (make easier/lead) · feasible (possible) · finalise (make final) · forecast (predict) · foster (encourage growth)

**G** — gauge (estimate) · govern (control with rules) · granular (detailed)

**H** — hedge (be cautious about certainty) · hierarchy (levels of rank) · hypothesise (suggest to test)

**I** — implement (put into action) · incorporate (include) · infer (work out from evidence) · initiate (begin) · integrate (combine systems) · iterate (improve step by step)

**J** — jurisdiction (area of authority) · justify (give reasons for)

**K** — KPI (key performance indicator) · kickoff (start of a project)

**L** — leverage (use to advantage) · liaise (work with another team) · liability (risk/obligation)

**M** — mandate (official instruction) · mediate (help two sides agree) · migrate (move data/systems) · mitigate (reduce a bad effect) · monitor (watch over time)

**N** — negotiate (discuss to agree) · notify (formally tell)

**O** — onboard (get started) · optimise (make best) · outline (give main points) · oversee (be responsible for)

**P** — pivot (change direction) · prioritise (order by importance) · proactive (acting ahead) · procure (buy/obtain) · proposal (suggested plan)

**Q** — quantify (measure in numbers) · quarter (three-month period)

**R** — rationale (the reasoning) · reconcile (make two things match) · refine (improve slightly) · reiterate (say again) · resolve (settle/fix) · roadmap (plan over time) · robust (strong/reliable)

**S** — scalable (able to grow) · scope (what's included) · scrutinise (examine closely) · stakeholder (interested party) · streamline (make efficient) · substantiate (back up with evidence) · synthesise (combine into a whole)

**T** — tangible (real/measurable) · trade-off (giving up one thing for another) · transparent (open/clear)

**U** — undertake (take on) · uphold (maintain/support) · utilise (use)

**V** — validate (confirm correct) · verify (check true) · viable (workable) · vet (check carefully)

**W** — workflow (sequence of work steps) · workstream (a strand of a project)

## Appendix C: Country Notes (English-speaking European workplaces)

### United Kingdom
- **Communication:** indirect, polite, lots of understatement (see Chapter 33 decoder). "Quite good" ≠ great.
- **Humour:** heavy irony and self-deprecation; sarcasm is common.
- **Etiquette:** queue politely; "please/thank you/sorry" used a lot; punctual with slight grace.
- **Language:** British spelling; "holiday" not "vacation"; "cheers" means thanks.

### Ireland
- **Communication:** friendly, warm, indirect like the UK but more relaxed and chatty.
- **Etiquette:** relationships and small talk matter; good humour valued.
- **Language:** British spelling; very informal first-name culture.

### Netherlands
- **Communication:** very direct — people say exactly what they think. Not rude; it's honesty. Disagreement is normal and expected.
- **Etiquette:** extremely punctual; flat hierarchy; excellent English everywhere.
- **Work-life:** strong boundaries; part-time and flexible work common; take your leave.
- **Tip:** Don't take blunt feedback personally. Give your honest view too.

### Germany
- **Communication:** direct and precise; value clarity, facts, and structure. Vague buzzwords disliked.
- **Etiquette:** very punctual (early = on time); more formal than NL — titles (Dr.) can matter; "Sie" (formal you) in some settings, though English-speaking tech is more relaxed.
- **Work-life:** clear separation of work and personal time; efficient meetings; thorough planning.
- **Tip:** Be well-prepared, on time, and precise. Say what you mean plainly.

### Nordics (Denmark, Sweden, Norway, Finland)
- **Communication:** direct but understated; consensus-driven; humble ("Law of Jante" — don't boast).
- **Etiquette:** very flat hierarchy; punctual; excellent English; trust-based.
- **Work-life:** strong balance; short working hours respected; generous parental leave.
- **Tip:** Avoid showing off; include everyone; decisions are often made by group consensus, which can feel slow but is respected.

## Appendix D: Data-Science Glossary in "Explain to Business" Language

For each term: the plain-English one-liner you can say to a non-technical stakeholder.

- **Accuracy** — "How often the model is right overall."
- **Precision** — "When the model says 'yes,' how often it's actually right." (Few false alarms.)
- **Recall** — "Of all the real cases, how many the model catches." (Few misses.)
- **Precision vs recall trade-off** — "We can catch more cases but with more false alarms, or fewer false alarms but miss some — we tune it to what matters to you."
- **False positive** — "A false alarm — we flagged someone who was actually fine."
- **False negative** — "A miss — we failed to flag someone we should have."
- **A/B test** — "We show version A to one group and version B to another, then see which does better — a fair experiment."
- **Baseline** — "The simple benchmark we compare against, to prove the model adds value."
- **Churn model** — "A model that predicts which customers are likely to leave, so we can act early."
- **Feature** — "An input the model uses — like a customer's age or how often they log in."
- **Feature engineering** — "Turning raw data into useful signals the model can learn from."
- **Overfitting** — "When a model memorises the past instead of learning general patterns — it looks great on old data but fails on new data."
- **Training data / test data** — "We teach the model on one set of data and test it on data it hasn't seen, to check it really works."
- **Confidence interval** — "The range the true value is likely to fall in — our honest margin of uncertainty."
- **Correlation** — "Two things that move together — but that doesn't prove one causes the other."
- **Causation** — "One thing actually causing another — harder to prove; usually needs an experiment."
- **Model drift** — "When a model gets worse over time because the world changed — so we retrain it regularly."
- **Pipeline** — "The automated flow that takes raw data and turns it into results, step by step."
- **Confusion matrix** — "A simple table showing what the model got right and wrong, by category."
- **Threshold** — "The cut-off we set for the model to say 'yes' — we can move it to catch more or fewer cases."
- **Segmentation** — "Splitting customers into meaningful groups so we can treat each differently."
- **Forecast** — "A data-based prediction of future numbers, like next quarter's demand."
- **Baseline vs uplift** — "The uplift is how much better we do than doing nothing — that's the real value."
- **P-value / statistical significance** — "How confident we are that a result is real and not just luck."

**How to use this glossary:** When a stakeholder looks confused, drop the jargon and use the one-liner. Saying "recall is how many of the real churners we catch" builds far more trust than "the recall is 0.8." (See Chapters 17 and 31.)
