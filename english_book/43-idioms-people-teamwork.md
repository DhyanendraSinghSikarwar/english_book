# Chapter 43: Professional Idioms — People, Teamwork & Office Politics

This chapter covers 40 idioms about working with people: teamwork, relationships, leadership, and the quiet politics of any office. These help you describe people and situations naturally — and understand what colleagues really mean.

**How to read each entry:** the idiom, a simple meaning, an example, and a note where useful. **[Casual]** = informal. **[Careful]** = may confuse non-native speakers.

---

1. **pull together** — work as a team toward a goal.
*Example:* "The team pulled together to hit the deadline."

2. **team player** — someone who works well with others.
*Example:* "She's a real team player."

3. **carry the team** — do most of the work for a group.
*Example:* "He carried the team on that project." **[Casual]**

4. **see eye to eye** — agree fully (also Ch 40).
*Example:* "The two leads finally see eye to eye."

5. **get off on the right foot** — start a relationship well.
*Example:* "I want to get off on the right foot with the new team."

6. **rub someone the wrong way** — annoy someone without meaning to.
*Example:* "His blunt style rubs some people the wrong way." **[Careful]**

7. **on good terms** — having a friendly relationship.
*Example:* "We're on good terms with the vendor."

8. **build bridges** — improve relationships between people or teams.
*Example:* "She's good at building bridges across departments."

9. **burn bridges** — damage relationships so badly they can't be repaired.
*Example:* "Don't burn bridges when you leave a job."

10. **have someone's back** — support and protect someone.
*Example:* "Don't worry, I've got your back." **[Casual]**

11. **throw someone under the bus** — blame or sacrifice someone to protect yourself.
*Example:* "He threw the team under the bus in the meeting." **[Careful]**

12. **take the credit** — claim praise for work.
*Example:* "She always takes the credit for the team's work."

13. **give credit where credit is due** — praise the person who deserves it.
*Example:* "Give credit where it's due — Priya solved this."

14. **step on someone's toes** — upset someone by doing their job or role.
*Example:* "I don't want to step on your toes, so tell me if this is your area." **[Careful]**

15. **pass the buck** — pass responsibility to someone else.
*Example:* "Nobody wanted to own it — they just passed the buck." **[Careful]**

16. **the buck stops here** — I take final responsibility.
*Example:* "As the lead, the buck stops with me." **[Careful]**

17. **wear many hats** — do many different roles.
*Example:* "In a small team, you wear many hats."

18. **a safe pair of hands** — someone reliable you can trust with important work.
*Example:* "Give it to Anna — she's a safe pair of hands."

19. **go above and beyond** — do far more than expected.
*Example:* "The team went above and beyond this quarter."

20. **pull rank** — use your senior position to get your way.
*Example:* "She didn't pull rank; she listened to everyone." **[Careful]**

21. **keep someone at arm's length** — stay a little distant from someone.
*Example:* "It's wise to keep that contact at arm's length." **[Careful]**

22. **see the bigger picture** — understand the whole situation, not just details.
*Example:* "Good leaders see the bigger picture."

23. **on the same team** — working toward the same goal (not against each other).
*Example:* "Let's remember we're on the same team here."

24. **bring out the best in someone** — help someone perform at their highest.
*Example:* "A good manager brings out the best in the team."

25. **lead by example** — show the right behaviour yourself.
*Example:* "She leads by example — always well prepared."

26. **take someone under your wing** — guide and protect a junior person.
*Example:* "He took the new intern under his wing." **[Careful]**

27. **learn the ropes** — learn how things work in a new job.
*Example:* "Give me a week to learn the ropes."

28. **show someone the ropes** — teach someone how things work.
*Example:* "Anna will show you the ropes."

29. **a people person** — someone who enjoys and is good with people.
*Example:* "She's a people person, great with clients."

30. **rock the boat** — cause trouble by challenging the normal way.
*Example:* "He doesn't like to rock the boat." **[Careful]**

31. **toe the line** — follow the rules and expectations.
*Example:* "Everyone's expected to toe the line on data policy." **[Careful]**

32. **put your foot in it** — say something embarrassing by mistake.
*Example:* "I put my foot in it by forgetting his name." **[Careful]**

33. **on the same page** — in agreement (also Ch 40).
*Example:* "Let's get the whole team on the same page."

34. **a fresh pair of eyes** — a new person to review something.
*Example:* "Could you take a look? I need a fresh pair of eyes."

35. **bounce ideas off someone** — test ideas by discussing them.
*Example:* "Can I bounce a few ideas off you?"

36. **pick someone's brain** — ask someone knowledgeable for their ideas.
*Example:* "Can I pick your brain about the data model?" **[Casual]**

37. **get on like a house on fire** — get along extremely well.
*Example:* "The two teams got on like a house on fire." **[Careful]**

38. **the new kid on the block** — the newest person in a group.
*Example:* "As the new kid on the block, I asked a lot of questions." **[Casual]**

39. **read the room** — sense the mood of a group and act accordingly.
*Example:* "She read the room and kept her update short."

40. **win someone over** — gain someone's support or trust.
*Example:* "The results won over the doubters."

---

## Using these idioms wisely

- Idioms about people can carry emotion, so use the negative ones ("throw under the bus," "pass the buck") carefully — never lightly accuse a real colleague in writing.
- Positive relationship idioms ("have your back," "a safe pair of hands," "lead by example") are great in reviews and praise.
- With non-native speakers, "read the room" or "get on like a house on fire" may not land — say "sense the mood" or "get along very well."

**Common Indian-English note:** Use first names, not "sir/madam" (Ch 14, 33). Avoid "he is my senior/junior" as a status label; say "she's more experienced" or "he reports to me." Say "colleague," not "batchmate," at work.

---

**Chapter 43 summary.** These 40 idioms describe the human side of work — teamwork, trust, credit, and office politics. They help you sound natural and, just as importantly, understand what colleagues mean. Lean on the positive ones freely; handle the negative ones with care, especially in writing.
