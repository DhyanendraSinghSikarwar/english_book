# Chapter 15: British vs American vs Indian English Quick Map

When you move to a European workplace, you'll mostly meet British-style English, because Europe learned English from Britain, not America. But American English is everywhere too (in tech especially). And you're bringing Indian English habits. This chapter helps you know which version to use, and when it matters.

**Good news:** most differences are small. Nobody will judge you for saying "elevator" instead of "lift." But knowing the British forms helps you fit in faster in Europe, and knowing which Indian forms are non-standard (from Chapter 14) matters most.

**Rule of thumb for Europe:** when in doubt, use British spelling and vocabulary. It's what most European colleagues learned and expect.

---

*Note for data scientists:* In code and technical contexts, American spelling often wins because programming languages use it (`color`, `analyze`, `normalize`, `initialize`). So you may write `optimize` in Python but "optimise" in an email. That's normal — match the context.

## 2. Dates

This one causes real confusion and mistakes. Be careful.

- **British/Indian/European:** DD/MM/YYYY → 03/07/2026 means 3 July 2026.
- **American:** MM/DD/YYYY → 03/07/2026 means 7 March 2026.

**The safe solution:** write the month as a word. "3 July 2026" or "July 3, 2026" can never be misread. Use this in all important emails and reports. Europe generally writes "3 July 2026" (day first).

## 3. Time

- **Europe:** often uses the 24-hour clock. "14:00" means 2 p.m. Meetings are often set as "14:00 CET."
- **Time zones:** learn CET/CEST (Central European Time) if you work with Germany, Netherlands, France. The UK and Ireland use GMT/BST. Always state the zone: "2 p.m. CET."

## 5. Floors (a real source of confusion)

- **Europe/UK/India:** The floor at street level is the **ground floor (G or 0)**. The next floor up is the **first floor**.
- **America:** The street-level floor is the **first floor**.

So if a European colleague says "meet on the first floor," they mean one floor **above** the ground. Good news: India follows the European system here, so you already have this right.

## 6. Holidays vs vacation, and leave culture

- Europeans say "I'm on **holiday**" (UK) or use "**annual leave**." They rarely say "vacation" (that's American).
- Europe has generous leave — often **25 to 30 days** of annual leave, plus public holidays. Taking your full leave is normal and expected, not a sign of low commitment. (More on this culture in Chapter 33.)
- "Bank holiday" (UK) = a public holiday.
- "Sick leave" / "sick day" = time off when ill; in much of Europe this is separate from annual leave and well protected.

## 7. CV vs resume

- In Europe, it's called a **CV** (curriculum vitae), not a resume.
- European CVs are usually **1–2 pages**, focused and factual.
- **Avoid "bio-data"** — that term is only used in South Asia and looks dated.
- Some European countries traditionally included a photo on the CV (Germany, France), but this is fading. When in doubt, leave the photo off unless asked.
- Don't put age, marital status, religion, or a photo unless specifically expected — in some countries these are actively discouraged to avoid bias.

## 8. Punctuation and number formatting

- **Decimal point vs comma:** This is a big one in Europe. Many European countries (Germany, France, Netherlands) use a **comma** as the decimal separator and a **dot or space** for thousands. So "1.000,50" means one thousand and fifty cents, not one point zero zero zero.
  - UK/Ireland/India/US: 1,000.50
  - Germany/France/NL: 1.000,50 or 1 000,50
  - *When writing for a mixed audience, spell it out or use clear labels to avoid costly mistakes.*
- **Currency:** Europe uses the euro (€) in the eurozone; the UK uses pounds (£). Write "€2.3 million" or "£500,000." The € sometimes goes before (€100) or after (100 €) depending on country.
- **Quotation marks:** British English often uses single quotes ('like this'), American uses double ("like this"). Either is fine; be consistent.

## 9. Grammar differences (small but noticeable)

| Point | American | British / European |
|---|---|---|
| Collective nouns | "The team is..." | "The team are..." (both heard) |
| Past participle | "gotten" | "got" |
| "at the weekend" | "on the weekend" | "at the weekend" |
| "different than" | "different than" | "different from / to" |
| "write me" | "write me" | "write to me" |

## 10. What European colleagues actually expect

Putting it all together, here's what will help you fit in fastest in a European English-speaking workplace:

- **Use British spelling** in documents and emails (English UK spell-check).
- **Write dates with the month as a word** to avoid confusion.
- **Say "holiday" or "annual leave,"** not "vacation."
- **Use the 24-hour clock and state the time zone** for meetings.
- **Drop the Indian-English forms** from Chapter 14 (do the needful, revert, prepone, etc.).
- **Use first names,** not sir/madam.
- **Be careful with numbers and decimals** when working with continental European colleagues.

But most of all: **don't stress about accent or the occasional American word.** Clear communication matters far more than perfect British English. Your colleagues care that they understand you — and that you understand them.
